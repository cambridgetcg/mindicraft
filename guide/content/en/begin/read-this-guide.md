This guide is not a book you read front to back. It is a tree. Each page teaches one skill, and each skill is a branch that other branches grow from. You learn to make fire, and fire unlocks safe water, cooked food, and hardened tools. You learn rope, and rope unlocks shelters, traps, and bridges. Nothing here asks you to know anything in advance — every page either stands alone or tells you exactly which pages come before it.

That matters because when you have nothing, the most dangerous thing you can do is the wrong thing first. This page teaches you how to find the right thing.

## You need

- **Any page of this guide.** You are holding one now, so you already qualify.
- **An honest look at where you are.** Not where you wish you were. The tree only works if you start from your real situation — cold, thirsty, lost, or just curious at a kitchen table.
- **Optionally, a way to keep notes** — a pencil, a stick and bare earth, or just your memory. Marking what you have finished helps you see what you have unlocked.

## Steps

1. **Start where you are, not at page one.** If you are safe and warm, browse freely. If you are in trouble tonight, go straight to "Stop, breathe, take stock" and then "What matters first" — they were written for exactly that moment.
2. **Look at the top of every page before reading it.** Each page carries four labels:
   - **summary** — one sentence saying what the page gives you.
   - **difficulty** — *easy* means anyone can do it today; *steady* takes practice; *hard* takes days and failures; *mastery* takes a season or more.
   - **time** — an honest guess at how long the doing takes, not just the reading.
   - **needs** — the list of pages that come before this one. This is the tree's whole trick.
3. **Follow the needs downward until you hit a page you can already do.** If a page needs "fire" and you cannot make fire, go to the fire page. If *that* page needs nothing you lack, start there. That is your next step — always the lowest branch you have not yet climbed.
4. **Read a whole page once before doing anything on it.** The "Watch out" section near the bottom often changes how you do step one. Then go back and do the steps in order.
5. **Do, don't just read.** A skill you have read about is a rumor. A skill you have done once, badly, is yours. Practice the easy things while life is easy, so they are ready when it is not.
6. **When you finish a page, look upward.** Ask: what does this unlock? That is how a night of misery becomes a shelter, a shelter becomes a camp, and a camp — page by page — becomes a working village.

## Watch out

- **Skipping the needs list.** Trying a page whose foundations you lack wastes hours and can be dangerous — building a roof with knots you cannot tie. Drop down a branch instead; it is faster in the end.
- **Reading everything and doing nothing.** Ten pages read is worth less than one page done. If you notice you have been reading for an hour, stop and go do the easiest step of the nearest page.
- **Starting with what excites you instead of what you need.** Forging metal is thrilling; tonight's shelter is boring. The next page, "What matters first," gives you the order that keeps you alive. Trust it over your enthusiasm.
- **Trusting memory under stress.** Frightened people forget steps and skip warnings. If you can, bring the page with you. If you cannot, re-read the Watch out section twice before you go.

## How it works

Knowledge compounds the way interest does: each skill is not just a result but a tool for earning the next one. A tree structure keeps that honest — no page can quietly assume something you were never taught, because every assumption must appear in its needs list. Follow the branches and there is always exactly one kind of next step: something you can do today that you could not do yesterday.
