When everything is missing at once, your body tells you lies about what to fix first. Hunger shouts the loudest, but it is the least urgent. Cold whispers, and it is the one that can kill you tonight. This page gives you the true order, so that on a bad day you spend your strength where it actually buys you time.

The old rule of thumb is called the rule of threes, and though the numbers are rough, they are roughly right: you can live about **3 minutes without air**, about **3 hours without shelter** in bad cold or wet wind, about **3 days without water**, and about **3 weeks without food**. Fix things in the order they can kill you.

## You need

- **Nothing physical.** This page is a way of thinking, and it works empty-handed.
- **The honesty to overrule your stomach.** Hunger feels like an emergency; the rule of threes says it almost never is.
- **A quick read of the weather.** Wind, rain, and coming darkness decide how much of your 3 hours of shelter-time you really have. Wet plus windy plus near freezing can shorten it badly.

## Steps

1. **Fix breathing and bleeding first — you have minutes.** If you or anyone with you cannot breathe freely, or is losing blood fast, nothing else on this list matters until that is handled. Press firmly on heavy bleeding with cloth and keep pressing.
2. **Fix shelter second — you have hours.** Shelter does not mean a house. It means three things, in this order: get **out of the wind**, get **off the wet ground**, and get **out of the rain**. A wall of packed branches, a dry hollow, extra layers stuffed with dry leaves — any of these counts. Water and wind pull heat out of a body far faster than still air does, which is why a wet, windy 5 °C day can be deadlier than a dry, calm freezing one.
3. **Fix water third — you have days.** By the end of the first day you should know where water is and have a plan to make it safe, which usually means boiling it. Thirst dulls your thinking long before it kills you, so do not leave this for day three.
4. **Fix fire fourth — it multiplies everything.** Fire is not on the death-clock itself, but it buys shelter (warmth and dry clothes), safe water (boiling), safe food (cooking), light, and a signal. Once the first three are handled, fire is almost always the best next hour you can spend.
5. **Fix food last — you have weeks.** A healthy person can go two to three weeks without eating. You will feel weak and miserable long before you are in danger. Spend food-hunting energy only after shelter, water, and fire are settled.
6. **Re-run the list whenever anything changes.** New weather, an injury, a move to a new spot — start again at step 1 and walk down the order.

## Watch out

- **Chasing food on day one.** This is the classic fatal mistake: hours spent hunting berries while clothes stay wet and the sun goes down. Say the rule out loud — "weeks without food, hours without warmth" — and go build the wall first.
- **Getting wet to get water or food.** Wading a cold stream soaks you, and wet clothing near freezing can pull your body below 35 °C — the point called hypothermia, where shivering turns to confusion. Stay dry even at the cost of a slower route.
- **Sweating while building shelter in the cold.** Sweat is water on your skin, and wet kills warmth. Work slowly, open your layers while you work, and close them when you stop.
- **Drinking clearly foul water in a panic on day one.** You have days of margin — use them to boil or find a cleaner source. (In a true last resort, days from help, dehydration is the faster killer — but almost nobody reading this is there, so make it safe.)
- **Treating the threes as exact.** They are honest averages, not promises. Cold water immersion, injury, illness, or desert heat shrink every number. When conditions are harsh, act as if each clock runs at half speed.

## How it works

Your body is a warm-water machine that must stay near 37 °C. Air you replace in minutes, heat in hours, water in days, food in weeks — the order of the list is just the order of the machine's fuel gauges, fastest-emptying first. Instinct evolved when food was the everyday problem, so it still shouts about hunger; the rule of threes is how you overrule an instinct that is wrong about emergencies.
