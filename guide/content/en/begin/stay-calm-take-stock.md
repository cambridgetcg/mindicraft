The first danger in any emergency is not cold or thirst — it is panic. A frightened body floods itself with alarm chemicals that make legs want to run and make thinking almost impossible. People in trouble have walked past food, water, and shelter because panic had shrunk their vision to a tunnel. Ten calm minutes at the start routinely save whole days later.

The good news: calm is not a personality trait. It is a procedure, and this is it.

## You need

- **A spot that is safe enough for ten minutes.** Not perfect — just not actively dangerous. Step away from the cliff edge, the rising water, or the traffic first.
- **Your breath.** It is the only lever you always carry that directly slows a panicking body.
- **Whatever is in your pockets and bag.** You will lay it all out; every object is a possible tool.

## Steps

1. **Stop moving.** Sit down if the ground is safe and dry enough. Movement feeds panic; stillness starves it. Say out loud: "I am stopping to think." Sounds silly, works anyway.
2. **Slow your breathing.** Breathe in through your nose for a count of 4, then out slowly through your mouth for a count of 6, like blowing gently on hot soup. Do this 10 times — about two minutes. The long, slow out-breath is the part that tells your body the emergency signal can stand down.
3. **Check your body, head to toe.** Run your hands over yourself and look: bleeding, pain, anything that will not move right, wet clothes, numb fingers or toes. Press firmly on any heavy bleeding with cloth and keep pressing. Small injuries get noted now, treated soon — and get real medical care as soon as you possibly can.
4. **Check anyone with you the same way.** Speak simply and calmly; your calm is contagious, and so is your panic.
5. **Lay out everything you have.** Empty pockets and bags onto the ground or a jacket. Look at each object and ask "what could this do?" — a phone (even dead, its screen reflects light), string, a wrapper, a lighter, keys, a plastic bag. Nothing is junk yet.
6. **Look around slowly, one full circle.** What is here? Water sounds, tree cover, wind direction, high ground, paths, anything human-made. What is the weather doing, and how many hours of daylight are left? (Rough check: hold your hand flat at arm's length under the sun — each finger-width between sun and horizon is about 15 minutes.)
7. **Make one plain sentence of a plan.** Say it out loud: "Before dark I will get out of the wind and find water." One or two goals, no more. If you know the guide, the page "What matters first" tells you which goals to pick.
8. **If you move, mark where you started.** A stack of three stones, an arrow of sticks, a strip of cloth on a branch. People searching for you start from where you were last known to be — and you may want to find your way back to it yourself.

## Watch out

- **Walking before thinking.** The urge to *do something* — usually to walk fast in a guessed direction — is the most common and most costly mistake. It burns energy, causes falls, and moves you away from the place searchers will check. Sit first. Ten minutes of stillness has never killed anyone; ten minutes of running has.
- **Ignoring small problems.** A pebble in a shoe becomes a blister becomes a foot you cannot walk on. A damp sock becomes numb toes. Fix small things now, while they are small.
- **Making a ten-part plan.** Big plans collapse and take your morale with them. One sentence, one or two goals, then re-plan when those are done.
- **Doing this once and never again.** Take stock again whenever anything changes — weather turns, someone gets hurt, you arrive somewhere new. The procedure is reusable forever.

## How it works

Fear triggers a body alarm that speeds the heart, dumps sugar into the blood, and narrows attention for fighting or fleeing — brilliant for escaping a predator, terrible for planning. Breathing is the one alarm wire you can reach by choice: a slow out-breath activates the body's own calming signal, and the alarm winds down within minutes. Once the alarm quiets, the planning part of your mind comes back online — and that is the tool every other page of this guide depends on.
