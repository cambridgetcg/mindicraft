Most danger in wild places does not pounce. It waits, quietly, in a spot that looks fine — a dry sandy channel that becomes a river after distant rain, a dead branch balanced overhead until one gust drops it. The skill is not bravery. It is reading the ground before you trust it, especially before you sleep on it.

Do this survey in daylight, before you commit to a camp or a route. It takes a slow walk and your full attention — which is why "Stop, breathe, take stock" comes first.

## You need

- **Daylight.** Almost every hazard on this page is invisible in the dark. If dusk is close, survey a small patch well rather than a large one badly.
- **Your eyes, ears, and nose.** Danger leaves signs. A strong musky or rotten smell near a hole in the rocks is a sign, not a curiosity.
- **A slow walk in a circle** around any spot you plan to stay, out to about 30 big steps. Fast walkers see paths; slow walkers see hazards.

## Steps

1. **Look up first.** Stand under where you would sleep and study everything overhead:
   - **Dead branches** — bare, grey, no bark, no leaves while neighbours have them. Loggers call a hung dead branch a *widow-maker*, which tells you all you need. If there is dead wood above, move; never camp under it.
   - **Whole dead trees** within falling distance — a tree can reach the ground as far away as it is tall.
   - **Loose rock above you** on slopes and cliff bases. Fresh broken stones at your feet mean more are coming.
2. **Look down and read the water story on the ground.**
   - A **dry channel** of smooth sand or rounded gravel, with clean-washed banks, is a stream bed resting between floods. Never camp in one, however flat and inviting — rain you never see, falling far uphill, can fill it fast.
   - **Debris caught in bushes** at knee or waist height shows how high past floods reached. Sleep above that line.
   - Low bowls collect cold air and rainwater. Camp on gently raised, flat ground instead.
3. **Respect edges.** Stay at least 3 metres — about two body lengths — back from any cliff edge or steep drop. Edges overhang and crumble, and grass or wet rock near them is as slippery as ice. If you must look over, lie flat on your belly and inch forward. Never walk near an edge at night or in wind.
4. **Look for animal signs and give them room.**
   - Signs: droppings, well-worn narrow trails, scattered bones or fur, rubbed bark, and holes with smooth, hair-snagged entrances — those are dens.
   - Never reach into, poke, or camp beside a den or burrow, even one that looks empty.
   - Do not camp *on* an animal trail — animals commute along them at night — and keep your camp about 100 big steps from dens, carcasses, and berry patches that bears or scavengers may claim.
   - Keep food smells away from where you sleep.
5. **Judge moving water and ice with suspicion.** Fast water above your knees can knock an adult over, and cold water steals strength in minutes. Never cross ice unless you already know it is thick and solid — there is no safe way to test unknown ice alone.
6. **Choose your spot by elimination.** Pick the place with nothing overhead, no water story underfoot, no edge nearby, and no animal signs — then do one final look up before you settle. Boring ground is safe ground.

## Watch out

- **The flat, sandy, sheltered spot in a gully.** It is comfortable precisely because floods swept it clean. This mistake drowns campers in deserts, where storms are rare but violent. Sleep on the boring higher ground instead.
- **Sheltering under the biggest tree in a storm.** The tallest tree draws lightning, and storms are exactly when dead branches fall. In thunder, prefer low ground among small trees of even height.
- **A "perfect view" campsite.** Views mean edges and wind. Take the photo, then camp back from the edge.
- **Trusting a spot because you checked it yesterday.** Wind, rain, and animals rewrite the ground daily. Re-do the look-up and the circle each time you return.
- **Surveying at dusk in a hurry.** If light is failing, shrink your ambitions: a small, well-checked spot beats a large, guessed one.

## How it works

Hazards gather where energy is stored and waiting: water piled uphill, wood dying overhead, rock leaning past its balance, a mother animal guarding young. Each leaves signs *before* it lets go — washed gravel, bare grey limbs, fresh rockfall, worn trails. Reading signs is letting past events warn you about future ones. The ground itself is the most honest guidebook you will ever own.
