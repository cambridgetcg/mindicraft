---
title: Stop sickness from spreading
summary: When someone is ill, let them rest apart, wash often, share no cups, and keep their water and food separate.
difficulty: steady
time: a day
needs: [keep-clean, dig-a-latrine]
---

One sick person is a hard week. A whole household sick at once — including everyone who fetches water, cooks, and cares for the others — is a disaster. Most sickness travels in only a few ways: on hands, in shared cups and food, in coughs sneezed into shared air, and through stool getting into water. Block those few roads and most sickness stops with the first person.

None of this needs to be unkind. The sick person is resting, not being punished. Say so out loud, especially to children.

## You need

- **A separate resting spot** — a corner, a screen of cloth, or a separate shelter, a few paces from where others sleep and eat. Fresh moving air is better than a sealed stuffy room.
- **One carer** — a single person who tends the sick one, so only one pair of hands is at risk instead of everyone's.
- **Soap or wood ash and water** — set right where the carer will use it: at the sick person's spot and at the latrine.
- **A marked set of things for the sick person only** — cup, bowl, spoon, cloth, water container. Mark them with a scratch or a tied string so no one grabs them by mistake.
- **A bucket or pot with a lid** — if the sick person cannot reach the latrine.
- **Sunlight** — free and useful: it dries and helps kill germs on washed cloth and bedding.

## Steps

1. **Move the sick person to their own spot** as soon as sickness shows — fever, vomiting, diarrhea, or a heavy cough. Comfortable, shaded, aired, and apart. The first day matters most; do not wait to "see if it gets worse."
2. **Choose one carer.** Everyone else keeps their distance — talk from a few steps away, wave, sing, but do not touch, hug, or share air close up. The carer should be a healthy adult, not a child and not someone already frail.
3. **The carer washes hands every single time** — after touching the sick person, their dishes, their bedding, or anything from the latrine, and always before touching food or their own face. Wet, lather with soap or scrub with ash, rub twenty seconds, rinse with clean water.
4. **Give the sick person their own marked cup, bowl, and water.** Their drinking water comes from the common supply by pouring — never by dipping their cup into the family container. After each meal, wash their dishes apart from the family's, last, in hot water.
5. **Handle stool and vomit like poison.** It goes into the latrine or is buried well away from any water source. The carer washes hands after, every time, no exceptions. If cloths or bedding are soiled, wash them in hot water, apart from other laundry, and dry them spread out in full sun.
6. **Teach the cough rule to everyone.** Cough or sneeze into the elbow or a cloth, never into the open air or a hand that will touch things. The sick person keeps a cloth for this; it gets washed in hot water daily.
7. **Guard the water supply.** Only healthy, washed hands touch the family water containers. Keep them covered and pour, don't dip.
8. **End it cleanly.** When the sick person has been well for two full days, wash their bedding and dishes in hot water, sun everything, and welcome them back. The carer washes last, top to bottom.

## Watch out

- **Sharing "just one sip" undoes everything.** The most common failure is a small kindness — a shared cup, a taste from the same spoon. Kindness here is a separate cup, filled generously.
- **The carer is the bridge.** If the carer skips one hand-wash, the whole system falls. Put the soap and water where they cannot walk past it.
- **Do not seal the sick room tight.** Stale shared air spreads coughing sickness faster. Open air flow, distance, and cloth-covered coughs do more than a shut door.
- **Children drift.** They wander to the sick parent, share food, touch everything. Give them a clear job — "you are the water pourer" — and a hard line they can understand.
- **Watch everyone else for the first signs** — fever, loose stool, new cough. Whoever shows them joins the sick spot and gets their own marked cup that same hour.
- **If many people fall sick fast**, or the sickness includes bloody diarrhea or a fever with a rash, this may be an outbreak — go for real medical help and warn your neighbours; do not tough it out quietly.

## How it works

Germs cannot fly far or walk on their own — they ride on hands, in droplets from coughs, in shared food and water, and in stool. Separation, washing, and unshared dishes cut every one of those rides. That is the entire trick, and it is why one disciplined day of setting this up protects the whole household for the length of the illness.
