A field of ripe grain is not yet food. Between the standing gold and the sack in your store are three old jobs with old names: **reaping** (cutting the stalks), **threshing** (beating the seeds loose), and **winnowing** (letting wind carry the trash away). None is hard. All must be done before rain, mice, or mold get there first.

Do this well and you hold the calmest thing a person can own: a dry, full grain store.

## You need

- **A sharp cutting tool** — a sickle, a big knife, or sharp shears. Dull blades pull plants out by the roots and shake grain onto the ground.
- **String or twisted stalks** to tie cut grain into bundles (called sheaves).
- **A clean, hard surface** for threshing — a swept patch of packed earth, a large cloth or tarp, or a wooden floor.
- **A stick or flail** — any smooth stick as long as your arm; a flail is just two sticks joined by a short cord so the free one swings hard.
- **Baskets** — one wide and shallow for winnowing, others for carrying. This is where basket-weaving pays off.
- **Dry, tight storage** — clay pots or jars with lids, wooden bins, or thick sacks hung off the ground.
- **A steady breeze, or a friend with a fan**, for winnowing day.

## Steps

1. **Cut on a dry day**, after dew dries. Grab a fistful of stalks, cut low with the sickle, lay handfuls all facing one way. Test first: ripe kernels barely dent under a thumbnail.
2. **Tie sheaves.** Bundle stalks into sheaves as thick as your arm can circle. Tie each with string or a twisted rope of stalks.
3. **Dry the sheaves standing up.** Lean 6–8 sheaves against each other, heads up, like a little tent (a stook). Leave them 3–10 days in sun and wind until stalks are crackly dry. If rain threatens, carry them under a roof.
4. **Thresh.** Lay a few sheaves heads-inward on your tarp or clean floor. Beat the heads with the stick or flail — steady whacks, not wild swings — until the kernels rattle loose. You can also hold a sheaf and whip its head against the inside of a barrel or a slanted board. Set the empty straw aside — it is bedding, mulch, and kindling.
5. **Winnow.** On a breezy day, pour grain slowly from your wide basket, held at chest height, onto the tarp below. The heavy kernels fall straight down; the light husks (chaff) blow aside. Repeat 3–4 times, then toss the grain gently in the basket, blowing across it, until clean.
6. **Pick it over.** Spread the grain and pull out stones, green kernels, and anything odd — especially any grain that looks **purple-black, hard, and banana-shaped, bigger than its neighbors: that is ergot, a poisonous fungus.** Throw those out, and everything touching them, without exception. Also discard any pink, moldy, or musty-smelling grain.
7. **Dry the grain itself.** Spread it one finger deep on cloth in sun and moving air for a few days, stirring twice a day. Done test: bite a kernel — it should crack sharply between your teeth, never squash or feel chewy.
8. **Store hard, cool, dry, and sealed.** Fill clean, bone-dry pots or bins, lid them tight, and keep them off the floor on smooth stands — that defeats most mice. Keep a cat if you can. Check every week for the first month: any warmth, damp smell, or clumping means pour it out and dry it again.

## Watch out

- **Rain on cut grain is the great destroyer.** Wet sheaves mold, and ripe grain lying wet will sprout in days. Watch the sky; move sheaves under cover at the first threat.
- **Waiting too long to cut.** Over-ripe grain "shatters" — kernels fall off at a touch and feed the birds. Cut when hard but before heads start dropping seed.
- **Storing grain even slightly damp.** It heats, molds, and can spoil the whole container. Moldy grain can carry poisons you cannot see or boil away — when a batch smells musty or looks blue-green or pink, it is not food and not seed. Never "just cook it anyway."
- **Ergot** (step 6) has poisoned whole villages through history, mostly in rye but also wheat and barley. Sorting it out by eye works — do it in good light, every harvest.
- **Mice and insects.** Tight lids beat mice; cold and dryness slow insects. If tiny weevils appear, spread the grain in the sun for a day and sieve them out; use that batch first.
- **Eating your seed grain.** Before storing, set aside next year's seed from the plumpest, cleanest kernels — sealed, labeled, untouchable.

## How it works

Dry is the entire secret. Below roughly one part water in eight, grain is too dry for mold or sprouting, and it will keep for years. Every step — stooking, dry-day threshing, sunning, sealed pots — is one more way of getting water out and keeping it out.
