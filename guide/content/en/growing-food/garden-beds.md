A garden lives or dies by where you put it. Plants need three things from the ground: sun to grow, loose soil so roots can push down, and water they can reach. Pick the spot well and half the work of the whole year is already done. Pick badly and you will fight shade, rock, and drought until you give up.

The digging is one hard day. The choosing deserves a slow one first.

## You need

- **A digging stick** — from "Carve wooden tools". A flat wedge tip breaks and lifts soil; it is the oldest farm tool on Earth.
- **Your hands and eyes** — the soil tests below need nothing else.
- **A full day of watching light** — you must see where the sun actually falls, morning to evening.
- **Stakes and cord (or scratched lines)** — to mark bed edges so they stay straight.
- **Water within an easy carry** — a stream, well, or rain store within about 50 paces. You will haul water often; every extra pace is paid a hundred times.

## Steps

1. **Find the sun.** Watch the spots you are considering through one day. You want ground that gets **at least 6 hours of direct sun** — 8 or more is better. Morning sun is best of all: it dries dew fast, and wet leaves breed disease. Avoid the shade side of trees, walls, and hills.
2. **Test the soil with your hands.** Dig a hole two hand-spans deep and squeeze a damp handful:
    - **Good soil** is dark, smells sweet and earthy, and squeezes into a lump that crumbles when you poke it. Dig here.
    - **Sand** will not hold a lump at all — water and food run straight through it.
    - **Clay** squeezes into a shiny, sticky ribbon — roots and rain both struggle to enter.
    - Sand and clay can both be mended with compost, but if a dark crumbly patch exists, take it. Where nettles or tall lush weeds grow thick, the soil is usually rich — weeds are honest.
3. **Check the water twice.** Close to a water source, yes — but not in a puddle. After rain, look: if water stands on the spot for more than a few hours, roots there will drown. Gentle slope or flat, never the lowest bowl of land (that is also where frost pools).
4. **Mark out beds you can reach across.** Stake out beds about **1 to 1.2 metres wide** — narrow enough to reach the middle from either side without stepping in — and as long as you like. Leave **paths of 40–50 cm** between them. From now on, feet on paths only, never on beds.
5. **Strip the surface.** Cut away grass and weeds in slabs with your digging stick, shaking the loose soil back onto the bed. Stack the slabs grass-side-down in a pile — they rot into fine soil in a few months. Get the white roots out; every piece left regrows.
6. **Dig deep and loosen.** Work down the bed, driving the stick in and levering up, breaking every clod to crumbs. Loosen to **at least 20 cm, 30 cm if you can** — a forearm deep. Pull out rocks and thick roots as you go. Loose soil lets roots dive, rain soak in, and air reach the living things below.
7. **Shape and finish.** Rake the bed with your fingers or a branch into a flat top, slightly raised above the paths. Raised soil warms faster in spring and sheds heavy rain. If you have compost ready, spread a few centimetres on top now. Let the bed settle a few days before planting.

## Watch out

- **Judging sun in winter or at noon.** Shade moves through the day and the year. One quick look lies; a full day of watching does not.
- **Digging waterlogged clay** turns it into brick when it dries. If a squeezed handful drips, wait for it to dry to merely damp.
- **Old bad ground.** Avoid spots where trash was burned or dumped, or beside old painted or oiled structures — soil holds poisons for decades. When in doubt, dig elsewhere.
- **Stepping on the beds** squashes out the air you just dug in, and roots stop dead at packed soil. Paths are for feet; this rule is forever.
- **Beds too wide to reach across** get walked on — see above. 1.2 metres, no more.
- **Tree roots and tree shade** both steal from a garden. Stay at least as far from a tree as the tree is tall.

## How it works

Roots do not push through solid ground; they follow cracks and air spaces, drinking water and food from the soil grains they touch. Digging makes millions of those spaces. Dark colour means rotted plant matter — humus — which holds water like a sponge and feeds plants as it breaks down. That is why dark and crumbly beats every other test.
