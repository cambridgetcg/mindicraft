Every plant you grew came from a seed. If you save seeds from this year's harvest, you never have to find seeds again — your garden feeds itself forever. Even better: by always saving from your best plants, your seeds slowly get better at growing in your exact patch of ground.

One jar of dry seeds, kept safe through winter, is a whole garden waiting to happen.

## You need

- **Your best plants, left unpicked.** Choose the healthiest, biggest, earliest plants — not the leftovers. The seed remembers its parent.
- **A dry, breezy place** — the same kind of spot you use for drying food. Damp is the enemy of seeds.
- **Cloth or flat baskets** to spread seeds on while they dry.
- **Jars or pots with tight lids**, clean and bone-dry inside.
- **Something to label with** — scratch marks, charcoal, or ink. In spring, all dry seeds look alike.

## Steps

1. **Mark your best plants early in the season.** Tie a strip of cloth on them so nobody eats them. Pick plants that grew fast, tasted good, and stayed healthy.
2. **Let the seeds ripen fully on the plant — past the eating stage.**
   - Beans and peas: leave pods until they turn brown, dry, and rattle.
   - Squash and melons: let the fruit sit until fully ripe, even overripe.
   - Lettuce, onions, herbs: let them send up a flower stalk and let it go brown.
   - Grain: wait until the seed head is hard and dry.
3. **Collect on a dry day**, in the afternoon, after any dew has burned off.
4. **Clean the seeds.**
   - Dry seeds (beans, grain, lettuce): rub the pods or heads between your hands, then blow gently across them so the light husks fly away.
   - Wet seeds (squash, cucumber, tomato): scoop them out and rinse off the flesh. For tomato, leave seeds and their jelly in a cup of water for 2–3 days until a scum forms, then rinse — this eats away the jelly coat that stops sprouting.
5. **Dry the seeds hard.** Spread them one layer deep on cloth, in shade with moving air — not hot sun, which can cook them. Stir daily. Small seeds take about a week; big beans and squash seeds take two to three.
   - Test: a truly dry bean shatters when hit with a stone instead of squashing. A dry squash seed snaps, not bends.
6. **Jar and label.** Put fully dry seeds in a dry jar with a tight lid. Write the plant name and the year. Add a small cloth bag of dry ash or well-toasted rice to catch stray dampness if you have it.
7. **Store cool, dry, and dark** — a cellar, a buried pot, the coldest corner of the house. Cool and dry is the whole secret. Most seeds kept this way last 2–5 years.
8. **Test before spring.** A few weeks before planting, roll 10 seeds in a damp cloth and keep it warm. If 7 or more sprout in one to two weeks, your seeds are good.

## Watch out

- **Damp seeds rot or sprout in the jar.** If in doubt, dry longer. A seed sealed up wet is a seed lost.
- **Heat kills seeds.** Never dry them over a fire or in strong midday sun. Warm hand-heat is fine; too-hot-to-hold is death.
- **Saving from the worst plants.** It is tempting to eat the best and seed the rest. Do the opposite, or your crop gets weaker every year.
- **Some plants take two years to make seed.** Carrots, onions, beets, and cabbage grow a root or head the first year and only flower the second. Keep a few of the best through winter and replant them.
- **Neighbors cross.** Two kinds of squash or corn growing close together will mix their seeds. If purity matters, grow one kind, or keep kinds far apart.
- **Mice and weevils love a seed jar.** Use tight lids, check monthly, and if you see tiny holes in beans, spread everything out, re-dry, and pick out the damaged ones.

## How it works

A seed is a tiny sleeping plant with a packed lunch. Water and warmth wake it up — so storage must give it neither. Cool, dry, and dark keeps it asleep, and a well-slept seed wakes strong. Choosing your best parents each year is slow, gentle breeding: over a few seasons your seeds become a variety tuned to your soil, your weather, and your hands.
