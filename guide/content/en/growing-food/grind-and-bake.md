Grain in a jar is wealth; bread is that wealth made into dinner. You do three things people have done for ten thousand years: crush the grain between stones into **flour**, wake up **wild yeast** — invisible living things on the grain and in the air that make dough puff up — and bake the risen dough in a hot oven. The yeast takes about five days to catch; everything after is quick.

Your first loaf will be heavy, a bit rough, and the best thing you have ever eaten.

## You need

- **Clean, dry grain** — wheat makes the puffiest bread; barley and corn make flatter, denser loaves.
- **Two hard stones**: a wide flat or dish-shaped base stone, and a smooth rounded rubbing stone that fits your hand. **Hard stone only** — granite or other tough rock that doesn't scratch easily. Soft, crumbly stone sheds grit into your flour and wears your teeth down over the years.
- **A jar** for the starter, plus cloth to cover it.
- **Water** — clean drinking water.
- **Salt** if you have it — bread works without, but tastes flat.
- **A bowl, and cloth for covering dough.**
- **Your clay oven**, plus dry firewood — enough for a 1–2 hour fire.

## Steps

1. **Grind.** Put a small handful of grain on the base stone. Press and rub the hand stone over it, away and back, adding grain a little at a time. First passes make cracked grain; sweep it up and grind again until it is powder. Expect an hour or more of grinding per loaf at first — grind over several days and store the flour dry.
2. **Start the wild yeast (day 1).** In the jar, mix a small handful of flour with the same amount of water into a thick paste. Cover with cloth — air in, flies out. Keep it comfortably warm (roughly 20–25 °C).
3. **Feed it daily (days 2–5).** Once a day, throw away half, then stir in a fresh half-handful each of flour and water. By day 3–5 it should bubble and smell pleasantly sour, like yogurt or beer. When it doubles within a few hours of feeding, it is ready. This living paste is called a **starter**.
4. **Mix the dough (evening).** In the bowl: about two double-handfuls of flour, a fist-sized blob of bubbly starter, a big pinch of salt, and enough water to make a soft, sticky mass — roughly 2 parts flour to 1 part water by volume.
5. **Knead**: press, fold, and turn the dough for about 10 minutes until it goes from shaggy to smooth and stretchy. Cover it and leave it somewhere warm.
6. **Let it rise** until it grows about half again to twice its size. With wild yeast this is slow — 4–12 hours, so overnight is perfect.
7. **Shape and rise again.** Punch the dough down gently, shape it into a round, set it on flour-dusted cloth, cover, and let it puff up again, 1–3 hours.
8. **Fire the oven.** Burn a good fire in it for 1–2 hours until the inside walls turn from black soot to whitish — that color change is your thermometer. Rake out all coals and ash, wipe the floor with a damp (not wet) rag on a stick.
9. **Bake.** Slide the loaf onto the hot oven floor, close the door, and leave it about 40–50 minutes. Done test: the crust is deep brown and the loaf sounds hollow when you tap its bottom.
10. **Wait half an hour before cutting** — the inside is still finishing as it cools. Save the starter jar and keep feeding it daily; tomorrow's bread is already alive in there.

## Watch out

- **Grit in the flour** means your stones are too soft. Change stones. A lifetime of gritty bread wears teeth flat.
- **Starter gone wrong:** pleasant sour smells are fine, but **pink, orange, or black streaks, fuzzy mold, or a rotten smell mean throw it all out**, wash the jar with boiled water, and start again. Never eat a doubtful starter.
- **Dough that never rises:** the room is too cold or the starter too young. Move it somewhere warmer and give it more hours; feed the starter a few more days before trying again.
- **Burnt outside, raw inside** means the oven was too hot. Let the fired oven stand 15–30 minutes longer before loading, or bake a flatter loaf.
- **Steam burns** when opening a hot oven — open it away from your face.
- **Damp flour molds.** Grind only what you'll use within a few weeks; store it dry, sealed, and cool. Whole grain keeps for years; flour does not.

## How it works

Grinding breaks the kernel open so water and yeast can reach the starch. The wild yeasts eat that starch's sugars and breathe out gas; wheat dough holds a stretchy web (gluten) that traps the gas as tiny bubbles. The oven's heat sets that foam solid — bread is simply a cooked sponge of grain, air, and time.
