Vegetables feed you today. Grain feeds you in February. Wheat, barley, and corn are grasses whose seeds are dense little packets of food that keep for years when dry — this is why every settled people on Earth built their lives around a grain field. One good patch, about 10 by 10 paces, can grow enough to bake bread for weeks.

Grain is grown differently from a vegetable bed: you sow it thick, all at once, walk away for months, then harvest it all at once. The work is heavy at the start and the end, and light in the middle.

## You need

- **Seed grain** — whole, unbroken kernels of wheat, barley, or corn that will still sprout. Test first: 10 seeds in a damp cloth; 7 sprouting means good seed. You need roughly 1–2 big handfuls of wheat or barley per pace-wide strip, or about 2–3 kg for a 10-by-10-pace patch. For corn, far less — one seed per plant.
- **Open, sunny ground** — grain hates shade and soggy soil. A gentle slope that drains is perfect.
- **A digging tool** — hoe, spade, or a strong forked stick — to break and loosen the top hand-depth of soil.
- **A rake or leafy branch** to cover the seed after sowing.
- **A fence or guard plan** — brush fence, thorn barrier, or a person with time. Birds, deer, and pigs consider your field a gift.
- **Your seasons, read** — grain must be sown so it ripens in a dry, warm stretch, not in the rains.

## Steps

1. **Pick your grain to match your place.**
   - Barley: toughest, fastest (about 90–110 days), handles poor soil and cold. Best first grain.
   - Wheat: the bread grain, about 100–130 days for spring wheat. Winter wheat is sown in autumn, sleeps under the cold, and ripens early next summer.
   - Corn (maize): needs warmth and a frost-free 100–140 days, but gives the most food per seed.
2. **Clear and break the ground** while the soil is moist but not muddy. Cut weeds, dig or hoe the top 10–15 cm loose, break lumps, rake it roughly level. Work in old manure or compost if you have it.
3. **Sow.**
   - Wheat and barley: scatter seed evenly by hand, walking the patch in two directions so it lands thick — seeds about a thumb-width to two apart. Thick sowing shades out weeds.
   - Corn: plant seeds one by one, 3–5 cm deep, a foot (30 cm) apart, in a block of short rows — never one long line. Corn's pollen falls on the breeze, and a block catches it; a single row makes half-empty cobs.
4. **Cover the seed.** Drag a rake or leafy branch over the patch so wheat and barley end up 2–3 cm under the soil. Press the soil down with your feet or a board — seed touching firm, moist soil sprouts best.
5. **Guard it hardest in week one and at ripening.** Birds pull sprouting seed; everything eats ripe grain. Fence, scare, and check daily at those two times.
6. **Weed once or twice early.** When the grain is a hand tall, pull the biggest weeds. After the grain closes over the ground, it mostly defends itself.
7. **Water only in true drought.** Grain roots go deep. If the leaves roll up tight for days, give the patch one deep soaking.
8. **Watch for ripeness.** The field turns from green to gold, stalks dry, and heads nod. Bite a kernel: milky juice means wait; soft like cheese means soon; hard so your thumbnail barely dents it means harvest now. Corn is ready when husks are brown and kernels dented and hard.

## Watch out

- **Sowing at the wrong time is the whole crop lost.** Spring grain goes in as soon as the soil is workable and frosts are ending. Winter wheat goes in about a month before the ground freezes. When unsure, ask what local farmers sow and when — copy them.
- **Birds at sowing.** If they strip your seed, sow again, cover deeper, and keep watch the first ten days.
- **Waterlogged soil rots seed.** If puddles stand a day after rain, pick different ground or dig small drainage ditches first.
- **Too thin a stand grows weeds instead of grain.** When in doubt, sow thicker — grain crowds happily.
- **Lodging:** rain and wind can flatten tall, overfed grain. Don't over-manure; harvest flattened patches quickly before the grain sprouts on the ground.
- **Save nothing back and you farm only once.** Before eating any of the harvest, set aside next year's seed — the plumpest kernels, at least as much as you sowed.

## How it works

Grain plants are grasses racing to make seed before the season ends. Sown thick, they form a closed roof of leaves that starves weeds of light. All the plant's summer sunlight ends up packed into the kernels as starch — dry, dense energy that neither rots nor freezes. That is why a sack of grain is a winter survived.
