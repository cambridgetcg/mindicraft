summary: Sew a leather-and-wood air pump, because metal-melting fire must breathe harder than lungs can blow.

time: two days

An open charcoal fire reaches maybe 800–900°C. Melting metal out of rock needs 1100°C and more. The only difference is air: the harder a fire breathes, the hotter it burns. You could blow through a tube, and people once did — but lungs tire in minutes, and a smelt takes hours. A bellows is a leather bag between two boards. Squeeze it and it blows; lift it and a flap valve lets it refill. It never gets dizzy.

## You need

- Two flat boards, teardrop shaped, about 50 cm long and 30 cm wide at the fat end — the top and bottom of the pump. Split and smooth them from any sound wood.
- Soft tanned leather, one hide's worth — the flexible sides. Stiff rawhide cracks; it must be real leather.
- Pitch or glue — every seam must be airtight. A leaky bellows feeds the seam, not the fire.
- Small nails, wooden pegs, or strong sewing (awl and sinew or cord) — to fasten leather to wood.
- A nozzle tube with a thumb-thick hole: two sticks grooved down the middle and glued face to face, or a straight tube of dried clay.
- A palm-sized scrap of soft thin leather — the one-way valve.

## Steps

1. Cut both boards to the same teardrop shape, with the points where the nozzle will go.
2. Make the valve in the bottom board: bore or burn a hole about 3–4 cm wide near the fat end. On the inside face, tack the soft leather scrap over the hole along **one edge only** — it must swing free like a pet door. Lift the board: air comes in. Press: the flap slaps shut.
3. Fix the nozzle between the two pointed ends, aiming forward, and pack every gap around it with pitch.
4. Hinge the pointed ends together with a strap of leather so the boards open and close like a clam.
5. Cut a long strip of leather wide enough to wrap the open gap all around the edges, letting the fat end open a good 20–30 cm.
6. Fasten the leather to both board edges: fold the edge over for strength, then nail, peg, or sew it down tight, working around from the nozzle. Paint every seam with warm pitch inside and out.
7. Add a handle to the top board — a stick or a leather loop — and a way to hold the bottom still (kneel on it, peg it down, or add a second handle).
8. Test it. Pump toward dust or a feather; you should feel a steady punch of air at the nozzle. Then hunt leaks: pump slowly with your wet lip or a licked finger near each seam. Any cool hiss gets more pitch.
9. If you can, make a second bellows. Two, pumped in turn, give the furnace one unbroken breath — that is what smelting really wants.

## Watch out

- **Leaks are the number one failure.** A bellows that hisses feels busy but starves the furnace. Seal, test, seal again — a tight small bellows beats a leaky big one.
- **The valve flap must be soft and free.** Too stiff and it won't lift to let air in; tacked on more than one edge and it can't move at all. Thin soft leather, one edge, that's the whole trick.
- **Fire eats nozzles.** Never push the wooden tip into the coals. At the furnace, the bellows blows into the outer end of a clay tube (called a tuyere — a fireproof clay pipe through the furnace wall). Keep the wooden nozzle a finger-length back from any glow.
- **Sparks blow back.** Air out means the fire spits. Kneel to the side of the blast line, never in it, and keep the leather sides away from stray coals.
- **Untanned or dried-out hide splits** within an hour of work. Use properly tanned leather, and grease it now and then to keep it supple.

## How it works

Pressing the boards shrinks the bag, and the trapped air has only one exit: the nozzle. Lifting the boards grows the bag, the pressure inside drops, and outside air pushes the flap open to refill it — the flap is a door that only opens inward. More air means more oxygen reaching the charcoal each second, and burning faster is exactly what burning hotter means.
