---
title: Carry and store water
summary: Turn gourds, bark, and hollow wood into bottles, catch rain when it falls, and keep stored water covered and clean.
difficulty: easy
time: a day
needs: [make-cordage]
---

Water is heavy — one litre weighs one kilogram — and you need two to four litres a day just for drinking, more in heat or hard work. If you can only drink at the stream, the stream owns you: every camp and every journey bends around it. A bottle sets you free, and a stored supply means a sick day or a dry spell does not become an emergency.

Nature already makes containers. Gourds grow into bottles, bark folds into buckets, bamboo comes pre-divided into sealed cups — and rain, the cleanest water you will ever get for free, just needs something spread out to catch it.

## You need

- **A dried bottle gourd, if they grow near you** — a hard-shelled fruit that dries into a natural flask. Ready when it is light, tan-brown, and the seeds rattle when shaken.
- **Or bark from a downed birch or similar tree** — the papery kind that peels in flexible sheets.
- **Or a section of thick bamboo or a hollow branch** — bamboo has natural walls (nodes) inside; one closed node makes a ready-made cup bottom.
- **Pine or spruce resin** — the sticky amber sap that beads on trunk wounds; melted, it is waterproof glue for seams.
- **Cordage** — string you have twisted from plant fibre, for slings, handles, and tying covers on.
- **A cloth, tarp, or big smooth leaves** — for catching rain.
- **A knife or sharp stone, and a stick for scraping.**

## Steps

1. **Make a gourd bottle.**
   - Cut a coin-sized hole at the narrow top of a fully dried gourd.
   - Break up the dry insides with a stick and shake out seeds and pith.
   - Scour the inside with a handful of gravel and water: shake hard, pour out, repeat until clean. Then let it dry open-mouthed for a day or two.
   - **Taste-test water left in it overnight — sip and spit.** If it tastes bitter, scour and rinse again; if the bitterness stays, use that gourd for washing water only. Bitter gourd taste can mean a natural substance that upsets the gut.
   - Tie a cordage sling around the gourd's narrow waist; a whittled wooden plug makes the stopper.
2. **Fold a bark container.** Cut a rectangle of pliable bark, warm it near the fire so it bends without cracking, fold the corners up, and pin the folds with split sticks or cordage stitches. Drip melted resin along every seam inside.
3. **Cut a bamboo canteen.** Saw through the stalk just *below* one node, and again just below the *next* node up: the lower node stays inside your piece as the sealed bottom, and the top cut — made before the next node — leaves the mouth open. Bind a cordage ring around the rim so it does not split, and add a sling.
4. **Catch rain.** Stretch your cloth or tarp at a slant between sticks, with the low corner draining into a container. No cloth? Angle big leaves into a chain, each dripping into the next.
   - After a dry spell, let the first minutes of rain wash the dust off your catcher before you start collecting.
5. **Set up storage at camp.** Keep the main supply off the ground on stones or a rack, in shade, every mouth covered — a tied-down cloth at minimum, a fitted lid or plug when you can.
6. **Split your water into two families and mark them.** Boiled drinking water in one set of vessels, raw washing water in another; never mix them, and pour out rather than dipping in.

## Watch out

- **Uncovered water goes bad fast.** Dust, mice, and drowned insects find it, and in warm weather mosquitoes lay eggs — wriggling larvae within days. Covered, shaded, off the ground beats all of that.
- **Old stored water is suspect water.** Drink stored water within a couple of days; after that, boil it again. If it smells odd or feels slimy, scrub the vessel with boiling water and start over.
- **Hot resin sticks and burns.** Melt it gently at the fire's edge and apply with a stick, never a finger; it catches fire easily.
- **Bitter means no.** Bitterness in water is a stop sign, whatever the vessel. Do not push through it.
- **Never reuse a container that held fuel, poison, or you-don't-know-what.** No amount of rinsing makes it trustworthy.
- **Cut bamboo bites.** Split edges are knife-sharp; smooth the rim on a rough stone before it goes near your mouth.

## How it works

A gourd's shell is dense woody tissue that dries watertight — people on every continent used them for thousands of years before pottery. Resin waterproofs because it hardens as it cools, filling every gap. And covering works for a plain reason: almost everything that ruins stored water has to get in from above.
