---
title: Clean and cook your catch
summary: Gut and skin a fish or small animal, waste nothing, and cook it all the way through.
difficulty: steady
time: half a day
needs: [stone-tools, cook-on-fire]
---

A caught fish or rabbit is not food yet. The germs that make people sick live in its guts and on its skin — almost never in the muscle of a healthy animal. Cleaning is simply the job of separating the safe part from the dirty part without letting them touch. Do it soon: guts start to spoil within an hour or two of the kill, and warm weather makes that faster.

The second half of the job is heat. Wild meat and freshwater fish can carry parasites — small worms that live inside the flesh — and full cooking kills every one of them. Raw or pink is never an option out here.

## You need

- **A sharp blade** — a fresh stone flake works; sharp matters more than big, because a shallow, controlled cut is the whole skill.
- **A fire burned down to coals** — glowing embers cook evenly; flames just burn the outside and leave the middle raw.
- **Clean water** — boiled if you have it — for rinsing the meat and your hands.
- **A clean work surface** — a slab of bark, a flat stone, or big leaves. Never bare dirt.
- **Two spots for the parts** — one for food, one for scraps (bait, or a hole to bury them away from camp and water).

## Steps

1. **Kill quickly, then start right away.** A fast, humane kill and a fast cleaning give the safest, best-tasting meat.
2. **For a fish, scale it first** if it has large scales: hold the tail and scrape the blade's back edge from tail toward head until the skin feels smooth.
3. **Gut the fish.** Make a shallow cut from the vent — the small hole near the tail — forward to the jaw. Shallow means through the skin only; do not stab deep, or you burst the intestines.
   - Pull everything out of the body cavity with your fingers.
   - Scrape out the dark red line along the backbone (the kidney) with a thumbnail, then rinse the cavity.
4. **For a small animal, skin it before gutting.** Cut through the skin in a ring around each back leg, join the rings with a cut along the inside of the legs, then peel the skin down toward the head like pulling off a sock. It comes off with a steady pull.
5. **Gut the animal.** Pinch up the belly skin, make a small shallow cut, slide two fingers inside to lift the belly wall away from the guts, and extend the cut from between the back legs to the ribs. Tip the animal and let the innards roll out.
6. **Look at the liver before you keep anything.** A healthy liver is smooth and one even dark-red color. If it has white or yellow spots, the animal was sick — bury the whole carcass, do not eat any of it, and wash your hands well.
7. **Waste nothing that passed the check.** Heart, liver, and kidneys are rich food — cook them thoroughly. Bones and the head make broth. Guts become fish bait or buried fertilizer. The skin can be scraped and dried.
8. **Rinse everything, then cook it the same day.** Cut meat into pieces no thicker than your wrist and cook over coals — on green-wood skewers or a flat hot stone. Turn often.
9. **Check for doneness before anyone eats.** Fish is done when the flesh turns from glassy to milky-white all the way through and flakes apart — about 10 minutes over good coals per finger-width of thickness. Meat is done when the inside shows no pink at all, even next to the bone, and the juices run clear, not red. When in doubt, cook longer.

## Watch out

- **A burst gut is the classic mistake.** If green-brown gut contents touch the meat, rinse hard with clean water, cut away any stained flesh, and cook that piece extra thoroughly.
- **A sick animal is not food.** Thin, slow, staggering animals, or any carcass with a spotted liver or lumps of pus — bury it. Rabbits especially can carry diseases that enter through cuts in your skin, so cover any wounds on your hands before cleaning one.
- **Never eat freshwater fish raw or pink.** Its parasites die only with full cooking. Sea fish are safer but cook them fully too.
- **Wild pig and bear must be cooked gray to the center.** They can carry a worm (trichinella) that survives light cooking.
- **Cooking does not rescue spoiled meat.** Heat kills germs but not the poisons they leave behind. Sour smell or slimy feel means bury it, however hungry you are.
- **Finish clean.** Wash hands and blade when done, and never touch your eyes or mouth mid-job.

## How it works

Germs and gut contents sit on the outside and inside surfaces of the animal, not in healthy muscle — so careful, shallow cuts keep the danger away from the food. Heat above about 70°C (hotter than any water you could hold your hand in) kills both germs and parasites, and it takes time to travel to the middle of thick meat. The color change — pink to gray, glassy to milky — is your visible proof the heat got all the way through.
