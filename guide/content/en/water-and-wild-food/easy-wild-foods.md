The wild is full of food, but most of it is not worth the risk or the work. A small handful of plants are both safe to recognize and rich enough to matter. Learn those few well, and you can eat from the land in every season while your garden is still a dream.

This guide assumes you already know the safety rules from "Pick wild plants without being poisoned." Never eat a plant you cannot name with certainty.

## You need

- **Your foraging rules, already learned** — never guess, know the deadly lookalikes, test new foods slowly.
- **A bag or basket** — nuts and berries add up fast when you find a good patch.
- **A digging stick** — any strong stick with a flattened end, for roots.
- **Water for washing** — grit and dirt hide on everything that grows low.

## Steps

1. **Start with nuts in autumn.** They are the biggest prize: fat and protein that store for months.
   - **Walnuts and hazelnuts** are hard to confuse with anything dangerous. Crack and eat.
   - **Acorns** (from oak trees) are everywhere but bitter with tannin — a plant chemical that upsets your gut. Shell them, crush the insides, and soak in many changes of water over one to several days until the bitterness is gone. Then they are good food.
   - **Sweet chestnuts** have a spiny burr like a hedgehog and a tassel on the nut tip. **Horse chestnuts (conkers) are poisonous** — their husk has only a few short bumps, not dense fine spines, and the nut has no tassel. When unsure, leave it.
2. **Pick aggregate berries in summer.** An aggregate berry is one made of many small juicy beads stuck together, like a blackberry or raspberry. Nothing that looks like that in most temperate lands is deadly. Single smooth berries are a different story — **deadly nightshade** has shiny black cherry-sized berries that can kill; leave all single berries alone unless you know the plant beyond doubt.
3. **Gather greens in spring.** Young leaves are the mildest.
   - **Dandelion** — the familiar yellow flower; every part is edible, leaves best when young.
   - **Stinging nettle** — grab with covered hands; two minutes of boiling kills the sting and makes a spinach-like green rich in iron.
   - **Broadleaf plantain** — the low ribbed-leaf weed of paths and lawns, not the banana. Edible raw when young.
4. **Dig roots in autumn and winter,** when plants store their energy underground.
   - **Cattail** (the tall marsh plant with a brown sausage-shaped head) has starchy roots and edible young shoots. Only harvest it when the brown head is present or you can trace the plant to one — **iris leaves look similar and are poisonous**, and **water hemlock**, the deadliest plant in the northern half of the world, grows in the same wet ground. Its carrot-family flowers and bundled roots must never touch your food.
5. **Learn the calendar of your place.** Walk the same routes each month and note what appears: greens in spring, berries in summer, nuts and roots in autumn. In a year you will have a map of free food.
6. **Take some, leave most.** Take no more than one part in three from any patch, so the plants — and the animals that also eat them — come back next year.

## Watch out

- **Single shiny berries.** Deadly nightshade, and the white berries of baneberry, have killed foragers. Rule of thumb: aggregate berries yes, lone berries only with certain identification, white or yellow berries never.
- **Horse chestnut vs sweet chestnut.** Every autumn someone eats a conker. Check for dense fine spines and the nut's little tassel.
- **Anything carrot-like near water.** Water hemlock and hemlock kill in small amounts. Do not gather wild "wild carrots" or "wild parsnips" until you are far more experienced.
- **Bitter acorns eaten unsoaked.** Not deadly, but enough will make you sick and stop you absorbing food. Soak until a nibble tastes bland.
- **Roadside and sprayed ground.** Plants soak up exhaust metals and weedkiller. Gather at least 50 steps from busy roads and never from land that looks treated.

## How it works

Plants concentrate their energy where the season demands: leaves in spring, fruit in summer, seeds and roots in autumn. Following that rhythm means you always harvest the part where the food actually is. And the "easy" foods are easy precisely because their dangerous lookalikes are few and well known — which is why the short lookalike list above is the most important part of this page.
