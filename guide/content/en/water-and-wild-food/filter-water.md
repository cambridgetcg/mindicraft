---
title: Make a charcoal and sand filter
summary: Layer cloth, sand, and crushed charcoal so cloudy water runs out clear — then boil it anyway.

Sometimes the only water you can find is brown soup — mud, leaf bits, a swampy smell. Boiling kills the germs in it, but it does not remove the dirt, and boiled mud is still mud: gritty, foul-tasting, and hard on your stomach. A filter fixes that. Water trickles down through layers of gravel, sand, and crushed charcoal, and each layer catches something the one above missed.

One rule before anything else: **a filter cleans, it does not disinfect.** Germs are small enough to slip through the finest sand. Filter first to make the water clear, then boil it to make it safe. Always both, in that order.

## You need

- **A tall container with a small hole at the bottom** — a hollowed log or thick bamboo section, a bark cone rolled and tied, or a bottle with the base cut off, hung upside down. Knee-high is a good size.
- **Charcoal** — real wood charcoal from your own fire (see "Make charcoal"): black, light, and shiny-broken. Never use ash, part-burnt wood, or shop briquettes, which are glued with chemicals.
- **Sand** — clean and washed, from a stream bed or dug from below the surface. Rinse it in water until the rinse runs clear.
- **Gravel or small pebbles** — pea-sized to thumb-sized, also rinsed.
- **Tightly woven cloth** — two pieces: one inside the filter, one to strain water before it goes in.
- **Something to crush with** — a rock or club, and a flat stone or hide to crush the charcoal on.
- **A clean container underneath** — to catch the good water.

## Steps

1. **Crush the charcoal.** Break it down to grains between rice-sized and coarse-sand-sized. Too chunky and water rushes past; powder packs solid or washes through. Aim for "coarse black sand."
2. **Plug the bottom.** Fold cloth into a pad and press it over the hole inside the container, then hold it down with a layer of pebbles. This keeps the layers in and lets water out.
3. **Build the layers, bottom to top.** Each main layer about a hand-width (10 centimetres) deep:
   - **Gravel** on the cloth — the coarse base.
   - **Sand** on the gravel.
   - **Crushed charcoal** — the heart of the filter.
   - **Sand** again, to hold the charcoal down.
   - **Gravel or a flat stone** on top, so poured water does not gouge a hole in the layers.
4. **Rinse the filter itself.** Pour water through and watch it come out grey with charcoal dust. Keep pouring — a few full containers' worth — until it runs clear. Throw all that grey water away.
5. **Pre-strain, then filter.** Pour source water through your spare cloth first to catch leaves and grit, then pour it slowly into the top of the filter. Catch what drips from the bottom.
   - Slow is good — a trickle means real time against the charcoal. If it gushes through, the grains are too coarse or a layer has channeled; rebuild it.
6. **Boil everything the filter gives you.** No exceptions. Clear is not the same as safe.
7. **Rest the filter dry between uses.** Let it drain fully; a filter left soggy for days grows slime that makes water worse, not better.

## Watch out

- **The deadly shortcut: drinking straight from the filter.** Clear, sweet-tasting water can still be full of germs. This filter's job is clarity and taste — boiling's job is safety.
- **Wrong charcoal.** Ash is not charcoal — it makes bitter, harsh water. Briquettes carry binders and lighter chemicals. Use only black char from plain wood.
- **A tired filter turns traitor.** When filtered water starts to smell or taste of the source again, the charcoal is full. Dig out the charcoal, burn it, and repack fresh — every few weeks in regular use, sooner for very dirty water.
- **Channels cheat the layers.** If water finds one fast path down the side, it skips the cleaning. Pour gently onto the top stone, and repack if you see the layers slumping.
- **Chemical-tainted water stays tainted enough to matter.** Charcoal grabs some chemicals but you cannot know how much. Water smelling of fuel, or from near mines and sprayed fields, should be left alone, filter or no filter.

## How it works

Sand strains: particles bigger than the gaps between grains get stuck, and each layer's gaps are finer than the last. Charcoal does something stranger — it is riddled with microscopic pores, so much that one thumb-sized lump hides an inner surface bigger than a garden. Bad-tasting and bad-smelling substances drift into those pores and stick to the walls, a trick called adsorption. That is why charcoal fixes taste and smell — and why, once its pores are full, it stops working and must be replaced.
