title: Boil water to make it safe

difficulty: easy
time: an hour

Clear, cold, delicious-looking water can still make you badly sick. The dangers are germs — living things far too small to see — washed in from animal droppings, dead things upstream, or dirty hands. They cause days of vomiting and diarrhea, and diarrhea in the wild is not just miserable: it drains the water out of you faster than you can drink, and that can kill. One hour of boiling discipline prevents almost all of it.

The good news: every disease germ that lives in water dies from heat. You do not need chemicals or filters to kill them. You need a fire, a container, and one simple visual signal — the rolling boil.

## You need

- **Water** — the clearest you can find. Boiling kills germs but does not remove mud or chemicals, so start with the best source you have.
- **A fire** — steady flames or good coals, enough to keep a pot hot for a while.
- **A container that can sit on fire** — a metal pot or can is ideal. Scorch off any paint or plastic lining outside your drinking batch first.
- **No metal? Then fist-sized dry stones** — from a dry hillside, never from a river or damp ground (wet stones can burst in fire). You will heat these and drop them into a wooden, bark, or hide container of water.
- **A cloth** — to strain cloudy water before boiling, and to cover the water after.
- **Two long green sticks** — as tongs for moving hot stones, if you use the stone method.

## Steps

1. **Strain the water through cloth** into your container if it is cloudy. This removes dirt and wrigglers, not germs — it just makes the water nicer and the boiling more effective.
2. **Put the container on the fire**, resting on stones or hung above the flames. Fill it no more than three-quarters full so it does not boil over.
3. **Wait for a rolling boil.** That means big bubbles rising fast all across the surface, so violently that stirring does not calm them. Small bubbles on the bottom or a gentle shimmer is *not* enough — wait for the full roll.
4. **Hold the rolling boil for one full minute.** Count it out or watch it steadily.
   - High in the mountains — roughly above 2,000 metres (6,500 feet) — boil for three minutes instead. Water boils at a lower temperature up there, so give the heat more time.
5. **No pot? Use hot stones.** Heat several fist-sized dry stones in the fire for about half an hour, then lift them with your green-stick tongs and lower them one at a time into the water container. Keep adding hot stones until the water reaches and holds a rolling boil for a minute. It hisses, spits, and steams — keep your face back.
6. **Cover the water and let it cool** on its own. Do not add cold untreated water to "speed it up" — that undoes everything.
7. **Store it covered and pour, don't dip.** Dipping a dirty cup or hand into boiled water re-poisons the whole batch.

## Watch out

- **"It looks clean" means nothing.** Germs are invisible. Boil every drop that goes in your mouth — including water for rinsing food, washing wounds, and brushing teeth.
- **Boiling does not fix everything.** Salt water, water tasting of fuel or chemicals, and water near mines or sprayed fields stay dangerous no matter how long you boil. Find a different source. Never drink seawater — boiling it makes it saltier.
- **Warm is not boiled.** The most common failure is impatience: water taken off at "steaming" can still carry live germs. Wait for the true rolling boil.
- **River and wet stones can explode in fire.** Trapped water inside turns to steam and bursts the rock, throwing hot shards. Use only stones from dry ground.
- **Scalds are the everyday injury here.** Set the pot where it cannot tip, keep children back, and lift with cloth or sticks, never bare hands.
- **Re-dirtying is silent.** A fly, a dusty lid, a dipped finger — keep boiled water covered and use it within a day or two, or boil again.

## How it works

Germs are made of delicate machinery — proteins — that heat wrecks the way heat wrecks a raw egg: once cooked, it cannot go back. Most disease germs are already dying by about 70°C (158°F), well below boiling. But you have no thermometer, and a rolling boil is one you can see with your own eyes: when the water rolls, you *know* it got hot enough, everywhere in the pot.
