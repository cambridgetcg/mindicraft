A snare is a small loop of cord hung in an animal's path: the animal walks through, the loop tightens, and it is held until you come. It is the quietest way to catch meat — no chasing, no strength needed, and it works all night while you sleep. Rabbits and similar small animals are the target; nothing bigger.

Two duties come with this tool, and they are not optional. First: check every snare at dawn and dusk at the least, so no animal is left suffering. Second: count your snares and take every one down when you are done — a forgotten snare keeps catching forever, for no one. In many countries snaring is restricted or banned in normal times; this is knowledge for when you must feed yourself from the land, not a hobby to try in the park.

## You need

- **Strong thin cordage** — an arm's length per snare, your tightest two-ply string, about as thick as a matchstick. It must slide easily and hold a hard pull.
- **A game trail** — a narrow worn path through grass or brush, marked by flattened vegetation, droppings (rabbit droppings are pea-sized round balls), and tunnels through low growth. Snares only work where animals already walk.
- **A stout anchor** — a living sapling, root, or a stake driven deep. The anchor takes all the force.
- **A few twigs** — to prop the loop and to fence the trail edges.
- **A knife or sharp stone** — for cutting cord and pointing stakes.

## Steps

1. **Make the noose.** Tie a small fixed loop, the size of your fingertip, in one end of the cord. Pass the other end through it. Now you have a running loop that tightens when pulled and stays tight.
2. **Find fresh sign.** Walk the edges of clearings at first light. Choose a trail with fresh droppings and pressed-down grass — a used road, not an old one.
3. **Size and place the loop.** For a rabbit, open the loop to about the size of your fist (roughly 10 cm across) and hang it over the trail with its bottom edge one hand's width (about 7–10 cm) above the ground — head height for a hopping rabbit.
4. **Anchor it hard.** Tie the free end to your anchor with two firm knots. Test with a strong steady pull; if the anchor shifts, find a better one.
5. **Prop and fence.** Rest the loop against a light twig pushed into the ground so it holds its shape and position. Push a few small sticks into the trail edges on both sides, so the easiest path leads exactly through the loop. Make it look natural — animals shy from fresh, obvious changes.
6. **Set several, and write them down.** One snare rarely catches. Set 4–6 along different trails, and record each location — a scratched map, a knot-count, marked landmarks. You must be able to find every single one again.
7. **Check at dawn and dusk, every day, no exceptions.** An animal in a snare must not wait. If you catch one, end its life at once with one hard, decisive blow to the back of the head — quick and certain, never hesitant. If a snare has caught something you did not intend, free it if you safely can.
8. **Clean and cook thoroughly.** Skin and gut the animal away from camp, wash your hands well, and cook the meat until no pink remains anywhere.
9. **Strike camp clean.** Moving on? Take down every snare, checked against your list.

## Watch out

- **Snares left unchecked.** This is the one unforgivable failure: it means slow suffering and wasted meat. If you cannot check twice a day, do not set snares at all.
- **Paths people or dogs use.** Never set a snare where a pet, livestock, or a person might walk. Choose tunnels through brush too low and tight for anything but small game.
- **Weak cord or a loose anchor.** A caught animal pulls with shocking force; poor cord means an escaped animal trailing a cruel loop. Test every part hard before you leave it.
- **Loop the wrong size or height.** Too big or too low catches a leg instead of the neck — a worse outcome for the animal and for you. Fist-sized, one hand up, for rabbits.
- **Sick-looking animals.** If a catch was sluggish, thin, or covered in sores, do not eat it. Rabbits in particular can carry tularemia, a disease people catch through skin — cover any cuts on your hands when skinning, wash afterwards, and cook the meat completely through.
- **Bare-ground trails with no fresh droppings.** Old trails waste days. Fresh sign or move on.

## How it works

A snare succeeds because small animals reuse exact routes night after night, placing their feet in the same prints. The loop simply stands where the animal's head already goes, and the running knot turns the animal's own forward push into the closing force. That is also why placement is everything and strength is nothing: the animal, not you, powers the catch.
