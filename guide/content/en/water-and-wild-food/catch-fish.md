Fish are the steadiest wild meat there is: they gather in predictable places, they bite all year, and a trap catches them while you do other work — or sleep. With string you have twisted and a hook you have carved, you can fish forever without buying anything.

Two tools, two days. Day one: hook and hand line. Day two: the funnel trap.

## You need

- **Strong thin cordage** — several arm-spans of your best two-ply string for the line, and rougher string for tying the trap.
- **A bone sliver or a stout thorn** — for the hook. Bone from a cooked meal, split and ground sharp, or a hard thorn from hawthorn or a similar tree.
- **Bait** — worms, insects, shellfish scraps, or a bit of any meat. Fish are not fussy.
- **Long flexible sticks** — about 30 straight shoots, finger-thick and arm-long (willow is perfect), for weaving the trap.
- **A good spot** — a narrow, slow part of a stream, the mouth of a pool, or a calm bay edge. Fish travel along edges and through narrows.

## Steps

1. **Carve a gorge hook.** This is the oldest hook: not curved, just a straight sliver of bone or hard wood, about as long as your thumbnail is wide (2–3 cm), sharpened to a point at both ends, with a shallow groove cut around its middle.
2. **Tie the line to the groove** so the sliver hangs level, like a tiny seesaw balanced on the string.
3. **Bury the gorge in bait.** Thread a worm or wrap meat around it so the whole sliver lies hidden lengthwise inside the bait.
4. **Fish the hand line.** Lower the bait near the bottom or beside weeds. When you feel steady pulling — not just nibbles — wait three slow breaths, then pull firmly. The fish swallows the bait, the sliver turns sideways inside, and it holds like a toggle. Keep the line tight and bring the fish in.
5. **Weave the funnel trap** (day two). Picture a basket shaped like a rugby ball with one open end, and a separate cone that fits into that opening pointing inward.
   - Make the body: lash the tips of a dozen long sticks together, spread the other ends into a circle about two hands wide, and weave thinner shoots around and around them to make a closed basket the length of your arm.
   - Make the funnel: a short open cone from the remaining sticks, its wide mouth matching the basket's opening, its narrow inner end just wide enough for your fist.
   - Tie the funnel into the basket mouth, pointing inward, so the trap has an easy way in and a hard way out.
6. **Set the trap in moving water.** Place it in a narrow channel with the funnel mouth facing downstream, so fish nosing their way upstream swim straight in. Pile rocks or push in sticks on both sides — a fence called a weir — so the trap is the only way through. Weigh it down with stones. Drop bait inside. In still water, just sink it baited near weeds.
7. **Check the trap every morning and evening.** Take your catch, re-bait, reset.
8. **Cook every fish fully.** Gut it (slit the belly, remove the innards), then roast or boil until the flesh is opaque and flakes apart. Freshwater fish especially can carry parasites — worms killed only by real cooking. No raw freshwater fish, ever.

## Watch out

- **Pulling too soon on the hand line.** A gorge hook only works after the bait is swallowed. Nibbles mean wait; a steady pull means strike.
- **Raw or rare freshwater fish.** Parasites in raw fish can live in you for years. Cook until it flakes; no exceptions.
- **Fish spines and gill covers.** Many fish have sharp defensive spines. Grip firmly across the body behind the head, and treat any puncture like a dirty wound: wash it well.
- **Deep or fast water.** No fish is worth being swept away, and cold water steals your strength in minutes. Set traps where the water is below your knees.
- **A trap that gaps.** Fish escape through any hole wider than two fingers. Weave tight, and check the funnel-to-basket joint first when a baited trap comes up empty.
- **Leaving a trap behind.** An abandoned trap keeps killing for no one. Take it with you or break it open when you move on.

## How it works

A funnel is a one-way door made of geometry: fish follow the wide walls inward with ease, but the way out is a small dark hole they rarely find, because fish search along edges, not toward the center. The gorge hook works on the same trick of angles — it slides in straight and easy, then turns crosswise, and what fit one way cannot come back the other.
