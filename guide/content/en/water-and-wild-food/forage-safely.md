The wild is full of food — and salted through it, a few plants that can kill an adult with one mouthful. The killers look like dinner: the deadliest plants are wild cousins of the carrot, and the deadliest mushrooms look like market mushrooms. Hunger makes people gamble; foragers who live long lives never do, because they follow one rule.

**The iron rule: never eat anything you cannot surely name.** Not "pretty sure," not "it looks like the picture" — every feature checked, no doubt left. If doubt remains, it is not food.

## You need

- **A teacher, if you can possibly find one** — one afternoon walking with a local who forages beats a month of guessing.
- **Patience** — you will learn three to five plants this week, not thirty. That is correct, not slow.
- **A way to keep notes** — drawings, pressed leaves, anything that fixes what you learned.
- **A small bag** — plus a sample of everything you eat, in case someone must later know what it was.

## Steps

1. **Swear the iron rule out loud**, with whoever forages with you — especially children: *if you cannot surely name it, it does not touch your lips.*
2. **Learn three to five unmistakable local plants — completely.** Good first choices in much of the world: stinging nettle (eaten cooked; the sting cooks away), dandelion, broadleaf plantain (a low roadside leaf, not the banana), cattail, and blackberry or raspberry. For each, learn *every* feature: leaf, stem, flower, smell when crushed, where it grows, how it looks in each season. If you can name only some features, you do not know the plant yet.
3. **Learn the forbidden groups and skip them entirely as a beginner:**
   - **All mushrooms.** The death cap and destroying angel kill more foragers than everything else combined, look harmless, and reportedly taste fine. Symptoms start 6 to 24 hours later, sometimes ease — then the liver fails. No mushroom passes the iron rule without expert knowledge. None.
   - **The wild carrot-and-parsley family** — lacy leaves, umbrella-shaped heads of tiny white flowers. Poison hemlock and water hemlock hide among its edible members — water hemlock's root even smells of parsnip — and a few bites can kill. Leave the whole family alone.
   - **White and yellow berries** — very often poisonous.
   - **Look-alike bulbs.** Death camas mimics wild onion; lily-of-the-valley leaves mimic wild garlic. If it does not smell strongly of onion or garlic through and through, it is not your onion.
   - **Tempting berries you cannot name.** Deadly nightshade berries look like sweet black cherries; unripe pokeweed mimics elderberry; every part of yew is deadly except the red berry-flesh — including the seed inside it.
4. **Distrust the folk shortcuts.** "Birds eat it, so it's safe" — false; birds eat deadly nightshade unharmed. "Boiling makes anything safe" — false. "Red means danger, blue means safe" — false both ways. One honest exception: a berry built like a raspberry — many tiny juice-balls in a cluster — has no deadly copy, but name it anyway.
5. **Confirm each new plant three times** — different spots, different days — before the first taste.
6. **First-taste protocol, even for a surely named plant:** eat a small amount of one new food only, cooked if it is normally cooked, then wait a full day before eating more. Never test two new foods at once, or you will not know which one went wrong.
7. **Harvest clean and gentle.** Skip plants beside roads, sprayed fields, and dirty water. Take a little from many plants, never all of one patch, so food is there next season.

## Watch out

- **Hunger is the real poison-pusher.** Nearly every foraging death starts with "we were hungry and it looked right." Decide now, on a full stomach, that the rule holds on the worst day. A hungry day is survivable; hemlock is not.
- **Children eat with their eyes.** Bright berries look like candy. Teach the rule as a game: name it fully, or bring it to a grown-up — never mouth-first.
- **"I feel fine" can be a trap.** Some poisons — above all the deadly mushrooms — show nothing for hours.
- **If poisoning is suspected:** stop eating, keep the person resting and sipping safe water, keep a sample of the plant, and get real medical care fast — a doctor, a poison line, any trained help. No folk antidotes; speed to real care saves people.

## How it works

Plants cannot run, so many defend themselves with chemistry — a few strong enough to stop a human heart or liver. No look, smell, or taste reliably marks the deadly ones; the only defence that has ever worked is knowing exactly which plant is which. That is why foraging cultures teach a few plants at a time, named surely, learned from someone who knows.
