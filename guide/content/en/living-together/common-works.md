One family can raise a house. No family alone can dig a proper well, bridge a river, or raise a hall big enough for the whole village. These shared things — call them common works — are what turn a cluster of houses into a village. So a common work is really two builds at once — the thing itself, and the agreement around it. The agreement is the harder build.

## You need

- **A council decision** — the work chosen, out loud, by the people who will sweat for it. A build imposed by one family stays that family's build.
- **A clear, small first project.** Water usually comes first: a well, a protected spring, or a rain store; then paths, a footbridge, the hall. Pick something finishable in one season — the village needs an early win.
- **A work-shares list** — every household's promised contribution written down or witnessed: days of labor, materials, or food for the workers. What is promised in public gets done.
- **A steward** — one person the council names to keep the plan, call work days, and track shares. A steward serves the plan; they do not own the well.
- **Materials and tools gathered before the first work day** — stone, timber, rope, digging tools, and the skills from raising a house.

## Steps

1. **Choose the work in council, and say plainly what it is for.** "A well within two hundred paces of every door." "A bridge a loaded person can cross in flood season." A clear purpose settles later arguments about size and site.
2. **Walk the ground together and pick the site.** For a well: low ground where greenery stays lush in dry spells, but at least 30 meters from, and uphill of, any latrine or animal pen — filth flows downhill through soil too. For a bridge: the narrowest point with firm banks. For a hall: central, level, and dry.
3. **Break the work into named stages.** Dig, line, cover; or footings, beams, deck, rails. Each stage gets a rough number of work-days. Small stages show progress, and progress feeds effort.
4. **Divide the burden by ability, in public.** Strong backs give labor days; skilled hands give craft; those who can't dig give tools, timber, or the workers' midday meal. Aim for equal sacrifice, not identical tasks. Record it.
5. **Hold set work days, not "whenever."** Fixed days — say two mornings a week — with the steward calling them ahead of time. Feed everyone at midday; a fed crew comes back.
6. **Build to house standards or better.** A common work carries the whole village's weight. Foundations deeper, timbers thicker, joints checked twice.
7. **Finish completely — including the boring ends.** The well gets its cover and wall so children and animals can't fall in; the bridge gets rails; the hall gets its door. The last tenth of the work prevents most future deaths and repairs.
8. **Agree the upkeep before the celebration.** Who inspects it each season? What does each household give yearly to repairs? What are the use-rules — whose turn at dawn, what the bridge may carry in flood? Decide now, in the glow of finishing, not later, in a quarrel.
9. **Then celebrate.** Open it with a feast and give it a name — a named work is one people defend and maintain.

## Watch out

- **Starting with the grandest project.** A half-built hall demoralizes; a finished well recruits.
- **The free-rider rot.** One household skipping work days while using the well breeds ten more. The steward's list, read at council, keeps shares honest. The remedy is catching up the missed days, decided by agreed rules.
- **Nobody responsible for upkeep.** "Everyone's job" means no one's job. Every common work needs its steward and its repair-share, forever.
- **One person quietly owning the shared thing.** If the well sits on someone's land or the hall key lives in one pocket, agree access rules in council first and witness them.
- **Skipping safety on the ends.** Uncovered wells drown children; unrailed bridges drown adults; a hall without two ways out is a fire trap. These are the first things to build properly, not the last.
- **Building past the village's strength.** If the work stalls for a month, shrink it in council rather than abandoning it. A modest finished bridge beats a magnificent abandoned one.

## How it works

Each household gives a little labor and gets back a thing worth far more than its share, because a well or bridge serves everyone at once. The written shares and the steward guard the one weakness: the temptation to let others do the work. And a village that has built together trusts together, which makes the next work easier.
