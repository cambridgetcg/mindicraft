Put ten people together and quarrels will come: a borrowed tool broken, a fence in the wrong place, a harsh word at the wrong moment. The quarrel itself is not the danger. The danger is a quarrel with no ending — one that turns into a grudge, then a feud, then two families who won't share a well. Villages rarely die of hunger first. They die of grudges.

The cure is a way to end disputes that everyone trusts, built **before** anyone is angry. Rules made in the heat of one fight always favor someone. Rules made calmly, in council, when no one knows who will need them, feel fair to all — because they were.

## You need

- **Agreed rules, written or memorized, made in council beforehand** — a short list of what counts as a wrong (taking, breaking, hurting, cheating) and what usually mends it. Five to ten rules is plenty to start. If your village can write, write them where all can see.
- **A hearer** — one calm, respected person, or three, who listen and decide. Three is better: an odd number can't tie, and three are harder to bully or bribe than one.
- **A set time and open place** — disputes heard in daylight, in front of the village, within a few days of the complaint. Secrecy breeds rumour; delay breeds rage.
- **Both people willing to accept the outcome** — get their word on this out loud, before the hearing starts, in front of everyone.

## Steps

1. **Cool it down first.** When a quarrel flares, separate the two people and set a hearing day — usually one to three days away. Sleep shrinks most fights by half.
2. **Open the hearing in front of the village.** The hearer says the rule being used and asks both people to promise, aloud, to accept the decision.
3. **Let the first person speak all the way to the end.** No interruptions — not from the other side, not from the crowd, not from the hearer except to ask "what happened next?" Being fully heard is half of every settlement.
4. **Let the second person speak all the way to the end.** Same protection. Same patience.
5. **Hear witnesses, one at a time.** Anyone who saw or knows something speaks. Ask each: "Did you see it yourself, or did someone tell you?" Only what they saw themselves counts fully; the rest is rumour.
6. **Name the facts everyone now agrees on.** The hearer says them plainly: "The pot was borrowed. The pot came back cracked." Often the dispute shrinks to one small disagreement once the agreed facts are laid out.
7. **Decide by the rule, and aim to repair, not to wound.** The best remedies restore: replace the pot, redo the work, give back the days of labor. A remedy should mend the loss and cost the wrongdoer something real — but never so much that it starts the next feud.
8. **Close it out loud.** Both people say, in front of all: "It is settled." Then it is over. No one may bring it up again as a weapon.
9. **Check in a week later.** The hearer quietly asks both people if the remedy was done. An unpaid remedy is a new dispute wearing old clothes.

## Watch out

- **Judging your own case.** A hearer who is kin to either side, or has anything to gain, must step aside. Even honest people cannot see straight over their own fence.
- **Deciding before the second person speaks.** Every story sounds true until you hear the other one. Force yourself to hold no opinion until both are done.
- **Punishments that create wounds.** Shaming, beating, or ruining someone ends this dispute and plants three more. Repair, don't destroy.
- **Trial by gossip.** If the village judges around fires before the hearing, the hearing becomes theatre. Set the hearing fast and ask people to save their views for it.
- **Letting the strong ignore the outcome.** If a big family can shrug off a decision, the rules are dead for everyone. The whole village must back the remedy — quietly refusing help and trade to whoever defies it is usually enough.
- **Rules nobody remembers.** Recite them at council a few times a year. A rule no one can say is a rule no one agreed to.

## How it works

People can bear losing far better than they can bear being unheard or cheated. A full, uninterrupted telling drains most of the anger; a decision made by a rule that existed *before* the fight can't be called favoritism; a remedy that repairs removes the reason to retaliate. Together these turn a quarrel from a fire that spreads into a fire that burns out.
