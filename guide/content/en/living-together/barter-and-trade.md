No household makes everything it needs. You have extra eggs; your neighbor has extra rope. Trade — swapping one thing for another, which people also call **barter** — turns two piles of extra into two solved problems. A village that trades is richer than the same village hoarding, because each person can do more of what they do best.

But trade only works while people trust it. One rigged scale, one broken promise, and neighbors stop swapping and go back to scraping by alone. So the craft here is not haggling — it is honesty you can *show*.

## You need

- **Something extra** — goods you can spare after your own needs are met. Never trade away your seed grain or your last blanket.
- **Honest measures** — a scale, a marked stick, a standard basket or jug. Both sides must be able to check the amount themselves; that is the whole point of shared measures.
- **Knowledge of what things are worth locally** — ask around before you trade: what did a chicken fetch last month?
- **A way to record promises** — a written note, two matching tally sticks, or two witnesses, for any trade not finished on the spot.

## Steps

1. **Know your surplus and your need.** Write two short lists: "I can spare…" and "I lack…". Trade from the first list only.
2. **Find out the going rate.** Ask three people what a fair swap looks like before you agree to anything. One opinion can be wrong or self-serving; three sketch the truth.
3. **Let the other side inspect everything.** Open the sack. Pour the grain through your fingers to show there are no stones at the bottom. Let them heft the tool, taste the honey, count the eggs. Hiding flaws wins one trade and loses all future ones.
4. **Measure in front of each other.** Weigh, count, or fill the standard container while both watch. If they bring their own measure, use both and compare — an honest trader welcomes the check.
5. **State the deal out loud, completely, before handing anything over.** "Two baskets of apples, this size, today, for one repaired axe by market day." Amount, quality, time, and place — all four, spoken plainly. Vague deals breed feuds.
6. **Swap on the spot when you can.** Goods for goods, hand to hand, then say "done" and mean it. A finished trade cannot rot.
7. **When you can't swap on the spot, make the promise solid.**
   - Write it down twice — one copy each — or notch two tally sticks together so the notches match and neither side can add marks alone.
   - Name a witness both sides trust.
   - Set an exact day for delivery, not "soon."
8. **Deliver early or explain early.** If you will be late or short, walk over and say so before the due day, and offer how you'll make it right. Bad news delivered early is honesty; delivered late, it is theft.
9. **Build a reputation on purpose.** Give slightly generous measure — the extra egg, the topped-up jug. It costs little and makes you the neighbor everyone prefers to trade with.

## Watch out

- **Rigged or worn measures.** A scale with a heavy pan, a "standard" basket with a false bottom. Test measures against a known weight or against each other before trading, and check your own too — worn tools drift without malice.
- **Good layer on top, bad goods below.** The classic cheat: fine apples covering bruised ones. Always inspect from the bottom of the sack, and let others inspect yours the same way.
- **Trading away what you'll need in winter.** Surplus in autumn can be starvation in February. Set aside your household's yearly need first, and treat it as untouchable.
- **Vague promises.** "I'll help you out sometime" is not a trade; it is a future argument. If it isn't finished today, it must be exact: what, how much, what quality, by when.
- **Pressure and rushing.** Anyone who says "decide right now or the deal's off" is usually hiding something. Walking away is always allowed; sleep on any big trade.
- **One-sided deals with the desperate.** Squeezing a hungry neighbor for triple value works exactly once, and the whole village remembers. Trade fair even when you don't have to — especially then.

## How it works

Every trade is really two exchanges: goods for goods, and trust for trust. The goods are spent at once; the trust compounds. Shared measures and clear spoken terms exist so that neither side has to rely on memory or hope — the deal can be checked by anyone. A village full of checkable deals trades more, specializes more, and eats better than one where every swap is a gamble.
