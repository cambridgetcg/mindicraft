A village is not a pile of houses. It is a promise a group makes to one place and to each other: we will stay, we will feed ourselves, we will decide together. Everything else in this book is a chapter. This is the binding.

You will not finish it. A village is planted like a tree: you do the first seasons well, and people not yet born do the rest.

## You need

- **People — roughly 20 to 150.** Fewer than 20, and one bad season or feud can end it; more than 150, and strangers appear before the habits are set.
- **A year of food, or a plan for one.** The first harvest often fails or comes late, and hunger is the number one village-killer.
- **Water you have tested through the seasons.** A spring or stream that runs in the driest month, not just the wet one.
- **The three skills from the earlier chapters, in living heads.** A builder of shared works, a grower and storer of grain, and the habit of council.
- **A clear claim to the land.** Agreed with neighbors, rulers, or whoever else believes they own it. A village built on a disputed claim is a war waiting.
- **Written first rules.** Even ten lines: how decisions are made, how newcomers join, how someone may leave with their fair share.

## Steps

1. **Choose the site — spend a full year on it if you can.** Walk it in every season. You want:
   - drinking water within about 400 meters (a five-minute carry),
   - ground above the highest flood you can find evidence of,
   - soil that grows things (dark, crumbly, full of worms),
   - some shelter from the coldest wind, and sun on the fields.
2. **Settle the claim before the first post goes in.** Meet everyone with a stake in the land, write down what was agreed and who witnessed it, and mark the boundaries.
3. **Secure water first.** Protect the spring, dig the well, or cut the channel before building houses. People can sleep rough for a month; they cannot go dry for three days.
4. **Build the store before the fine houses.** A dry, raised, rodent-proofed granary and a tool shed. Sleep in simple shelters for now — a village that stores well outlasts one that builds pretty.
5. **Break ground on fields in the first season, even a small one.** One planted field teaches you the soil, rain, and pests a year early. Save seed from the best plants for next year.
6. **Lay out the commons.** Mark the meeting ground, water point, paths, and shared works — then keep them common. Write it in the rules so no one fences them later.
7. **Hold the council on a fixed rhythm** — every new moon, say — even when there is nothing to decide. The habit itself holds the village together. Record decisions in one book kept in one place.
8. **Add households slowly, one or two at a time.** Each newcomer learns the rules, contributes to the store, and gets a voice at council. Growth faster than trust is how villages split.
9. **Plant the long things early: trees, terraces, ponds, orchards.** They pay off in ten to thirty years, so year one is the cheapest time to start.
10. **Train your replacements.** Every keeper of a skill — well, granary, council book — teaches at least one younger person. A village where knowledge lives in one head is one funeral from collapse.

## Watch out

- **Beautiful sites that flood or dry up.** A riverside meadow is often a floodplain. Ask the oldest people nearby how high the water has ever come; build above that line.
- **Eating the seed grain.** Store next year's seed separately, sealed, and make opening it a council decision, never one person's.
- **A founder who never steps back.** If everything runs through one person, the village dies with them. Push decisions to the council early.
- **Unwritten deals.** "Everyone knows" lasts one generation at most. Boundaries, shares, and rules go in the book, with dates and witnesses.
- **Growing for growth's sake.** Every ten new people need more water, more stored grain, and more patience at council.
- **Feuds left to fester.** One unresolved grievance splits more villages than any drought. Use the council to hear disputes early and in the open.

## How it works

A village survives on three loops: food grown and stored, water protected and shared, and grievances aired before they turn to hatred. The works — well, granary, fields — keep bodies alive through the first winters; the council and the written rules keep the group alive through the first quarrels. When both outlast their builders, the camp has become a village.
