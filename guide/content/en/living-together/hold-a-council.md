Some choices are too big for one person to make for everyone: where to dig the well, whether to take in newcomers. Decide those alone and half the group resents it; leave them undecided and they fester into feuds. A **council** — a meeting where everyone affected sits together, speaks in turn, and decides as a group — is the oldest tool for this.

A good council is not talent — it is a small set of habits any group can learn in a week.

## You need

- **Everyone the decision touches, or a person each household sends to speak for it** — a decision made without someone is a decision they'll ignore.
- **A circle to sit in** — around a fire, a table, a tree. A circle has no head, so no one talks down to the rest.
- **A talking token** — a stick, a stone, anything you can hold up and pass. Only the holder speaks; everyone else listens.
- **One person to keep the meeting on track** — call them the **facilitator**: not a boss, just the person who passes the token, watches the time, and keeps the question in view. Rotate this job every meeting.
- **A way to write the decision down** — a book, a board, a sheet kept in one known place. Spoken decisions melt; written ones hold.

## Steps

1. **Announce the council early and openly.** Say when, where, and the exact question: "Where should the new well go?" Give people at least a day to think.
2. **Open by stating the question and the rules.** One minute: "Tonight we decide X. The token speaks. We go around; everyone gets a turn; then we discuss; then we decide; then we write it down."
3. **Do a first full round before any debate.** Pass the token around the circle. Each person says their view in a minute or two — or says "pass." No responding yet, only listening. This round is the heart of the council: the quiet people speak *before* the loud ones set the frame.
4. **Then open discussion, still through the token.** Now people may answer each other. The facilitator keeps it on the question, keeps any one voice from taking every turn, and asks: "Who hasn't spoken who wants to?"
5. **Gather the choices and test for agreement.** The facilitator restates the two or three real options that emerged. First seek **consensus** — a choice everyone can live with, even if it's no one's favorite. Ask exactly: "Can everyone *live with* option A?"
6. **If consensus won't come, vote by the fallback rule you agreed in calm times:** simple majority (more than half), or two-thirds for big irreversible things. Count hands in the open. The losing side must be heard once more first: "What would make this bearable for you?"
7. **Write the decision down before anyone leaves.** What was decided, who does what, by when, and when the group will check on it. Read it aloud once so all hear the same words.
8. **Set the review date in the same breath.** "We try this until the next full moon, then look again." Decisions with a review date are easier to accept and easier to fix when wrong.

## Watch out

- **The loudest voice becomes the decision.** The silent first round, the token, and "who hasn't spoken?" exist to prevent it. If one person still dominates, the facilitator must say so plainly: "We have your view — I want to hear three others."
- **Deciding without the people it affects.** They will quietly refuse to follow it. Wait a day for the missing person rather than decide around them.
- **Endless talk, no decision.** Set a time limit at the start — "we decide by the time this candle burns down" — and let the fallback vote catch what consensus can't. A decent decision made beats a perfect one forever pending.
- **Nothing written down.** In a month, five people will remember five different decisions, and you'll fight the same fight again.
- **Punishing honest dissent.** If people who disagreed get frozen out afterward, they'll stop speaking honestly, and the council goes blind. Praise the good objection out loud, especially when it loses.
- **Re-opening settled matters weekly.** Once decided, it stands until the review date unless something truly new appears. Otherwise the stubborn outlast the tired.

## How it works

A council trades a little speed for a lot of durability. A chief's order is fast but brittle. A decision people helped make, they enforce themselves, because their own voice is inside it. The circle, the token, and the written record are all doing one job: making sure every member can honestly say "I was heard, and I know exactly what we agreed."
