Everything your village knows — fire, clean water, planting, mending, the rules that keep the peace — lives only in heads and hands. Heads and hands wear out. If the young do not learn it all, the village ends when the last knower dies, and the children start again from zero. Teaching is how a village survives its own people.

The good news: children are learning machines. They copy everything they see, ask endless questions, and want real work long before adults trust them with it. You do not need a school building or a trained teacher. You need adults willing to slow down and let small hands try.

## You need

- **Every adult, not one teacher.** Whoever holds a skill teaches that skill. If only one person teaches, only one person's knowledge survives.
- **Real work at child size** — a small basket to carry, a short row to weed, a blunt knife on soft wood. Pretend tasks teach pretending; real tasks teach responsibility.
- **Patience for slowness and mess.** A child doing a job takes three times as long and does it half as well — at first. That is the price of a village that outlives you.
- **Stories, songs, and rhymes** — knowledge with a beat sticks for life. "Leaves of three, let it be" outlives any lecture about poison plants.
- **A rough list of what must be passed on** — fire, water, food, shelter, healing, counting, the rules. Check it each year: which children can now do which things? Gaps in the list are holes in the village's future.

## Steps

1. **Keep children beside the work, from the start.** Babies watch from a sling; toddlers hand you things; five-year-olds fetch and carry. Most learning is soaked up, not taught. A child who has watched bread made two hundred times is already half a baker.
2. **Teach every skill in three passes: show, share, hand over.**
   - **Show:** do the task slowly while saying out loud what you do and why. "I feed the fire small sticks first, because big logs smother a young flame."
   - **Share:** do it together, their hands under yours, then yours under theirs.
   - **Hand over:** they do it alone while you watch and hold your tongue. Only step in for danger.
3. **Match the task to the child, and grow it every year.** Around 4–6: fetching, sorting, feeding animals, weeding. Around 7–10: tending fire, real cooking, watching younger children, simple tools. Around 11–14: full shares of planting, building, and craft beside an adult. By mid-teens they should be able to run a household's basic work alone.
4. **Fence the dangerous skills, don't hide them.** Fire, blades, deep water, large animals: teach these early, formally, and one-to-one — because a child forbidden a thing will try it in secret. The fence is your presence, not their ignorance.
5. **Let them fail small on purpose.** A burnt flatbread, a crooked stitch, a wobbly stool — cheap lessons that save expensive ones. Never rescue a task that is merely going badly; only rescue one going dangerously.
6. **Ask them to teach it back.** Once a child can do a thing, have them show a younger child. Teaching exposes every gap in understanding, and it doubles your teachers for free.
7. **Pack the head-knowledge into stories and songs.** Planting times, star names, which mushrooms kill, how the flood year happened, why the rules exist — tell them at the fire, in rhyme where you can. Repetition is not boring to children; it is how they store things.
8. **Once a year, take stock.** Go through the skill list and mark who can do what. If only one greybeard can find clean water or set a bone, that skill is one illness away from extinction — apprentice someone to it this season.

## Watch out

- **"They'll pick it up somehow."** They pick up what they see. Anything done out of sight of children — or done by only one person — quietly dies with its keeper.
- **Only using children as free labor.** Endless fetching with no new skills teaches resentment, not craft. Every season should add something they couldn't do before.
- **Only teaching hand-work.** The rules, the trades, counting, the reasons behind customs — these are skills too, and the first to vanish.
- **Teaching by shame.** Mockery makes children hide mistakes, and hidden mistakes around fire, water, and blades kill. Correct the work, never the worth of the child.
- **Teaching sons and daughters different halves.** Then every household needs two perfect adults to survive. Teach everyone everything; let taste, not ignorance, divide the work later.
- **Waiting for a school.** The workshop, the field, and the fireside are the school. It is open today.
