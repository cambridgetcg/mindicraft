---
title: Share work and food fairly
summary: Split the jobs and the harvest in ways everyone can see are fair, and take turns at the jobs nobody loves.
difficulty: steady
time: a week
needs: []
---

When people live together, the work never runs out: fetching water, cooking, cleaning, watching children, mending things. If one person quietly does most of it, they burn out and grow bitter. If nobody knows who does what, jobs get skipped and people argue. Most fights in a house or a village are really fights about unfair work or unfair shares.

The fix is not to make everyone equal at everything. It is to make the split *visible* — written where all can see — so nobody has to guess, and nobody can quietly take more or give less.

## You need

- **Everyone who shares the work, gathered at once** — a deal made behind someone's back is not a deal, and they will not honor it.
- **A list of every job** — walk through a whole day and a whole week and name each task out loud. Hidden work (like noticing what needs doing) counts too.
- **Something to write on that stays visible** — a board, a wall, a big sheet. Fairness lives in the open; a plan in one person's head is not a plan.
- **A way to mark turns** — pegs, stones, names in columns, anything you can move or cross off.

## Steps

1. **List every job, big and small.** Cooking, water, firewood, washing, animals, repairs, watching the little ones. Ask twice: "What did I miss?" The missed job is always the one someone resents doing alone.
2. **Sort the jobs into three piles.**
   - Jobs someone *wants* — give these to the people who want them, when possible.
   - Jobs nobody minds — split these by how much time and strength each person has.
   - Jobs nobody loves — cleaning the latrine, the cold early watch. These get **rotated**: everyone takes a turn, in a fixed order, no exceptions for the strong or the loud.
3. **Weigh jobs by effort, not by count.** One hour of heavy digging is not equal to one hour of shelling peas. Ask the group: "Do these two feel like a fair swap?" Adjust until people nod without being pushed.
4. **Write the plan where everyone passes daily.** Names, jobs, and which day the rotation turns over. A plan you cannot point at will be argued about within a week.
5. **Split food and goods in the open.** Divide the harvest or the meal where all can watch. Two old tricks keep it honest:
   - **One cuts, the other chooses**: for two people, one divides the shares, the other picks first. The cutter has every reason to cut evenly.
   - **For groups, deal in rounds**: one portion to each person, around and around, until it is gone — not one big scoop per person guessed by eye.
6. **Give bigger shares openly, for open reasons.** A sick person, a nursing mother, or someone doing the heaviest work may fairly get more — but say it out loud and let the group agree. Secret extras poison everything.
7. **Meet once a week to adjust.** Ten minutes. Ask each person: "Is your load fair? What should change?" Move jobs, rebalance, rotate. Then leave it alone until next week — constant renegotiation is its own kind of unfairness.

## Watch out

- **The quiet one carries too much.** The person who never complains is often the most overloaded. Ask them directly, alone if needed, and rebalance before they break.
- **"I'm just better at it" becomes a trap.** If one person always cooks because they cook best, they cook forever. Rotate anyway sometimes, so skills spread and no one is chained to a job.
- **Counting favors turns poisonous.** Aim for "fair over a week," not "even by the minute." People who tally every small act stop trusting each other. The written plan is the tally — let it do that job.
- **Children and the weak get squeezed out of shares.** Whoever cannot argue loudest still eats. Fix their shares first, before the strong divide the rest.
- **Rules made once and never revisited go stale.** Seasons change, people get sick, babies arrive. If the plan no longer fits, change it at the weekly meeting — do not just quietly stop following it, or the whole plan dies.

## How it works

People accept a smaller share far more easily than a *hidden* share. What breaks groups is not scarcity but suspicion — the fear that someone else is getting more for less. Writing the split down and rotating the hated jobs removes the guesswork, so trust can grow. And trust, once grown, is what lets a group survive the hard seasons together.
