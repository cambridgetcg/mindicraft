title: Make felt from wool
summary: Rub wet soapy wool until it tangles into thick warm fabric for hats, boots, and blankets — no weaving needed.

Felt is the oldest fabric of all. It needs no spinning, no loom, no thread. You take loose wool, wet it with warm soapy water, and rub it until the fibers tangle into one solid sheet. That sheet is thick, warm, and windproof. It does not fray when you cut it.

Only true animal wool works — sheep, goat, camel, rabbit. Plant fibers like flax and cotton will never felt, no matter how long you rub. If you have a sheep's fleece and a lump of soap, you can have warm fabric by tonight.

## You need

- **Clean, loose wool** — washed and teased apart into fluff, with no big clumps of dirt. Coarse or fine both work; fine wool felts faster.
- **Warm water** — as hot as your hands can stand, about as hot as a good bath. A bucketful or more.
- **Soap** — a small handful shaved or dissolved into the warm water, enough to make it feel slippery.
- **A flat surface** — a smooth board, a big flat stone, or a reed mat.
- **A rolling mat or cloth** (helpful, not required) — a reed mat, a strip of bark, or an old cloth to roll the felt inside.
- **Cold water** — a second bucket, for shocking the wool between rubbings. The swap from hot to cold speeds the tangling.

## Steps

1. Pull the wool into thin, even tufts, each so thin you can almost see through it.
2. Lay a layer of tufts on your flat surface, all pointing the same way, edges overlapping like roof shingles.
3. Lay a second layer on top, with the fibers pointing **crosswise** to the first. Add a third layer crosswise again.
   - Three or four thin layers make good felt. One thick layer makes weak felt.
   - Make the pile bigger than you want the finished piece — felt shrinks by about a third.
4. Sprinkle warm soapy water over the whole pile until it is wet through but not swimming.
5. Press down flat with both hands and rub gently in small circles. Keep your touch light for the first while — maybe a few hundred rubs — so the surface knits together without shoving the layers apart.
6. Rub harder as the wool firms up. Add warm soapy water whenever it feels dry. Turn the piece over now and then and rub the other side.
7. Test it: pinch the surface and pull up. If fibers lift away easily, keep rubbing. If the surface holds together, it has become felt.
8. Now shrink and harden it (this is called **fulling**): roll the felt up inside your mat or cloth and roll it back and forth under your forearms, fifty rolls, then unroll, turn the felt a quarter turn, and roll again. Repeat from every direction.
   - Dunk it in hot water, then cold, between rounds. You can also knead it, throw it down on the board, and slap it. Rough treatment is the point.
9. Stop when it is dense, stiff, and shrunk — you should not be able to poke a finger through it.
10. Rinse out all the soap in clean water, squeeze (do not wring hard), and dry it flat in the shade.

To shape a hat or a boot liner, do the same steps, but lay the wool around a flat pattern of bark or stiff leather in the middle, wrapping layers over both sides so there is no seam. Pull the pattern out through a slit once the felt holds together, then keep fulling it over a form — a smooth rock, a wooden last, your own knee.

## Watch out

- **It never firms up.** Either the fiber is not real wool, or the water is too cool, or there is no soap. Check all three. Water that has gone cold does almost nothing.
- **Layers slide apart while rubbing.** You rubbed too hard too soon. Start again with a feather-light touch and be patient.
- **Thin spots and holes.** The layers were uneven. Patch by laying fresh tufts over the thin spot, wetting, and rubbing them in — felt happily grafts to itself.
- **It shrank more than expected.** Normal. Always start about one-third bigger than the finished size you want.
- **Scalded hands.** Use water hot but bearable. You will have your hands in it for hours.

## How it works

Each wool fiber is covered in tiny scales, like a pine cone. Heat, soap, and rubbing make the fibers slide past each other — but the scales only let them slide one way, like a ratchet. Every rub tangles them tighter, and they can never slide back out. That one-way tangle is felt.
