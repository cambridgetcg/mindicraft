title: Weave cloth on a simple loom
summary: Stretch threads tight on a frame and weave over and under until real cloth grows row by row under your hands.

Cloth is just thread crossing thread: one set held tight and still, the other snaking over and under it, row after row. The still threads are called the warp; the moving thread is called the weft. A loom is any frame that holds the warp tight so your hands are free to work the weft. The simplest loom is four sticks lashed into a rectangle — and it makes real cloth: scarves, bags, blankets.

The work is slow and honest — a hand's-width of cloth per hour is good going at first — but watching cloth grow under your own hands is one of the oldest satisfactions there is.

## You need

- **A strong rectangular frame** — four straight sticks or boards lashed or nailed at the corners. About 40 × 60 cm is a friendly first size. It must not flex when the threads pull on it.
- **Plenty of thread.** The warp takes strong, tightly spun, plied thread (two strands twisted together) — test it: a hard yank should not snap it. The weft can be softer, even lumpy beginner thread.
- **A row of evenly spaced notches or small nails** along the frame's top and bottom edges, about 4–5 mm apart. These keep the warp threads parallel and evenly spread.
- **A flat smooth stick**, a bit wider than your cloth, like a long ruler with a tapered edge. This is your sword stick — it opens gaps and presses rows down.
- **A slim stick or large blunt needle** to carry the weft thread through. Wind the weft onto it like a little shuttle.
- **A comb or fork** for beating each new row snug.

## Steps

1. Warp the frame: tie your strong thread to a bottom corner, run it up over the top edge's first notch, down to the bottom's first notch, and back up — zigzagging until the frame is filled — then tie off.
   - Every thread should be tight as a guitar string, at the same tension — press them; they should all give equally.
2. Weave a spacer first: two or three rows of thick scrap yarn or thin sticks at the very bottom, to spread the warp evenly and give a straight starting edge.
3. Weave the first true row: with the weft on your shuttle stick, go over the first warp thread, under the second, over the third — repeat to the far side.
   - The sword stick makes this fast: weave the flat stick itself over-under across the warp, then turn it on edge. It holds a tunnel open (called the shed), and the shuttle slides through in one push.
4. Beat the row down: lay the weft in a shallow arc, like a smile — the slack feeds the over-under path — then press it firmly against the previous row with your comb. Every row, same arc, same strength.
5. Weave the second row in the opposite pattern — under where you went over, over where you went under. Alternate forever after; that alternation locks thread into cloth.
6. When you reach the top (a hand's width from the frame, for room to work), stop. Weave in the weft's tail, cut the warp threads off the frame, and knot them in pairs so nothing unravels. The knotted ends make a fringe.
7. Wash the finished cloth gently in warm water and dry it flat — washing settles the threads and makes the cloth softer and denser.

## Watch out

- **The waist.** Pulling the weft tight at each edge makes the cloth narrower and narrower, until it looks like an hourglass. Always leave the arc of slack, and check your width every few rows against a marked stick.
- **Uneven beating** makes stripes of dense and loose cloth. Same comb pressure every row, and beat straight, not at an angle.
- **Weak warp thread snapping mid-cloth.** Don't warp with soft, thin, or under-twisted thread. If one snaps, tie a new length to a stick above the cloth, run it down through the correct notch, and continue; weave the loose ends in later.
- **Skipped threads** — going over two warps where you meant one — leave floats, loose loops on the surface. Check each row before beating; unweaving one row is easy, ten is misery.
- **A flexing frame** slowly loses tension and the cloth goes baggy. Brace the corners with diagonal sticks if the frame gives at all.

## How it works

Nothing is glued — the cloth holds because every thread bends over and under its crossing neighbors, and each bend presses on the next. Friction at thousands of crossings adds up to a strong, flexible fabric. Tight and many crossings make dense, windproof cloth; loose and few make open, airy cloth.
