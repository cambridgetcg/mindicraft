A loom gives you flat cloth. A needle turns that cloth into clothes that stay on in the wind and hold warmth where you need it. The needle itself is simple: a sliver of bone with a point at one end and a hole at the other.

Avoid clever shapes: rectangles sewn together make a perfectly good tunic. Every cut edge you avoid is an edge that cannot fray.

## You need

- **A straight piece of bone** — a leg bone splinter from a bird or small animal, about as long as your finger and thinner than a twig. Bird bone is best: hard, light, already thin.
- **A rough stone and a fine stone** — for grinding the needle to shape. Sandstone works; finish on something smoother.
- **A sharp flint point or awl** — to bore the eye (the thread hole).
- **Woven cloth** — a piece at least as wide as your shoulders and twice as long as from your shoulder to your knee, for the tunic.
- **Strong thread** — your spun linen or wool. Wax it by dragging it across beeswax or hard fat, so it slides and does not tangle.
- **A sharp knife or flint blade** — for cutting cloth in straight lines.
- **Charcoal** — to mark cutting lines on the cloth.

## Steps

1. Make the needle:
   - Snap or saw a splinter of bone slightly longer than your final needle.
   - Grind it on the rough stone into a smooth rod, thinner than a grass stem, with one end tapering to a point.
   - Bore the eye near the blunt end: twist the flint point gently against the bone, first from one side, then the other, until the two pits meet in a hole. Go slowly — pressing hard splits the bone.
   - Polish the whole needle on the fine stone until nothing snags a strand of wool drawn along it.
2. Learn three stitches on scrap cloth first:
   - **Running stitch** — in, out, in, out, in a straight dashed line, each stitch about the width of a grain of barley. Fast; good for long seams.
   - **Backstitch** — one stitch forward, then bring the needle back to the end of the last stitch. Slower but far stronger; makes a solid unbroken line. Use it wherever a seam takes strain: shoulders, armpits.
   - **Hem stitch** — fold a cut edge over twice and sew the fold down with small slanted stitches. This stops fraying.
3. Sew the tunic:
   - Fold your long cloth in half. The fold sits on your shoulders.
   - Cut a slit along the fold for your head — a hand-span wide, no wider, or it slips off the shoulders. Try it on before cutting more.
   - Sew up each side from the bottom hem toward the fold, but stop a forearm's length below the fold. Those two openings are the armholes.
   - Hem the head slit, the armholes, and the bottom edge.
   - For sleeves later, sew two cloth tubes into the armholes.
4. Make the wrap: a plain rectangle, hemmed on all four edges, that winds around the waist or chest and tucks or ties. No other sewing needed. This is a skirt, a scarf, or a baby carrier.
5. Make the cloak: the biggest rectangle you can weave, hemmed all round. Sew a short thread loop on one corner and a bone toggle (a small polished bone bar) on another, so it closes at the throat. It doubles as your blanket.
6. Finish every seam: knot the thread, then run the tail back through the last few stitches before trimming, so it cannot pull free.

## Watch out

- **The eye splits while boring.** You pressed too hard or bored too close to the end. Leave at least two thread-widths of bone around the eye, and twist with patience, not force.
- **Cut edges fray into fringe.** Hem every cut edge the same day you cut it.
- **Seams tear at the armpit and shoulder.** Those points take all the strain. Backstitch them, and sew each one twice.
- **The head slit gapes and the tunic slides off.** You cut too wide. Sew the slit partly closed from each end, or add a tie.
- **Thread snarls into knots.** Use shorter lengths — arm's length at most — and wax them.
- **Clothes come out too tight.** Cloth does not stretch like skin. Cut everything a hand-span roomier than the body, everywhere. Loose clothes are also warmer: they trap air.

## How it works

A stitch is just thread trapping cloth against cloth, over and over. Strength comes from many small stitches sharing the load — ten small stitches hold better than three big ones, because each one carries less strain.
