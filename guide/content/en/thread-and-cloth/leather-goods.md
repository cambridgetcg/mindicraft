Cloth keeps you warm; leather keeps you moving. Stones, thorns, wet ground, and heavy loads destroy cloth in weeks, but a well-sewn pair of leather shoes lasts years, and a leather bag carries what a cloth sack cannot.

Leather sewing is different from cloth sewing in one big way: you cannot push a needle through leather. You punch every hole first with an awl (a sharp spike), then pass the thread through. The rhythm is always the same: punch, then stitch.

## You need

- **Tanned leather** — pieces that bend without cracking. Thick (about two coins thick) for soles and belts; thinner and softer for bags and shoe tops.
- **An awl** — a sharp bone or metal spike with a handle, for punching stitch holes.
- **Two needles** — bone or metal, sturdier than cloth needles; the strongest leather stitch uses both at once.
- **Strong thread** — thick spun linen or sinew, dragged through beeswax until stiff and sticky — wax stops rot and fraying.
- **A sharp knife** — leather blunts blades fast; sharpen often. Cut on a wood board.
- **Charcoal and a straight stick** — for marking patterns and lines.
- **Scrap bark or stiff cloth** — to make patterns before you cut precious leather.
- **A leather lace** — a long thin strip cut from the hide, for the shoe's drawstring.

## Steps

1. Learn the **saddle stitch** on scrap first — it is the stitch for everything here:
   - Mark a line and punch holes along it with the awl, about half a finger-width apart, all the same distance from the edge.
   - Thread a needle onto **each end** of one long waxed thread.
   - Pass one needle through the first hole and pull the thread to its middle. At every hole after, pass both needles through from opposite sides and pull tight.
   - If one thread wears through, the other still holds.
2. Make a belt (the easy win, do it first):
   - Cut a straight strip of thick leather, two fingers wide, long enough to circle your waist plus two hand-spans.
   - Punch holes at one end and fasten with a bone toggle or a simple loop-and-knot. Round the tip.
3. Make a bag:
   - Cut one long rectangle of soft leather. Fold the bottom third up to form the pouch; the top third becomes the flap.
   - Punch matching holes up both sides of the pouch and saddle-stitch them.
   - For a drawstring bag instead: cut a circle, punch holes around the rim, thread a lace through, and pull.
4. Make one-piece shoes (one piece of leather gathered around each foot):
   - Stand on bark or stiff cloth and trace your foot. Draw a second line three finger-widths outside the first and cut that bigger shape as your pattern. Test-fold it around your foot before touching leather.
   - Cut one from thick, flexible leather for each foot (flip the pattern — feet mirror each other).
   - Punch lace holes all around the edge, a thumb-width in from the rim and two finger-widths apart.
   - Thread the lace through the holes, put your foot in the middle, and pull — the leather gathers around your foot like a purse.
   - Arrange the gathers so the folds sit on top of the foot, not under the sole, then tie the lace around the ankle.
   - Sew the heel gathers closed with a few saddle stitches so the back keeps its shape.
5. Wet-shape anything that should hold a form: soak the finished piece in cool water, put it on the foot (or stuff the bag with dry grass), and let it dry slowly in the shade — it keeps that shape.
6. Feed finished goods with a thin rub of hard fat or tallow. It keeps leather supple and sheds rain.

## Watch out

- **Awl slips into your hand.** The awl causes more wounds than the knife. Always punch **away** from your body, onto a wood block, never toward the palm holding the leather.
- **Cutting without a pattern.** Leather is months of work; one bad cut wastes it. Test a bark or cloth pattern first, every time.
- **Stitches tear out along the edge.** Holes were too close to the edge or too close together. Keep holes at least half a finger-width from any edge.
- **Drying leather by the fire.** Heat turns wet leather rock-hard and cracked, for good. Shade and moving air only.
- **Soles wear through at the ball of the foot.** Normal — that is the sacrifice spot. Saddle-stitch a patch of thick leather over it before a hole appears.
- **Mold on stored leather.** Store dry, fed with fat, away from damp ground. Wipe mold off early with a vinegar-damp cloth and re-dry.
