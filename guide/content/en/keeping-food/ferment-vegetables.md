You cannot dry a cabbage. Juicy vegetables laugh at sun and wind — but there is another way, and it is close to magic. Pack them with salt under their own juice, wait two weeks, and an army of friendly invisible helpers turns the juice pleasantly sour. That sourness is a wall no rot can climb. Sour vegetables keep all winter in a cool place, stay crisp, and are actually better for your belly than fresh.

This is how whole peoples survived winters: sauerkraut, sour pickles, kimchi — all the same trick. Salt plus juice plus time plus keeping everything under the liquid.

## You need

- **Firm fresh vegetables** — cabbage is the classic and most forgiving. Also good: roots like carrots and turnips, cucumbers, wild garlic stems — but only wild garlic you are completely sure of: crush a leaf, and if it does not smell strongly of garlic, leave it, because its deadly lookalikes (lily-of-the-valley and autumn crocus) have no garlic smell. Fresh and firm; a half-rotten vegetable brings the wrong invisible guests.
- **Salt** — clean and dry, from your salt-making. The measure that matters: about one part salt to fifty parts vegetable by weight. In hand terms, a small handful of salt for a big armful (a large potful) of shredded vegetable. Better slightly too salty than not salty enough.
- **A clean clay pot with a wide mouth** — washed well and rinsed with boiled water. Wide mouth, so you can press and reach inside.
- **A weight** — a smooth washed stone that fits inside the pot, plus a flat piece of wood or a large clean leaf under it. Its job: hold every scrap of vegetable under the liquid at all times.
- **A cool shaded place** — comfortable-room cool, not freezing, not hot sun. A shaded corner of the hut, later a cold store or cellar.

## Steps

1. Wash the vegetables and your hands, pot, stone, and knife. Clean start, clean ferment.
2. Shred or slice everything small — thin ribbons of cabbage, thin sticks of roots. Small pieces give up their juice easily.
3. Put a layer of shredded vegetable in the pot, sprinkle a pinch of salt over it, and squeeze and pound it hard with your fists or a wooden pounder until it goes wet and limp.
4. Repeat, layer by layer — vegetable, pinch of salt, pound — until the pot is two-thirds full. Never fill it more; the ferment rises and bubbles over.
5. Press everything down hard. Juice should rise up and cover the vegetables completely. If after an hour it still does not cover them, top up with brine: boiled water, cooled, with salt stirred in until it tastes clearly salty, like seawater.
6. Lay the flat wood or big leaf on top, set the clean stone on that, and check that every last shred is drowned under the liquid. Anything poking into the air will mould.
7. Cover the pot mouth loosely with cloth or a lid resting ajar — bubbles must be able to escape. Do not seal it tight.
8. Put the pot in the cool shaded spot and wait. Within two or three days it should start to bubble quietly and smell sour and fresh, like something between vinegar and green apples. That smell is success.
9. Check every day or two. Push everything back under the liquid. Skim off any white film on the surface (see below).
10. Taste after one week, again after two. Done is when you like it: pleasantly sour, still crisp. Then move the pot somewhere colder — cold nearly stops the change — and eat from it all winter, always pushing the rest back under the brine with clean hands.

## Watch out

- **The one rule that keeps you safe: under the brine, always.** Everything below the liquid is protected by salt and sourness. Anything above it is just wet food rotting in air. Check the weight every visit.
- **A thin white film on the surface** is a harmless yeast that often visits. Skim it off, wipe the pot rim, push everything back under. It is a nuisance, not a danger.
- **Fuzzy mould — raised spots of blue, green, black, or pink — is different.** That batch has gone wrong. Throw it all away. Do not scoop out the mould and eat the rest.
- **The smell is your judge.** Right smells sour, sharp, fresh. Wrong smells rotten, like bad eggs, or makes you step back. Slimy, mushy vegetables in stinking brine: throw the whole pot's contents out, wash everything, start again. When in doubt, throw it out.
- **Too little salt** lets rot outrun the sourness in the first days. If you had to guess, guess salty.
- **Too warm a spot** turns vegetables soft and the taste harsh. Cool and shaded, always.

## How it works

On every vegetable live harmless invisible creatures that eat plant sugar and give off sourness. Salt at one part in fifty knocks out the rot-makers but not these salt-hardy friends. They eat, the juice grows sour, and soon the brine is a place where nothing harmful can live — a pickle, keeping itself safe in its own sour moat.
