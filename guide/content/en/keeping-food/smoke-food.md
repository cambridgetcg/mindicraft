Sun-drying works, but it needs sun, and flies love it, and fatty fish spoil anyway. Smoke fixes all three. Smoke is dry warm air full of substances that rot-creatures hate, so smoked food dries faster, keeps longer — weeks instead of days — and picks up a deep, wonderful taste as a free gift.

The trick to understand from the start: you are not cooking the food. You are drying it slowly in a cloud of cool smoke. Hot flame under the food makes dinner; cool smoke around the food makes stores.

And that is exactly why this craft has one step you may never skip. Food that never gets hot never has its germs killed by heat — so something else has to hold them back through the long slow days of drying, and that something is salt. Smoke flavours, and helps. Salt and dryness are what actually keep you safe. **No salt, no cold smoking.** If you have no salt yet, smoke it hot instead — there is a short section for that below — and eat it within a few days.

## You need

- **Fresh fish or lean meat** — the fresher the better. Smoking cannot rescue food that has already started to turn.
- **A fire you can keep low all day** — small coals, not flames. This is why keeping a fire alive comes first.
- **The right wood: hardwood** — oak, beech, alder, birch, apple, or other trees with broad leaves. Green (fresh-cut) or soaked hardwood smokes best.
- **The wrong wood, so you can avoid it** — pine, spruce, fir, and other needle-trees. Their sticky sap makes black, bitter, sooty smoke that ruins food. No needle-trees.
- **A frame to hang food in the smoke** — three long poles tied at the top like a tent (a tripod), with cross-sticks to hang food from, set over or beside the fire. Skins, bark, or cloth wrapped around it hold the smoke in.
- **Salt — not optional.** Enough to rub hard into every surface of every piece. Salt is what holds the germs back through two days of slow drying; cool smoke alone will not. This is why the make-salt guide comes before this one. No salt yet? Use the hot-smoke method below instead.

## Steps

1. Clean the food completely. Gut fish, take off heads and gills, split them open flat like a book. Cut meat into strips or flat slabs no thicker than two fingers. Thin pieces dry all the way through; thick ones stay wet in the middle, and the middle is where the danger lives.
2. **Salt it, and mean it.** Rub salt hard into every surface — into every crevice, both sides of a split fish, the whole face of every strip. Lay the pieces in a pot or on a board, cover them, and leave them a few hours; overnight is better. Water will pull out and pool underneath: that is the salt doing its work. Wipe the pieces dry before they go on the frame. Skimping this step is what turns smoked fish from a store into a danger.
3. Build the smoking frame. Hang the food pieces high — at least an arm's length above where the fire will be. Pieces must not touch each other.
4. Light a small fire and let it burn down to coals. Then feed it green or wet hardwood, a little at a time.
   - You want thick white smoke and almost no flame.
   - Test the heat where the food hangs with your hand. It should feel barely warm — like a hand held in weak sunshine, not like a hand in bathwater. If it is properly warm to your touch, the fire is too big or the food too close: food held at blood heat for two days is food kept exactly where germs like it best. If you must pull your hand away, it is far too hot — food that cooks now will rot later. Cooler and slower is always the safer mistake here.
5. Wrap the frame's sides to trap the smoke, leaving a gap at the top so it draws through.
6. Keep the smoke going all day. Add a stick when smoke thins. Knock down any flames with a splash of water or by smothering with more green wood.
7. Let the fire rest overnight if you must, and smoke again the whole second day.
8. Test, and be hard about it: the food is done when it is firm, browned, and — the part that matters — **dry all the way to the centre**, so a piece bends stiffly or cracks rather than folding softly. Tear the thickest piece open and look. It must be dense and dry right through, not wet, soft, or flaky-raw in the middle. A piece still moist inside is not a store, whatever the outside looks like: eat it today, cooked right through, or hang it back and smoke it longer.
9. Cool the food fully, then store it hung up or in a dry container, in a cool, dry, airy, shaded place. Only pieces that were salted and came out hard-dry go into store. Check every few days. If a piece grows soft or damp, smoke it again the same day.

## No salt? Smoke it hot

Cold smoke without salt is not a store — it is a gamble with your life. But fire and smoke can still give you a fine meal that keeps a few days.

1. Build the same frame, but hang the food closer to a livelier fire. This time you want the food properly hot, not merely smoky.
2. **Cook it through.** Fish flakes apart and is opaque white right to the bone; meat is firm and no longer red at the centre. This is real cooking, and cooking is the thing that kills germs outright.
3. Keep green hardwood on the coals while it cooks, for the taste and a drier surface.
4. Eat it within two or three days, kept cool and covered. Hot-smoked food is a good dinner and a short store — never a winter.

When you have salt, come back and do it cold and properly.

## Watch out

- **Unsalted cold-smoked fish can kill you.** This is the one warning here that is not about ruining food. Cool smoke never gets hot enough to kill anything, so unsalted fish held warm for two days can grow a germ that makes one of the deadliest poisons known — the sickness is called botulism. It gives no smell, no taste, no slime, no colour, no warning of any kind — it does not announce itself the way honest rot does, and smoked food is eaten as it is, cold, so nothing stands between the poison and you. Nothing in your senses can catch this one. Only the salt and the hard dry finish stand between you and it. Salt every piece, every time — or smoke it hot and eat it fresh.
- **Too hot is the classic failure.** Flames or strong heat cook the outside into a crust while the inside stays wet — it looks done and rots from within. Keep the fire tiny and the food high.
- **Needle-tree wood makes food bitter and sooty.** So do painted wood, driftwood crusted with unknown things, and anything mouldy. Clean hardwood only.
- **Smoking is not full protection.** Smoked food keeps for weeks in cool dry air, not forever. Fat still goes rancid in warmth. Eat the fattiest pieces first.
- **Trust your nose and eyes every time — and know exactly where they fail.** Sour smell, slime, or fuzzy mould means throw that piece far from camp; that is honest spoilage announcing itself, and your senses are good at it. The poison in the first warning does none of these things. That is why the salt and the dryness are not up for negotiation: they are the part of your defence that does not depend on noticing anything. When in doubt, throw it out — one bad meal can weaken you for a week, and the bad meal you cannot smell can do far worse.
- **Fire safety.** A smoker is a fire left burning near dry wrappings for two days. Clear the ground around it, keep water or dirt at hand, and never leave it completely unwatched.

## How it works

Three things keep smoked food, and it pays to know which does what, because the whole shape of this guide follows from it.

**Salt** goes first, and does the heaviest work. A germ is mostly water in a bag. Salt on the outside of that bag pulls water out through the bag's own skin — so salt never has to hunt germs down; it simply takes away the water they need to live and multiply. That protection begins the moment you rub it on, and it holds through the slow early days while the food is still damp and defenceless.

**Drying** finishes what salt starts. The gentle warmth and moving air pull the water out of the food itself, just like sun-drying but faster and under your control. Nothing living grows in food that is dry to the centre — which is why bend-and-crack is the true test of done, and why a wet middle means not done, no matter how good the outside looks.

**Smoke** is the third and the smallest. It carries substances from the burning wood that settle on the surface and poison mould and rot-creatures without harming you, it keeps the flies off, and it tastes wonderful. But it coats. It does not reach the middle.

Now the danger explains itself. Cold smoke brings no heat to kill with, so unsalted fish is guarded by nothing at all through the two days it takes to dry — coated on the outside, wet and mild in the middle. That wet middle, with air shut out by the coated surface, is exactly the home the worst germ of all is looking for: it wants no air, no salt, and no heat, and it pays you back with a poison you cannot smell or taste. Salt takes away its water. Drying takes away its home. Heat, in the hot-smoke method, kills it outright. Remove all three and the handsomest fish on the frame is the one that hurts you. Salted through, dry inside, coated outside — that is why smoked fish hangs safely in a winter hut for weeks.
