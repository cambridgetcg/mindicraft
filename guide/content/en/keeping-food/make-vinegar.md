Vinegar is sour water with a quiet power: nothing that makes food rot can grow in it. A splash keeps vegetables for months, cleans pots and surfaces, softens tough meat, and brightens a dull meal. (For a wound, use clean water, not vinegar — acid stings and harms the flesh.) And you can grow it yourself from almost any sweet fruit, using nothing but time, air, and patience.

The trick is that vinegar is made in two steps by two invisible helpers. First, tiny living specks called yeast eat the sugar in fruit juice and turn it into alcohol — the same way wine or cider is made. Then a second kind of tiny life, living in the open air, eats that alcohol and turns it into acid. Acid is what makes vinegar sour and safe. You do not add either helper by hand; they arrive on their own if you set the table for them.

## You need

- **Sweet fruit or fruit juice** — apples, grapes, pears, or berries. The sweeter the fruit, the stronger the vinegar. Bruised windfall apples are perfect.
- **A wide clay pot or jar** — wide-mouthed, so lots of air touches the surface. This is why a bowl beats a bottle.
- **Clean water** — only if you are crushing whole fruit and it needs loosening.
- **A cloth and string** — a breathable cover, like woven linen or thin cotton. Air must pass through; flies must not.
- **A dark, warm-ish corner** — a steady spot around 20–29°C (68–85°F). A cool room slows it; a hot one can spoil it.
- **Time** — three to six weeks, almost all of it just waiting.

## Steps

1. **Make sweet juice.** Crush your fruit and strain out the pulp, or use juice you already have. If it is very thick, add a little clean water so it pours easily.
2. **Pour it into the wide pot,** filling it no more than two-thirds. The empty space above the juice holds the air that the second helper needs.
3. **Cover with cloth and tie it on.** This is the most important step. The cloth lets air in but keeps out flies, which lay eggs and carry rot. Never seal it tight with a lid.
4. **Wait for the bubbling — the first stage.** Within a day or two it froths and smells like cider or wine. That is the yeast turning sugar into alcohol. Stir it once a day with a clean spoon for the first week to keep air moving.
5. **Wait for the souring — the second stage.** After a week or two the bubbling fades and the smell turns sharp and sour. The air-helper is now at work. A pale, wrinkled, jelly-like skin may form on top. This is the **mother** — a mat of the good acid-making life. Leave it be; it is a sign of success.
6. **Taste it, carefully, after three weeks.** Dip a clean spoon. When it tastes cleanly, mouth-puckeringly sour with no sweetness and no strong wine smell left, it is ready. This usually takes three to six weeks total.
7. **Strain and store.** Pour the finished vinegar through cloth into clean jars and seal them. It keeps for a year or more in a cool dark place.
8. **Keep the mother.** Save that jelly skin and a cup of the finished vinegar to start your next batch faster — days sooner than the first.

## Watch out

- **Fuzzy or coloured mould means stop.** The good mother is smooth and pale. If you see fuzzy green, black, or pink growth, or it smells rotten rather than sharp, throw the whole batch out and start clean. Do not eat it.
- **A sealed lid can burst.** The first stage makes gas. Always use cloth, never a tight lid, until fermenting is fully done.
- **Too cold and nothing happens; too hot and it spoils.** Keep it in that warm-ish room. If a batch just sits still and sweet for a week, it is too cold — move it somewhere warmer.
- **Weak sour is weak keeping power.** Watery, barely-sour vinegar will not preserve food safely. For pickling that must last, use a vinegar that tastes strongly, unmistakably sour.
- **Metal pots corrode.** Vinegar's acid eats iron and copper over time. Ferment and store in clay, glass, or wood.

## How it works

Sugar is food that many living things fight over. By inviting the right two — yeast, then the air-loving acid-makers — in the right order, you march the juice past every kind of life that would make it rot, and land it in a state where almost nothing can live at all. That is why a sour so mild it merely tickles the tongue can guard a jar of vegetables through a whole winter.
