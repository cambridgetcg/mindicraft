Go down a little way into the earth and the ground stays cool and steady all year — cold in summer, and in winter warmer than the frozen air above. A hole dug into that steadiness becomes a natural cold store, a **root cellar**. Without any ice or machine, it turns a pile of autumn roots into food that lasts through the whole winter. A cabbage that would wilt in a week on your shelf will keep for months down there.

Cold slows down rot. Everything that spoils food — the tiny life that rots it, and the plant's own drive to sprout — moves slower when it is cool. The earth gives you that cool for free.

## You need

- **A hillside, bank, or dry patch of ground** — a slope you can dig into sideways is easiest and drains well. Flat ground works too; you just dig down.
- **A digging stick and wooden tools** — a strong digging stick, a wooden spade or paddle, and a basket to haul soil.
- **Stone, wood, or bark** — to line the walls and roof so they do not cave in.
- **Straw, dry leaves, or dry grass** — a thick pile, for bedding the food and blocking frost.
- **A door or heavy cover** — planks, bark, or a flat stone to close the entrance against cold and animals.
- **Dry, high ground away from water** — the one thing that ruins a cellar is water seeping in.

## Steps

1. **Pick a dry, high spot.** Test by digging a small hole and leaving it overnight. If water pools in the bottom, choose somewhere higher.
2. **Dig into a slope, or dig down.** Aim to get the storage space at least 1 metre (about 3–4 feet) below the surface — deep enough that the earth around it stays cool and never freezes. A hole the size of a small room is plenty for one family.
3. **Line the walls and roof.** Stack stone or lay wood and bark against the walls and over the top so soil does not crumble in. Leave the floor as bare earth — its slight damp keeps roots from drying out.
4. **Make a small door.** A snug entrance you can close matters more than size. A tight cover holds the coolness in and keeps out mice and frost.
5. **Let a little air move.** Leave a small gap or a hollow stick as a vent near the top. A trickle of fresh air stops damp, stale air from building up — just a trickle, not a draught.
6. **Bed the food in dry straw.** Lay roots and fruit between thick layers of dry straw or leaves, not touching each other. The straw cushions them, holds the damp steady, and blocks frost.
7. **Keep different foods apart.** Store apples and pears away from potatoes, onions, and garlic — ripe fruit gives off a breath that makes roots sprout early. Onions and garlic like it drier; hang them near the door.
8. **Check every week or two.** Pull out anything soft, spotted, or smelling off before the rot spreads to its neighbours.

## Watch out

- **One rotten one spoils the rest.** A single rotting apple or root gives off a breath that ripens and rots everything around it. Remove bad ones the moment you find them — "one bad apple" is a real warning, not just a saying.
- **Water is the enemy.** A damp or flooding cellar rots food fast. Dig on high, dry ground, and never near a stream or where rain runs.
- **A cave-in can bury you.** While digging, line and support the roof and walls as you go, especially in loose or sandy soil. Never leave a wide roof unsupported; in loose ground, keep the span narrow.
- **Frost still reaches shallow stores.** In hard-freezing lands, dig deeper and pile more straw and soil over the top. Food that freezes and thaws turns to mush.
- **Never sleep in a closed pit.** Stale air low in a sealed hole can be short of the air you need to breathe. Prop the door open while you work inside, and keep the vent clear.
- **Wet or bruised food will not keep.** Only store roots and fruit that are dry, whole, and unmarked. Brush off soil; do not wash them — a wet skin invites rot.

## How it works

A few feet down, the earth barely notices the seasons. Summer's heat and winter's cold take months to soak that deep, so the ground holds a mild, steady coolness close to the year's average — often around 4–10°C (40–50°F). That unchanging cool slows rot and sprouting without freezing the food. You are not making cold; you are borrowing the calm the deep earth already keeps.
