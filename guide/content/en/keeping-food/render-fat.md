Raw fat from an animal rots within days. But melt it down gently, strain out the bits, and let it cool, and it becomes a smooth, pale, solid grease that keeps for many months without a cold store. This is one of the great quiet skills. Rendered fat is the richest cooking fat you will find, a fuel that burns bright in a lamp, and the main ingredient of soap. Nothing from a clean animal should be wasted, and its fat is treasure.

The word for melting fat down is **rendering**. All it means is: warm the fat slowly until the pure grease flows out of the tissue it was trapped in, then throw away the tissue and keep the grease.

## You need

- **Animal fat** — trimmed from meat you have already cleaned. Fat from around the kidneys and organs is the firmest and keeps best; back and belly fat work too.
- **A knife or grinding stone** — to cut the fat into small pieces. Smaller pieces melt faster and more fully.
- **A clay or metal pot** — heavy-bottomed if you have it, so the fat heats evenly and does not scorch.
- **A little water** — a splash in the pot at the start stops the fat catching before it starts to melt.
- **A cloth strainer** — clean woven cloth to filter out the solid bits at the end.
- **Clean containers with lids** — clay pots, jars, anything that seals. Fewer, fuller containers keep better than many half-empty ones.
- **A low, steady fire** — gentle heat is the whole game here.

## Steps

1. **Cut the fat small.** Chop it into pieces no bigger than a fingertip, or crush it. Trim off any red meat, blood, or skin — those bits are what make fat spoil, so the cleaner the fat, the longer it keeps.
2. **Start it gently with a splash of water.** Put the fat in the pot with a little water and set it over low heat. The water protects the fat until enough grease has melted to cover the bottom; it will boil away on its own.
3. **Melt low and slow.** Keep the heat low. You want the fat to melt, not fry. Stir now and then so nothing sticks. Over an hour or two the solid fat turns into a pool of clear liquid grease.
4. **Watch the bits sink and brown.** The leftover tissue, called **cracklings**, shrinks into small crisp pieces. When they turn pale gold and settle to the bottom, the melting is done. Do not let them go dark brown — burnt bits taint the whole pot.
5. **Take it off the heat and let it settle** for a few minutes so the last solids sink.
6. **Strain it clean.** Pour the hot liquid grease through cloth into your containers, leaving the cracklings behind. Straining out every bit is what lets the fat keep for months. (Eat the cracklings — salted, they are delicious.)
7. **Cool and seal.** Let it cool undisturbed. It turns from clear liquid to a smooth, pale, solid cream. Put lids on and store somewhere cool and dark.

## Watch out

- **Hot fat burns worse than boiling water.** It sticks to skin and does not wash off fast. Keep the fire low, keep children back, and never let water splash into a pot of very hot fat — it can spit violently. Add your starter water only at the beginning, when the pot is still cold.
- **High heat ruins it.** Fat that smokes and darkens has scorched. It tastes bitter and spoils faster. If you see smoke, pull it off the heat at once.
- **Leftover meat and blood spoil the batch.** Any red scraps rot inside the fat and turn the whole pot rancid — that means old, sour, off-smelling. Trim and strain well.
- **Water left in the fat causes rot.** If your finished fat has droplets of liquid trapped in it, gently reheat and let all the water steam off before sealing.
- **Sniff before you use it.** Good fat smells clean and mild. Fat that smells sharp, sour, or like old paint has gone rancid — do not eat it, though it may still serve for soap or lamp fuel.

## How it works

Fat rots because water, blood, and stray meat inside it feed the tiny life that causes rot. Rendering drives off the water and strains out the solids, leaving almost pure grease — a food with nothing left in it for germs to eat. Sealed away from air, which slowly turns fat rancid on its own, it waits patiently for the hungry days of winter.
