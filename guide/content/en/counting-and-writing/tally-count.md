---
title: Count with tallies
summary: Use notches and pebbles so you can count sheep, days, and sacks without holding them all in your head.
difficulty: easy
time: an hour
needs: []
---

Your memory is small and busy. It forgets numbers the moment something else needs attention. A tally — a scratch on a stick, a pebble in a pouch — is a number that lives outside your head. It does not get tired, it does not argue, and it says the same thing tomorrow.

This is the seed of all counting, record-keeping, and mathematics. One mark means one thing. That single idea lets one person keep track of a hundred sheep, forty days, or twelve sacks of grain — and prove it to someone else.

## You need

- **A stick or flat bone** — dry wood as thick as your thumb, so a knife can cut clean notches without splitting it.
- **A knife or sharp stone flake** — anything that scratches a mark you can still see next month.
- **A handful of pebbles and a pouch or pot** — pebbles are movable tallies; the pouch keeps them from wandering off.
- **A safe place** — tally sticks only work if they stay where you left them.

## Steps

1. **Decide exactly what one mark means.** One sheep. One day. One basket of grain. Never mix meanings on the same stick — one stick, one kind of thing.
2. **Make one mark per thing as it passes you.** Cut a notch as each sheep walks through the gate. Do not count in your head at the same time; trust the stick, not your memory.
3. **Group your marks in fives.** Cut four short notches, then a fifth one slanted across them, like a small gate.
   - Groups matter more than marks. Thirty-seven single notches are a blur; seven gates and two spare notches you can read at a glance.
   - Any group size works — five is good because you can check it against one hand.
4. **Count pebbles instead when things move both ways.** Sheep out in the morning: drop one pebble in the pouch per sheep. Sheep home at night: take one pebble out per sheep. Pebbles left over means sheep still missing — go look.
5. **Count days by adding one notch each morning.** Pick the same moment every day — first light works — so you never wonder whether today is marked yet.
6. **Label the stick.** Scratch a simple picture at one end: a sheep shape, a sun, a grain ear. Ten unlabeled sticks are worse than none.
7. **Make a split tally for deals between two people.** Cut all the notches, then split the stick lengthwise down the middle so each half carries the same notches. Each person keeps one half.
   - Neither half can be secretly changed: a new notch on one half won't line up when the halves are put back together. This is a contract before writing exists.

## Watch out

- **Counting in your head "to be quick," then marking later.** That is how counts die. Mark at the moment each thing passes — the whole point is to stop trusting memory.
- **Two people tallying the same flock on different sticks.** The counts will disagree and nobody knows which is right. One count, one stick, one counter — others may watch and check.
- **Marks too shallow or too crowded.** Rain, handling, and time blur them. Cut deep enough to feel with a fingertip, and leave a finger's width between groups.
- **Losing the stick.** A lost tally is a lost number forever. Keep counting sticks in one known place, and for anything important, keep a second stick as a copy.
- **Forgetting whether you already marked today.** Tie the marking to a fixed habit — notch the day-stick before you eat in the morning, every morning, no exceptions.

## How it works

A tally works because of matching, not arithmetic. You never need to know *how many* while you count — you only need one mark for one thing, nothing skipped, nothing doubled. The pile of marks *is* the number. Grouping in fives just makes the pile readable, and the split stick makes it honest, because two matching halves cannot both be lies.
