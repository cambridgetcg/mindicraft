Tallies count things; writing keeps *anything* — names, debts, recipes, laws, a message to a grandchild not yet born. Writing needs three humble tools: something black that stays (ink), something to lay it down with (a pen), and something to lay it on (a page).

Soot ink is worth the trouble: soot is pure carbon, which never fades in sunlight or washes off once dry. Words written this way have lasted thousands of years.

## You need

- **Soot (lampblack)** — the fine black powder from a smoky flame, caught on a cold surface; charcoal ground fine works too, just grittier.
- **A binder** — something sticky to glue soot to the page: hide glue (boiled-down skin scraps), tree gum (the clear ooze on cherry, plum, or acacia bark), or egg white in a pinch.
- **Clean water** — a little, added slowly.
- **Two smooth stones or a mortar** — for grinding. Ink lives or dies by fineness.
- **A reed or a big feather** — a hollow reed thick as a finger, or a big wing feather from a goose or other large bird.
- **A thin sharp knife** — for cutting the pen's nib, the shaped writing tip.
- **Pages** — well-cleaned clay for tablets; birch bark peeled in wide sheets; boards split and scraped smooth.

## Steps

1. **Collect soot.** Hold a cold pot lid or flat stone in the smoke just above a small, smoky flame of resinous wood or oil. Let black powder build, scrape it off, repeat. An hour gives a good spoonful.
2. **Grind the soot.** Grind it with a drop of water into a stiff black paste — a hundred slow circles at least, until it feels like wet silk between two fingers. Grit scratches pages and clogs pens.
3. **Make the binder.** Soak a small lump of tree gum in warm water until it dissolves into a syrup about as thick as honey-water, or warm hide glue until it runs thin.
4. **Mix the ink.** Work binder into the soot paste a little at a time — roughly equal parts — then thin with water drop by drop until it flows like thin cream and a test stroke dries even and black.
   - Smudges under a rubbing finger when dry: add binder.
   - Sits shiny and cracks: add soot and water.
5. **Dry spare ink into cakes.** Shape leftover ink into little blocks and dry in shade. Cakes keep for years; rub one with a wet brush and it is ink again.
6. **Cut a pen.** Take a dry reed or feather (dried a few weeks, or hardened a minute in hot sand until the shaft turns from milky to clear).
   - Slice the tip off at a slant to make a scoop.
   - Shave the sides so the tip narrows to a flat point about as wide as a grain seed.
   - Cut a short slit — a fingernail's length — up the middle of the point. The slit pulls ink down as you write.
   - Trim the very tip square. Dip, touch off the extra on the pot's rim, and write.
7. **Make clay tablets.** Pat clean, stiff clay into a hand-sized slab, a finger thick, and smooth the face. Write in the *soft* clay with a stylus — a stick cut to a blunt wedge tip — pressing marks, not dragging. Dry the tablet slowly in shade for several days; baked hard in a fire, it can outlast almost everything.
8. **Prepare bark and boards.** Peel birch bark in spring when it lifts easily, take the smooth inner layers, and press them flat under boards for a day or two. Scrape boards smooth; a thin wash of white clay makes them pale, and old writing can be scraped off for reuse.

## Watch out

- **Gritty ink.** The most common failure — it scratches pages and blocks the pen's slit. No shortcut: grind longer than feels reasonable.
- **Charcoal-and-water "ink" with no binder.** It writes, then brushes off as dust once dry. The binder is not optional.
- **Liquid ink going moldy.** Binders are food to mold. Mix only what you'll use in a few days; store the rest as dry cakes.
- **Cracked tablets, split pens.** Fast drying cracks clay — dry it slow, in shade. A green reed or fresh feather cuts soft and splits — dry or sand-harden it first.
- **Cutting the nib toward your hand.** The slant cut takes real force and the blade skips. Cut on a firm surface, blade moving away from every finger.
- **Smoke in your lungs.** Collect soot outdoors or beside strong draft, stand out of the plume, and take breaks. Never burn anything indoors without generous airflow.

## How it works

Grinding makes the carbon specks small enough to flow through a nib; the binder dries into a thin skin that locks them to the page. Light cannot bleach carbon, water cannot dissolve it — the words last as long as the page.
