Plant too early and frost kills the seedlings; too late and the grain never ripens. Season-signs give rough warnings; a calendar turns them into a count you can plan by: "plant 30 days after midwinter's third moon." A calendar is two clocks in the sky read together: the moon for short stretches, the sun for the year.

Neither clock needs any tool but your eyes, a fixed place to stand, and the patience to look every day. It takes a season because you are building a record, not a thing.

## You need

- **A tally stick for days** — one notch each morning, grouped in fives. This is the backbone of everything else.
- **A fixed watching spot** — a stone or post you stand at every single time. Move your feet one pace and every horizon mark becomes a lie.
- **A clear view of the horizon** — ideally east or west, with distinct landmarks: a peak, a notch between hills, a lone tree.
- **Markers** — stones or stakes to plant in the ground where the sun rose.
- **Your knowledge of the seasons** — the calendar hangs numbers on signs you already recognize.

## Steps

1. **Start the day-count.** Every morning, cut one notch. Do it before anything else so you never lose a day.
2. **Watch the moon.** It grows from a thin sliver to full in about 15 days, then shrinks to nothing in 15 more. New moon to new moon is 29 to 30 days — call that a "moon" (a month).
   - Start each moon-count at the first thin evening sliver in the west — far easier to spot than the exact dark night.
   - Twelve moons is about 354 days — 11 days short of a full year, so the moon alone drifts. The sun keeps the truth.
3. **Track the sunrise along the horizon.** From your fixed spot, note each clear morning exactly where the sun's edge first appears, and plant a small marker in line with it. Day by day the sunrise point creeps along the horizon — north half the year, south the other.
4. **Catch the turning points.** Twice a year the creeping stops, the sun rises in almost the same spot for several days, then turns back. These are the solstices — the sun's "standstills." The one in the cold season is midwinter, the year's anchor.
   - Take the middle day of the standstill stretch as the turning day.
   - Plant a permanent stone in line with each standstill sunrise. Next year the sun will return to your stone.
5. **Check with the noon shadow.** Set a straight post upright. Each day around midday, mark the tip of its shortest shadow. The day with the year's *longest* noon shadow is midwinter; the *shortest*, midsummer.
6. **Count the year.** Tally the days from one midwinter to the next — about 365. Now every date can be counted from midwinter.
7. **Hang your season-signs on the count.** Each year, tally how many days after midwinter the last frost came, the river rose, the first rains fell. After two or three years the pattern shows: "plant when it feels right" becomes "plant around day 80."
8. **Fix the moon to the sun.** Name the moons ("planting moon," "harvest moon") starting from the first new moon after midwinter. Most years hold 12 moons; about every third year add a 13th to keep the names matching the seasons — add it whenever the moons run visibly early.

## Watch out

- **Missing days in the tally.** One forgotten notch quietly shifts every date. Bind the notch to a daily habit, and let a second person keep a twin tally as a check.
- **Watching from "about the same place."** A step to the side shifts the sunrise against your landmarks by days. Set a stone and always stand touching it.
- **Trusting the moon for the year.** Three years of pure moon-counting puts planting a whole month wrong. The sun's standstills are the anchor; the moon only fills in between.
- **Cloudy mornings at the standstill.** No harm — the sun moves so little then that a few missed days barely matter. Take the middle of what you saw.
- **Staring at the sun.** Never look at the risen sun directly; it damages eyes permanently. Watch only the first instant the edge breaks the horizon, or judge position by the glow and your markers.
- **Calendar pride over field sense.** If the count says plant but the ground is frozen, the ground wins. The calendar predicts; the season-signs decide.

## How it works

The sunrise point swings along the horizon because the world tips toward and away from the sun over the year — the same tipping that makes the seasons. So the standstills and the seasons keep perfect time, forever. The moon's faces show how much of its sunlit side faces you, a steady 29.5-day beat. Sun for the year, moon for the month, tally for the days: three counts, one calendar.
