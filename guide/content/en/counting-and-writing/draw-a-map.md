---
title: Draw a map of your land
summary: Pace out paths, water, and fields and draw them small, so plans can be made on a table.
difficulty: steady
time: two days

---

A map is your land shrunk small enough to argue over at a table. Where to dig the ditch, which fields to swap, how far the spring really is — these plans go wrong when each person carries a different land inside their head. A map puts one land on the table that everyone can point at.

The whole trick is scale: every distance on the ground becomes the same small fraction on the page. Keep that one rule honest and the map stays true.

## You need

- Your legs. A pace is your natural walking step; step 1 turns your paces into real distance.
- A rod or rope of known length from your measuring kit — to check your pace against.
- Ink, pen, and pages for the clean map, plus a slate or scratch board for rough field notes.
- A straight edge and a length of string, for drawing lines and checking right angles.
- A clear morning — the sun will give you your directions.
- A helper, if you can get one. One paces while the other notes; the work halves.

## Steps

1. Measure your pace. Lay out a straight line of known length — say 20 rod-lengths. Walk it naturally three times, counting steps, and take the average. Now every count of paces converts to real distance. Recheck each morning; tired legs take shorter steps.
2. Choose a scale and write it on the page first. For a farm, try: 100 paces on the ground = one small ruled square on the page. Bigger land needs a smaller scale. Everything you draw obeys this one number.
3. Fix north. Around midday, watch a stick's shadow: at its shortest, the shadow points along the north–south line (toward north in northern lands, south in southern). Draw a north arrow on the page and keep the page turned the same way all day.
4. Walk a baseline. Pick two landmarks visible from most of the land — big tree to gate. Pace the distance between them and draw that line on the page, at scale. Every other point will hang off this line.
5. Fix each feature with two numbers. To place the well: walk along the baseline and count paces until the well lies straight off to your side, at a right angle. Note both numbers — paces along, paces out — and plot the point. Check the right angle with a string triangle of sides 3, 4, and 5 equal units; that corner is exactly square.
6. Trace crooked things as chains of straight steps. For a stream or path, walk it and take a paced note at every clear bend. Plot the dots, then smooth a line through them.
7. Use simple symbols and a legend. Wavy line for water, small square for a house, dotted line for a path. The legend is a little box on the page that says what each symbol means.
8. Walk the map. Take the rough draft outside, stand at a known point, and check three or four distances against the page. Fix whatever disagrees before inking.
9. Ink the clean copy with four things on it: a title, the north arrow, the scale ("one square = 100 paces"), and the date. Land changes; an undated map misleads.

## Watch out

- Guessed distances. "About that far" errors pile onto each other until the whole sheet is fiction. Pace everything you plot — pacing is fast.
- Slopes. Pacing up a steep hill measures the slope, not the flat distance a map wants. On steep ground, hold a rope level in short measured lengths, or at least write "steep" there and trust it less.
- Drifting stride. Uphill, downhill, mud, and fatigue all change your step. Pace important lines twice, once each direction, and average.
- Missing scale, north, or date. Without them the map is a pretty picture nobody can use or trust. Write all three before anything else.
- Drawing importance instead of size — the house huge, the far field tiny. Importance goes in labels; sizes obey the scale.
- One master copy. Maps get rained on and borrowed. Once it is checked, make a second copy and keep it elsewhere.

## How it works

Shrink every distance by the same fraction and every shape, angle, and direction on the ground survives onto the page — that is all a scale is. And two paced numbers, along the baseline and out from it, pin any point exactly; the baseline turns your whole land into positions you can measure.
