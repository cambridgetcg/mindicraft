---
title: Write with an alphabet
summary: Turn the sounds of speech into a small set of marks, and read the marks back into speech.
difficulty: hard
time: a month of practice
needs: [ink-and-pages]
---

Writing is speech that waits. Once you can write, a thought can cross years and miles without you carrying it. An alphabet is the cheapest kind of writing: instead of one picture for every word — thousands of pictures to learn — you use one mark for every sound. Most languages are built from only 20 to 40 distinct sounds, so 20 to 40 marks are enough to write anything you can say.

One rule first: if anyone near you already writes, learn their system instead of inventing one. A shared alphabet is worth far more than a clever private one. Build your own only if there truly is none to learn.

## You need

- Ink, a pen, and pages — for the master chart and your daily practice.
- A scratch surface — a tray of damp sand, a slate, or ash rubbed on a board. Practice there is free; save the pages for what you keep.
- A quiet hour every day for a month. The skill is small daily doses, not one heroic week.
- A partner — anyone who speaks your language, even a child. You cannot test your own writing; you already know what it says.

## Steps

1. Collect your sounds. Say simple words very slowly, stretching them: "mmm — aaa — nnn." Each piece you can stretch or pop separately is one sound.
2. Hunt sounds in pairs. Find word pairs that differ by only one sound — like "pat" and "bat," or "pat" and "pit." Every such pair proves two sounds are truly different. List every sound you find. Expect 20 to 40.
3. Design one mark per sound. Keep each mark to one, two, or three strokes. Make them wildly different from each other — a line, a hook, a circle, a cross — so no two can be confused at arm's length.
4. Make a master chart: each mark, and beside it a small picture of a word that starts with that sound. A drawing of a fish beside the mark for the "f" sound will remind anyone what the mark says.
5. Learn ten marks a day. Say the sound out loud each time you write the mark. Write each mark twenty times. In under a week you will know them all loosely; the month is for making them automatic.
6. Spell by stretching. To write a word, say it slowly and write one mark for each sound you hear. Do not worry about a "correct" spelling — write what you hear, the same way every time.
7. Read back every day. Each morning, read yesterday's page aloud. If you stumble, two marks are too alike — redesign the confusable one and note the change on the chart.
8. Test on your partner. Teach them the chart. Then write a sentence they have never heard, and have them read it aloud. When your words come out of their mouth, the alphabet works.
9. Freeze the chart. Once it passes the partner test, stop changing it, even to fix small ugliness. Copy the master chart several times and give the copies away. A frozen, shared chart is the whole point.

## Watch out

- One mark per word instead of per sound. That road needs thousands of signs and years of study. Marks stand for sounds, not things.
- Lookalike marks. Mirror-image pairs cause endless mix-ups. Check the chart at arm's length in dim light; redesign anything you hesitate on.
- Skipping the vowels — the open, singable sounds like "aa" and "ee." Without them, reading becomes guessing. Give every vowel its own mark.
- Changing marks midway. Every change makes your old pages unreadable. Freeze early and live with small flaws.
- Practicing writing but never reading. Reading back is half the skill and the harder half. Read aloud daily.
- Working alone. Without a partner test you will believe your writing is clear when it is not.

## How it works

Speech builds endless words from a small, closed set of sounds, recombined. An alphabet steals that trick for the page: a few dozen marks, endless writing. That is why a child can learn all the marks in weeks, while picture-writing takes a scribe years.
