Memory bends. Two honest people can remember the same debt differently within a season, and both will swear they are right. A written record does not bend. It turns "I am sure it was ten baskets" into "the page says eight, and we both marked it." Records end arguments before they start.

They also show slow truths no memory can hold: whether the grain store is quietly shrinking, which field actually pays, whether this winter is longer than the last. A week of setting up the habit buys you years of peace.

## You need

- Writing you can read back — your alphabet, practiced enough that a line takes a moment, not a struggle.
- One book of pages, bound or tied together. One book, not loose scraps — scraps scatter and vanish.
- Ink and a pen kept next to the book, so writing an entry costs nothing.
- Honest, fixed measures — the same marked basket, the same weight, the same rod, every time. A number means nothing if the measure behind it drifts.
- A dry shelf, off the ground, away from the fire — damp, mice, and sparks are the book's enemies.
- A witness for debts — a second person who watches the entry made.

## Steps

1. Make one book the book. Everything goes in it, in time order, newest entry below the last. Rule each page into four columns: date, who, what, how much.
2. Write at the moment, not at night. When grain comes in or goes out, the entry is made then and there. By evening the morning is already soft.
3. Count things in and things out. Harvest arrives: date, which field, how many baskets. Grain leaves: date, who took it, how much, and why. One line per event.
4. Keep a running total. After each entry, write the new total of the store in the last column. Then the book always tells you what you hold, without a recount.
5. Record debts with both parties present. Write the date, who lends, who borrows, how much, and when it is due. Read the line aloud, then both make their mark beside it. When the debt is paid, do not erase it — write a new line saying "paid," dated, with both marks again.
6. Never erase anything. Cross out mistakes with a single line, so the old words can still be read, and write the correction beside them. A scraped or torn page breeds suspicion; a visible correction breeds trust.
7. Recount once a month. Actually count and measure the real store, then compare it with the book's total. Write both numbers down, even when they disagree — especially when they disagree.
8. Chase every difference. Book says forty, floor says thirty-seven: that gap is rats, rot, spillage, theft, or sloppy entries. Find out which, and write down what you found.
9. Close full pages. When a page fills, write "closed," the date, and carry the totals to the top of the next page. When the book fills, start a new one the same way and keep the old one safe.

## Watch out

- Trusting memory "just this once." The one unrecorded debt is always the one that ends a friendship. No exceptions, however small or however trusted the person.
- A drifting measure. If this year's "basket" is bigger than last year's, every comparison in the book is a lie. Keep one standard basket and weight, mark them, and lend them out rather than accept someone else's.
- Delayed entries. A record written from memory inherits all of memory's bending. Write at the moment.
- Only one reader. A book only one person can read dies with that person, and invites that person to cheat. Teach at least two people to read and keep it.
- Ignoring small gaps at the recount. A steady loss of one basket a month is how theft and rot hide. Small and steady is a signal, not noise.
- One copy of everything. Fire or flood takes the book, and every debt with it. Once a month, copy the totals and open debts onto a single page kept in a different building.

## How it works

A dispute about memory has two stories and no judge. A dispute about a shared page has one story, written when the event was fresh, marked by both sides. The page is not smarter than the people — it is just unable to change its mind.
