"A big sack." "A long rope." "A fair share." Words like these start arguments, because your "big" is not my "big." A unit fixes that. A unit is one agreed amount — one stick's length, one stone's weight, one cup's fill — that everyone measures against. Once you have units, "a lot" becomes "eleven cups" — the same for you, for me, and for next year's trade.

Measuring is counting in disguise: you count how many times the unit fits into the thing. That is why tallying comes first.

## You need

- **A straight stick, about arm length** — this becomes your length unit. Hardwood, dry, and straight when you sight down it like an arrow.
- **A stiff bar and string for a balance** — a straight stick about as long as your forearm, strong string, and two light pans (flat baskets, bark trays, or halved gourds of matching size).
- **A set of matching small things for weights** — pebbles ground to match each other, or dried seeds of one plant; seeds of the same kind are naturally close in weight.
- **One cup, pot, or gourd** — this becomes your volume unit, the measure for grain, water, and anything poured.
- **Your tally kit** — stick and knife, to count how many units fit.
- **A trusted keeping place** — the master units must live somewhere everyone can check them.

## Steps

1. **Cut the master length stick.** Trim one straight stick to a handy length — from your elbow to your fingertips is a classic choice. Smooth the ends flat. This exact stick is now "one length." Copies are made *from it*, never from memory.
2. **Divide the stick for small measures.** Fold a string to the stick's length, fold that string in half, and in half again. Mark the stick at each fold: now you have halves and quarters. Notch the marks so they cannot rub off.
3. **Measure length by walking the stick.** Lay the stick down, mark where it ends with your thumb, move it forward from that mark, and cut a tally notch for each full stick. A field is "40 sticks and a quarter," not "pretty far."
4. **Build the balance.** Tie a string to the exact middle of the bar so it hangs level when empty — slide the knot until it does. Hang one pan from each end at equal distances, using strings of equal length.
   - Test it: swap the two pans. If the bar still hangs level, the balance is honest.
5. **Make a weight set.** Pick one pebble as "one weight." Grind or choose other pebbles until each balances exactly against it. Then make a "five" by balancing one stone against five singles, and a "ten" against ten. Mark each with scratched lines: one line per unit, a cross for five.
6. **Weigh by balancing.** Put the goods in one pan, add weights to the other until the bar hangs level, and count the weights with tallies.
7. **Make the master cup.** Choose one sound pot or gourd. "One cup" means filled level to the brim, then struck flat across the top with a straight stick — no heaping, no hollow. Measure grain, flour, and liquids by counting level cupfuls.
8. **Guard the masters and check the copies.** Keep the master stick, weights, and cup in one respected place — a headman's house, a shrine, a storehouse. Once a season, check every working copy against them and fix or destroy any that drift.

## Watch out

- **Body parts as units.** Feet, hands, and paces differ from person to person — fine for rough work, ruinous for trade. Cut a stick and let the stick be the ruler.
- **Wet wood and wet seeds.** Wood swells and shrinks with weather; seeds gain weight when damp. Use dry, seasoned materials, and store masters somewhere dry.
- **A balance that isn't level when empty.** It silently cheats one side on every weighing. Test empty, and swap pans to test again, before anything important.
- **Heaped versus level cups.** A heaped cup holds up to a quarter more than a level one. Agree on "struck level" and always use the strike stick.
- **Copies made from copies.** Each copy adds a little error, and errors pile up. Always copy from the master, and mark copies so people know they are copies.
- **Quiet cheating.** Shaved sticks and hollowed weights appear wherever trade grows; regular, open checking against the masters is the cure.

## How it works

Every measurement is a comparison against one agreed thing. The unit itself is a free choice — any stick, any stone, any cup will do — but the agreement is not. The moment a whole village compares against the *same* stick, lengths become numbers that can be tallied, written, and argued from evidence instead of shouting. Fair measures are trust, made solid.
