Most fires fail before they start. Not because the spark was bad, but because the wood was not ready. A flame is like a baby: at first it can only eat the tiniest, driest food, and it grows in stages. If you gather the right materials first — and enough of them — lighting the fire later becomes the easy part.

You are collecting three sizes of fuel. **Tinder** is the fluffy, hair-thin stuff that catches a spark or a small flame. **Kindling** is thin sticks that catch from burning tinder. **Fuel wood** is the thick pieces that burn for a long time. Gather all three before you strike a single spark.

## You need

- **Your hands and eyes** — nothing else is required.
- **Dry weather sense** — the driest material is up off the ground. Anything lying on soil soaks up damp, even on a sunny day.
- **A dry place to stash your pile** — under a rock ledge, inside your shirt, or on a bed of bark. Wet tinder is useless tinder.
- **Optional: a knife or sharp stone** — for scraping bark into fluff and splitting damp sticks to reach the dry inside.

## Steps

1. **Gather tinder first — a bundle at least the size of a grapefruit.** Look for:
   - Dry dead grass, crushed dead leaves, and dead pine needles.
   - Papery bark you can peel in thin curls — birch bark is the best; it burns even when damp because of natural oils.
   - Fluffy seed heads, like cattail down or thistle fluff — they flash fast, so mix them with grass.
   - Inner bark of dead cedar or similar trees, rubbed between your palms until it becomes soft fiber like coarse hair.
2. **Fluff the tinder.** Rub, shred, and tease it apart. The finer and airier it is, the lower the heat it needs to catch. Shape it loosely, like a bird's nest.
3. **Gather kindling — an armful, sorted by thickness.**
   - Match-thin to pencil-thin: about two big handfuls.
   - Pencil-thin to finger-thick: another armload.
   - Finger-thick to thumb-thick: another armload.
4. **Take dead wood that is still standing or hanging**, not lying on the ground. Snap-test everything: a dry stick breaks with a sharp *crack*. If it bends or tears quietly, it is wet or green (still alive) — leave it.
5. **Gather fuel wood — pieces from wrist-thick to arm-thick**, as long as your forearm to as long as your leg. A stack up to your knee will feed a small fire for an evening.
6. **Gather double what you think you need.** Everyone runs out. A fire that dies at midnight because the pile ran dry is the most common firewood mistake there is.
7. **Sort the pile by size and keep it dry**, tinder most protected of all. Now you are ready to build a fire pit.

## Watch out

- **Wood from the ground feels dry but is not.** Soil moisture creeps in from below. Take standing dead wood or branches caught in other branches. If you must use ground wood, split it — the inside is often dry.
- **Green (living) wood will not burn well.** It hisses, smokes, and steals heat. Signs of life: it bends instead of snapping, the bark is tight, the inside is pale and moist. Cutting live trees also wastes a living thing for nothing.
- **Rain does not have to stop you.** Look under dense evergreens, under logs, under rock overhangs. Standing dead wood sheds most rain; split it to reach dry wood inside. Birch bark lights even wet.
- **Too little tinder is the classic failure.** A pinch will not do. You want a full, loose grapefruit-sized nest — and a spare one.
- **Do not skip the middle sizes.** A flame cannot jump from grass to a log. Every size step should be no more than about double the one before.

## How it works

Wood does not burn as solid wood — heat first turns it into flammable gas, and the gas is what carries the flame. Thin, fluffy material heats up fast because it has lots of surface and almost no bulk, so even a tiny spark can push it past its catching point (roughly 300 °C). Thick wood needs minutes of strong heat to reach that point, which is why the fire must climb the sizes like a ladder.
