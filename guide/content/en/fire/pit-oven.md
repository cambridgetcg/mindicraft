Roasting on a stick needs your hands and your eyes the whole time. A pit oven — a hole in the ground filled with fire-hot stones and sealed over — cooks for you while you build, hunt, or sleep. It cannot burn the food, it turns tough meat and hard roots soft, and it feeds many people from one fire. People on every continent invented some version of it: the hangi, the imu, the clambake, the earth oven. It is the same simple idea everywhere.

The idea: stones soak up a fire's heat and hold it for hours. Buried with food and a little moisture, they turn the pit into a sealed steam oven that sits at gentle baking heat for half a day.

## You need

- **A digging stick or shovel** and a patch of dry ground, away from tree roots.
- **20–30 dry stones, fist- to head-sized.** From a hillside or dry ground only — **never from a river or wet soil** (trapped water can make them explode in the fire). Dense, smooth stones hold heat best; avoid layered or cracked ones.
- **Firewood for a strong 1–2 hour fire.**
- **A big armful of green vegetation** — fresh grass, seaweed, or big leaves you can name as safe (banana, maize husks, dock). This makes steam and keeps food off the scorching stones.
- **Food.** Whole fish, joints of meat, birds, roots and tubers — the pit oven loves big pieces that would burn on a stick.
- **Loose soil** for the cover — the soil you dug out works.

## Steps

1. **Dig the pit:** about 50 cm deep and 60–80 cm across for a family-sized meal. Pile the soil beside it — you will need it back.
2. **Build a fire in the pit** and pile your stones on top of and among the burning wood.
3. **Burn hard for 1–2 hours.** The stones are ready when a drop of water flicked onto one dances and vanishes with an instant hiss.
4. **Rake out the embers and unburned wood.** You want hot stones, not open fire — flames in the pit will scorch, not bake. Nudge the stones into an even layer on the bottom.
5. **Lay 5–10 cm of green vegetation over the stones.** It will hiss and steam. That is right.
6. **Put the food in,** wrapped in more safe leaves. Put the biggest, slowest pieces in the middle where the heat lasts longest. Splash in a cup or two of water for extra steam.
7. **Cover it:** another thick layer of green vegetation, then bark, a hide, or cloth if you have any (it keeps soil out of the food), then 10–15 cm of soil over everything.
8. **Seal the leaks.** Walk around the mound; wherever you see steam or smoke escaping, pat on more soil. A sealed pit is a working oven; a leaking one is a cooling hole.
9. **Wait.** Fish and vegetables: 2–3 hours. A large joint of meat or whole bird: 3–5 hours. Longer does little harm — a pit oven is very hard to overcook with.
10. **Open it carefully.** Scrape the soil off sideways, then peel back the cover layers so no dirt falls in. Check the thickest piece of meat at the centre: no pink, juices clear. If in doubt, reseal and give it another hour.

## Watch out

- **River and wet stones explode.** This is the pit oven's one real danger. Dry upland stones only, and stand back during the burn — a stone that is going to crack usually does it in the first hour.
- **Poisonous leaves poison the meal.** Never wrap food in oleander, rhododendron, yew, or any plant you cannot confidently name. Plain grass is always available and always safe.
- **Escaping steam is escaping dinner.** Every wisp you see leaving the mound is heat gone. Seal it, and re-seal after an hour as the soil settles.
- **Too few or too-small stones = a raw centre.** The stones are the fuel; skimping on them cannot be fixed later. When unsure, heat more stone than you think you need.
- **Big meat undercooked at the bone** can carry parasites. Always check the centre before anyone eats, and reseal to finish rather than risk it.

## How it works

Stone stores a huge amount of heat — far more than air or wood coals of the same size. The ground around the pit is a thick insulating blanket, so that heat has nowhere to go but into the food. The green plants add water, and trapped steam carries heat into food much faster and more evenly than dry air, at a steady temperature that never climbs high enough to burn.
