Starting a fire from nothing is the hardest job in this whole branch. Keeping one alive is easy. For most of human history, people did not start a fire every day — they kept yesterday's fire going and carried it when they moved. A fire kept is an hour or two saved every day, which is why the hearth — the family fire never allowed to die — sits at the center of nearly every old culture. Learn this, and you only ever win the hard fight once.

The trick is the coal: a lump of glowing wood that burns without flame — nearly pure charcoal, dense stored fire. Buried in ash, a good coal stays alive eight to twelve hours on almost no fuel. Banking — the real word for tucking coals under ash for the night — is putting the fire to sleep, not out: tuck coals in at night, wake them at dawn, wrap one up for the road.

One truth first: a banked fire is still a fire — it looks like cold grey ash and can relight dry leaves twelve hours later. Everything here assumes a proper pit with bare ground cleared for a metre — one long stride — around.

## You need

- **A fire that has burned for a while** — a bed of coals (glowing orange lumps), not just flames. An hour or two of steady burning makes them: solid orange chunks the size of eggs, not flaky black bits.
- **One or two thick logs**, each as thick as your upper arm or more (8–15 cm). Dense hardwood if you can — oak, beech, hickory: heavy-for-its-size wood from broad-leaved trees. Thick, dense wood burns slowest and leaves the fattest coals.
- **Ash** — the soft grey powder your fire leaves behind. This is your blanket, one of the best insulators (heat-holders) in nature. Never throw it away.
- **A stick for raking** — arm-long, green if possible so it does not catch. Hands never go near live coals.
- **For carrying an ember:** dry punky wood (wood so rotten it feels light and spongy, like stale bread) or a dried bracket fungus (a hard shelf fungus from tree trunks, firm cork inside); a big sheet of bark to roll into a forearm-long tube; slightly damp grass or green leaves; and string, vine, or bark to tie it.

## Steps

1. **In the evening, feed the fire one last time.** Half an hour before sleep, push one or two thick logs into the middle of the coal bed — lazy flames licking the sides, not a blaze. These logs become tomorrow's coals.
2. **Let the flames die down to glow.** Wait until open flame is mostly gone and the logs wear a coat of glowing orange cracks — the surface turning to charcoal. Bank while big flames still leap, and you bury a fire still burning at full speed: it eats the fuel by midnight, or flares up in wind.
3. **Rake all the coals into one tight pile.** Use the stick, never your hands. Pull every glowing lump into one mound in the pit's middle, thick logs at its heart. Spread-out coals die alone within the hour; piled coals keep each other warm. Aim for two fists together or bigger.
4. **Bury the pile in ash, 3–5 cm deep — two finger-widths.** Rake ash over until no orange shows and only a wisp of smoke leaks out. It should look dead. It is not — the ash lets in just enough air to keep the coals smoldering: burning slowly, with no flame. If orange flickers through after a few minutes, add more ash.
5. **Keep rain and dew off.** If rain threatens, lean a bark slab or a flat dry stone (never a damp or river one — see Watch out) over the pit like a little roof — a hand above the ash, not pressed onto it, so air still breathes. Never seal the pile airtight; a coal with zero air truly dies.
6. **In the morning, rake the ash aside gently** — a hard rake scatters and kills weak coals. You will find lumps still glowing, or grey ones that blush orange when blown on softly. One living coal the size of a walnut is enough.
7. **Wake the fire.** Rake the living coals into a tight cluster. Lay on your finest, driest stuff — dry grass, shredded bark, twigs no thicker than a match. Blow long, slow breaths at the base from a hand-span back, so the air arrives as a stream, not a blast. The coals brighten with a soft hiss, the smoke thickens to a rolling curl, and — usually within a dozen breaths — flame. Feed it pencil-thick sticks, then finger-thick, then wrist-thick. Rake to cooking fire: ten minutes. From scratch: an hour.
8. **To carry fire, build an ember bundle** — the slow-match of old: fire slowed down until it can travel. Nest a fat, egg-sized glowing coal in a fist-sized wad of dry punky wood or crumbled bracket fungus — slow fuel the coal chews through for hours. Wrap the nest loosely in slightly damp grass or green leaves (too damp to catch itself, so it holds heat in without letting flame break out), then roll it all in the bark sheet as a forearm-long tube. Tie it shut, with a finger-sized air hole at each end.
9. **Tend the bundle as you walk.** Carry it cradled, away from your clothes. Check every half hour: a palm near an end should feel steady warmth and a thin thread of smoke. Fading? Swing it gently through the air to feed it oxygen and add punk. Too lively — hot, smoke pouring? Pinch the holes smaller. A good bundle walks four to six hours. At the new camp, tip the coal into your tinder nest — a loose ball of that same finest, driest stuff — and blow it to flame.

## Watch out

- **Wind can wake buried coals into open flame while you sleep** — moving air pushes oxygen through the ash, and the sleeping fire stands up — the number-one way a banked fire escapes. The save: bank inside your pit, a metre of bare cleared ground around, thicker ash on windy nights — never against a log or tree, never near dry leaves or grass.
- **Never bank a fire against or inside any shelter wall.** Heat creeps through wood and dried grass for hours and can light your shelter while you sleep. The hearth lives in the open, or under a wide-open smoke gap, at arm's length or more from anything that burns.
- **Never bank a fire inside a closed shelter — carbon monoxide kills quietly.** Smoldering charcoal gives off carbon monoxide: an invisible, smell-less gas that puts sleeping people deeper to sleep, then stops their breathing. Flame mostly burns this gas away; a flameless coal bed does not. Banked fire sleeps outside. No exceptions.
- **Dead coals in the morning** mean one of three things. Logs too thin — burned to powder before dawn; go arm-thick or thicker. Blanket too thin — heat leaked away; bury a full two finger-widths. Pile too loose — coals cooled alone; rake tighter. If mornings keep failing, bank later so more fire is left at sunrise.
- **Wet ash kills coals.** Soaked ash turns to a grey paste that lets no air through. Keep the roof-slab on when the sky threatens; never bank into damp ash.
- **A bundle that bursts into flame got too much air** — flame eats your slow fuel in minutes and can burn you. Close the tube to smother it; rewrap tighter, smaller holes. **A bundle gone cold got too little air or ran out of punk** — open the holes, swing it, feed it.
- **Smoke is not harmless.** Stay upwind, blow from the side, never breathe deep over a smoldering pile — smoke sears the lungs.
- **Never put river or wet rocks in or over the coal bed.** Water trapped inside stone turns to steam and can split the rock with a bang, throwing hot shards. Dry rocks from high ground, or none.
- **When you leave for good, the fire dies for good — drown, stir, drown, feel.** Banking is only for a fire you are staying near. Leaving? Pour water until the hissing stops, stir to the bottom, pour again, then hold the back of your hand near the ashes. Any warmth means not out — drown and stir again. A banked fire abandoned is a wildfire waiting for wind.

## How it works

One picture holds it all: fire is a hungry eater with three needs — fuel, heat, and air. Give all three freely and it feasts, blazing through a night's wood in an hour; take any one away and it dies. Banking pinches all three to a trickle — never to zero — so the fire eats the way a hibernating animal breathes: just enough to stay alive.

Start with the coal. When wood burns with flame, the flame is really burning gases — heat cooks smoke and vapour out of the wood, and they ignite in the air above; that is why flame dances a little apart from the wood. A coal is what remains after the gases are gone: nearly pure charcoal, glowing where air touches it. With its gases already spent, a coal has nothing left to flare — no flame flinging heat uselessly into the sky — so it is stored fire spending itself as slowly as fire can, which is why a coal bed radiates warmth for hours after the flames quit.

Now the ash blanket. Ash looks like nothing, but it is full of tiny air pockets, and trapped, still air barely carries heat — the trick of a wool blanket or a bird's fluffed feathers. So ash does two jobs at once: it holds the coal's heat in, keeping the coal above the temperature where burning can continue, and it chokes the airflow to a whisper — oxygen seeps through, but only barely. The coal cannot blaze, and cannot cool off and die. It smolders, maybe a hundred times slower than open fire: slow enough to last the night, fast enough to never quite go out. Morning waking is the reverse: pull off the blanket, give the finest fuel, give breath. All three needs restored, the fire stands up.

The ember bundle is a banked fire you can carry. Punky wood is soft, porous, and dry — a coal creeps through it slowly, like a spark along a fuse. The damp-grass wrap is a portable ash blanket, and the two air holes are the valve: bigger holes, faster burn; smaller, slower. Swinging the bundle forces fresh air through — it does the work of blowing, without stopping to kneel.

And the deeper idea: a kept fire changes the shape of a day. No hour lost each dawn to sparks and friction; warmth waiting when you wake; fire that travels when you do. That is why the undying hearth sat at the center of every old household — the family's stored victory over the hardest problem, tended so it never had to be won again.

## Go further

- **Try it tonight, in miniature.** In a safe, legal outdoor fire spot, bank a fist-sized pile of coals before dark and open it in the morning. A live coal out of a dead-grey mound teaches more than any page. Next night, bank one pile under 2 cm and one under 5 cm — the stronger morning coal tunes you to your wood and weather.
- **Hunt punk wood and bracket fungus on your next walk.** Spot standing dead trees with spongy wood and hard shelf fungi on old trunks; dry a pocketful — spare batteries for fire.
- **Time your mornings.** Wake a banked fire with a watch running; another day, start from scratch. The gap is what this skill buys you, every day.
- **This unlocks cooking and warmth.** Steady all-day coals — not flames — bake, roast, smoke, and keep a shelter livable; hearth-keeping is their foundation.
