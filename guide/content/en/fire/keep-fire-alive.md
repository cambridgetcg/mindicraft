Starting a fire from nothing is the hardest job in this whole branch. Keeping one alive is easy. For most of human history, people did not start a fire every day — they kept yesterday's fire going, and carried it with them when they moved. Learn this, and you only ever have to win the hard fight once.

The trick is the coal. A coal is a lump of glowing wood that burns without flame. Buried in ash, a good coal can stay alive for eight to twelve hours on almost no fuel. Your job is to tuck coals in at night, wake them in the morning, and wrap one up for the road.

## You need

- **A fire that has burned for a while** — you need a bed of coals (glowing orange lumps), not just flames. A fire usually makes good coals after an hour or two.
- **One or two thick logs** — dense hardwood if you can get it (oak, beech, hickory: heavy wood from broad-leaved trees). Thick, dense wood burns slowest.
- **Ash** — the soft grey powder your fire leaves behind. This is your blanket. Never throw it away.
- **For carrying an ember:** dry punky wood (wood so rotten it feels light and spongy) or a dried bracket fungus (a hard shelf-shaped fungus that grows on tree trunks), plus a big piece of bark and some string or vine to tie it.

## Steps

1. **In the evening, feed the fire one last time.** Push one or two thick logs into the middle so they catch before you sleep.
2. **Rake all the coals into one tight pile.** Spread-out coals die; a pile keeps itself warm. Use a stick, not your hands.
3. **Bury the pile in ash, 3–5 cm deep.** Cover it completely. It should look dead. It is not — the ash lets in just enough air to keep the coals smoldering (burning slowly without flame).
4. **Keep rain and dew off.** If rain is likely, lean a slab of bark or a flat stone over the pit — above the ash, not pressed onto it.
5. **In the morning, rake the ash aside gently.** You will find coals still glowing, or grey lumps that glow when you blow on them.
6. **Wake the fire.** Put a handful of the finest, driest material you have — dry grass, shredded bark — right on the coals and blow long, gentle breaths until it flames. Add small sticks, then bigger ones.
7. **To carry fire to a new camp, build an ember bundle.** Take a fat glowing coal and nest it in dry punky wood or bracket fungus. Wrap that nest loosely in slightly damp grass or green leaves, then in a big sheet of bark rolled into a tube. Tie it shut, leaving a small air hole at each end.
8. **Tend the bundle as you walk.** Check it every half hour or so. If it is fading, swing it gently through the air to feed it oxygen. A good bundle lasts several hours. At the new camp, tip the coal into tinder and blow it to flame.

## Watch out

- **Wind can wake buried coals into open flame while you sleep.** Always bank coals inside your fire pit, with ground cleared bare for at least a metre around. Never bank a fire against a log, a tree, or in dry leaves.
- **Dead coals in the morning** usually mean the logs were too thin or the ash blanket too thin. Use thicker logs, more ash, and a tighter pile.
- **Wet ash kills coals.** Rain-soaked ash turns to a paste that smothers everything. Keep the pile covered.
- **A bundle that bursts into flame is getting too much air.** Wrap it tighter and make the air holes smaller. A bundle that dies got too little — open the holes a bit and swing it.
- **Never bank a fire inside a closed shelter.** Smoldering coals give off carbon monoxide, an invisible, smell-less gas that can kill sleeping people. Fire sleeps outside, or under a wide-open smoke gap.

## How it works

A coal only needs a trickle of air to stay alive. Ash is full of tiny air pockets, so it works like a blanket: it slows the escape of heat and slows the flow of air to almost nothing. The coal burns a hundred times slower than an open fire — slow enough to last the night, fast enough to never quite die.
