This is fire from nothing but wood, string, and effort — no stone, no steel, no luck. The bow drill is a machine that lets your whole arm rub wood on wood, fast and long enough to matter. The rubbing grinds off hot wood dust, and when the dust pile passes about 400 °C, it fuses into a tiny glowing coal called an **ember**. The ember, not a flame, is what you make. Flame comes later, from your breath.

It is honest, hard work, and almost nobody gets it on day one. But once your hands learn it, you can make fire anywhere dry wood grows.

## You need

- **Dry, dead, soft-to-medium wood** for the moving parts — willow, cedar, aspen, cottonwood, or basswood. Test: your thumbnail should leave a dent in it, but the wood shouldn't crumble. Rotten-spongy wood fails; rock-hard wood fails. Use the *same* wood for spindle and board.
- **A spindle** — a straight stick, thumb-thick (about 2 cm) and 20–25 cm long, carved round and smooth. Blunt-rounded at the bottom (the friction end), sharpened to a dull point at the top.
- **A hearth board** — a flat plank of the same wood, about 2 cm thick, a hand wide, at least a forearm long.
- **A bow** — a slightly curved stick, arm-long and stiff, with your cordage tied to both ends just loose enough to twist the spindle into it.
- **A handhold** — a palm-sized piece of *hard* wood, bone, or stone with a small dimple carved in it, to press down on the spindle's top.
- **An ember pan** — a dry leaf or bark chip to catch the ember under the board.
- **Tinder nest and a laid fire**, ready in your pit before you start.

## Steps

1. **Burn in the socket.** Carve a shallow dimple in the hearth board, one spindle-width in from the edge. Twist the spindle once around the bowstring so the string grips it. Kneel: left foot on the board (if right-handed), spindle upright in the dimple, handhold pressing on top, your left wrist locked hard against your left shin so the spindle cannot wobble. Saw the bow back and forth with slow, full strokes until the dimple burns smooth, round, and smoking.
2. **Cut the notch.** Stop. Carve a clean V-shaped slice from the board's edge into the burned circle — like one-eighth of a pie, its point almost reaching the circle's centre. Hot dust collects and packs together here, and packed dust holds its own heat. Too narrow and dust can't gather; too wide and the spindle falls out.
3. **Set the ember pan** flat under the notch.
4. **Grease the top, never the bottom.** Rub the spindle's pointed top and the handhold dimple with crushed green leaves, skin oil from beside your nose, or fat. The top must slip; the bottom must grip.
5. **Drill for the ember.** Same position, body locked. Start with long, slow, full strokes and moderate downward pressure. When steady smoke rises, shift gears: faster strokes, more pressure, and *keep going ten to fifteen seconds after* smoke pours out thickly on its own. Dark brown-black dust should fill the notch.
6. **Stop and be still.** Lift the spindle away gently. If the dust pile keeps smoking by itself, you have an ember. Do not blow on it yet — let it sit and strengthen for thirty to sixty seconds, fanning it with the gentlest hand-waves only if it fades.
7. **Deliver it.** Tap the board gently so the ember stays on the pan, carry the pan to your tinder nest, and tip the ember into the nest's heart. Fold the nest loosely around it.
8. **Blow it to flame** — long, soft, steady breaths, growing stronger as the smoke thickens, until it flames. Set the burning nest into your laid fire.

## Watch out

- **Damp wood is the number one killer.** It steals heat as steam. Everything must snap-dry; keep parts inside your clothes in wet weather. Squeaking while drilling often means moisture — or too little pressure.
- **The socket goes glassy and shiny** and stops making dust: pressure too low or wood too hard. Roughen it with a knife tip, add a pinch of sand, press harder.
- **String slips instead of spinning the spindle:** tighten the cord, or wrap it twice around. Cordage stretches; retie often.
- **Spindle keeps flying out:** your wrist isn't braced on your shin, or the spindle isn't vertical. Lock the frame of your body; the machine is you.
- **Smoke but never an ember:** almost always stopping too early, or a bad notch. When you think you're done, do ten more hard strokes. Check the notch is an open channel to the centre.
- **Blisters** end sessions early. Pad your hand and build up over days — needing days of practice is normal, not failure.
