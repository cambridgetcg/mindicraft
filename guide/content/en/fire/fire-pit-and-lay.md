title: Make a safe fire pit and lay a fire
summary: Clear the ground, ring it with stone or bare earth, stack tinder and sticks so a tiny flame can grow, and know how you will put it out.
difficulty: easy
time: an hour
needs: [gather-tinder]

A fire is a tool with no handle. It goes where the fuel is, and it does not care whose fuel it is. The pit is what keeps it yours. A good pit also makes lighting easier: a fire laid well catches from one small flame with no fiddling, while a bad heap can swallow a hundred sparks and give nothing back.

Build the pit and lay the fire *before* you try to make any flame or ember. When you finally get one, it will be small and short-lived — everything must be ready and waiting.

## You need

- **Your sorted fuel piles** — tinder nest, thin kindling, thicker sticks, fuel wood, all within arm's reach and dry.
- **An open, flat spot** — away from tents, dry grass, and anything with branches overhead. Fire climbs.
- **Dry stones or bare mineral earth** — for the ring. Mineral earth means dirt, sand, or gravel with nothing plant-like in it.
- **A way to put the fire out, ready first** — a container of water, or a big pile of loose dirt or sand beside the pit. If you cannot put it out, do not light it.
- **A digging tool** — a strong stick or flat stone is enough.

## Steps

1. **Choose the spot.** Flat ground, sheltered from strong wind, at least 3 metres (10 feet) from anything that can burn — bushes, dry grass, wood piles, shelters — and with open sky above, no branches within twice your height.
2. **Clear a circle down to bare earth**, about 2 metres (6 feet) across. Scrape away every leaf, root, and bit of dry grass. In forest soil, dig through the dark, root-filled layer to the pale mineral dirt below — the dark layer itself can smoulder (burn slowly without flame) underground for days.
3. **Dig a shallow pit** in the centre, a hand-depth deep (10–15 cm) and about as wide as your forearm is long. The pit shields the young flame from wind.
4. **Ring the pit with dry stones** the size of your fist or bigger, if stones are around. The ring stops embers rolling out. No stones? A raised lip of packed dirt works.
5. **Place your extinguisher** — water or dirt pile — beside the pit, on the side away from the wind. Do this now, not later.
6. **Lay the fire.** The simple, reliable lay is the teepee:
   - Put a piece of dry bark or a flat stone on the pit floor as a dry base.
   - Set your tinder nest on it.
   - Lean your thinnest sticks (match- to pencil-thin) over the tinder in a cone, like a tiny tent. Leave a doorway on the wind-facing side so you can reach in — and so air can too.
   - Lean pencil- to finger-thick sticks over those, still loose. Fire needs air gaps; if you can't see through the pile anywhere, it's too tight.
7. **Stage the next sizes** in reach: thumb-thick sticks to add once the cone is burning, then wrist-thick fuel. Add each size only when the fire eats the current size eagerly.
8. **Know your shutdown, in this order:** stop feeding it early; let it burn down to coals; drown it with water; stir the wet ash with a stick; drown again; then hold the back of your hand near the ashes. If you feel *any* warmth, repeat. Cold to the touch means out. With dirt instead of water: stir dirt through the coals again and again until everything is cold — never just bury coals, because buried coals stay hot for a day or more.

## Watch out

- **Stones from rivers, lakes, or wet ground can explode in fire.** Water trapped inside turns to steam and can crack the stone with a bang, throwing sharp hot pieces. Use stones from dry, high ground, and skip any that feel damp or layered like pages.
- **Overhead branches catch from rising sparks.** Look up before you dig. If you see branches, move.
- **Wind is the escape artist.** In strong wind, dig deeper, keep the fire small, or wait. One flying ember in dry grass can outrun you.
- **Packing the lay too tight smothers it.** Flames need air more than they need fuel. Loose, airy, cone-shaped — a fist should fit between the tinder and the outer sticks.
- **"It looks out" is not out.** Grey ash can hide coals hot enough to relight hours later. Only cold-to-the-touch is out. This is the rule people die by skipping.
- **Never leave it alone.** Not for a nap, not for a walk. An unwatched fire is a wildfire waiting for one gust.
