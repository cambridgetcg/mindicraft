Wood fire tops out around 600–800 °C — hot enough to cook and warm, not hot enough to change stone and metal. Charcoal is wood with the water and tarry gases cooked out, leaving nearly pure carbon. With air blown into it, a charcoal fire passes 1,100 °C. That is the doorway to hard-fired pottery, lime, glass, copper, and eventually iron. Almost everything above fire in the tech tree burns charcoal.

The trick: heat wood without letting it burn. Plenty of air turns wood into ash and smoke — gone. Almost no air can only cook the wood, and cooked wood is charcoal. So you start a fire, bury it alive, and let it smolder for hours under a skin of soil.

## You need

- **Dry wood, about arm-thick, cut or broken into 30–50 cm lengths.** Dense hardwood (oak, beech, hickory) makes strong charcoal that holds together; soft wood works but crumbles. A knee-high pile of wood makes a small, worthwhile first batch.
- **A patch of bare, diggable soil,** far from trees, roots, and anything dry — this fire runs unwatched for a day.
- **Green leaves, grass, or ferns** — a mat to stop soil falling straight into the wood.
- **Loose soil and a digging stick or shovel** — soil is your air valve.
- **Water nearby,** enough to douse trouble.
- **A long poking stick.**

## Steps

1. **Dig a pit** about 50 cm deep and 1 m across in bare mineral soil (soil with no roots or buried leaf litter in it). Clear the ground bare for 3 m around.
2. **Light a strong fire in the bottom** and feed it until you have a fierce bed of coals.
3. **Stack your wood into the pit,** tight and flat, layer on layer, until it is full and the whole load has caught and is burning well — usually 15–30 minutes of open burning.
4. **Bury it alive.** Lay the mat of green leaves or grass over the burning wood, then shovel on about 10 cm of soil — everywhere except three or four fist-sized vent holes around the edge.
5. **Watch the smoke.** Thick white or grey smoke means water and tar are cooking out of the wood. This is right. It will pour out for hours — roughly 6–12 for a pit this size.
6. **Patrol while it smokes.** The soil skin dries, cracks, and slumps; every new crack is an air leak that turns charcoal into ash. Pat soil over every leak. If flames ever break through, smother them with soil at once.
7. **Seal it when the smoke changes.** When the smoke turns thin, hazy blue — like heat-shimmer — the wood has become charcoal and is starting to burn itself. Close every vent and every crack. Airtight. The fire inside now suffocates.
8. **Wait 24–48 hours for it to cool.** Do not peek. This is the hardest step and the most important one.
9. **Open and sort.** Good charcoal is black all the way through, feels light for its size, and snaps with a clean ring. Pieces with brown middles are half-done — save them for the next batch. Expect about 1 part charcoal from every 4–5 parts wood by weight. Store it dry, off the ground.

## Watch out

- **Opening early sets the whole batch on fire.** Hot fresh charcoal meeting sudden air ignites almost instantly, and hours of work burn to ash in minutes. When in doubt, wait another day. Charcoal keeps; patience is free.
- **The smoke is heavy with carbon monoxide** — an invisible, smell-less gas that kills. Work upwind, never stand in the pit or lean over it breathing the smoke, and never run a charcoal burn near where anyone sleeps.
- **Fire can creep underground** through roots and buried litter and surface days later. This is why you use bare mineral soil and a cleared ring. Douse the whole area when you finish.
- **Leaks make ash, not charcoal.** If you open the pit to find mostly grey powder, the seal failed somewhere. Patrol more often next time and keep spare soil piled at hand.
- **"Cold" charcoal can reignite days later.** Before storing, spread it out and check every piece with a bare hand for warmth. Store it dry — wet charcoal is safe but useless until dried again.

## How it works

Heat without air cannot burn wood — it can only break it apart. The water and tars boil off as that thick white smoke, and what stays behind is nearly pure carbon. Carbon holds about twice the heat of wood, weight for weight, burns with almost no smoke or flame, and — fed with blown air — reaches temperatures raw wood never can.
