Cooking is not just about taste. Heat kills the germs and parasites (tiny creatures that live in raw meat and can make you very sick) that raw food carries. It also breaks food down so your body gets more energy from every bite. A cooked meal feeds you better and risks you less — that trade never stops being worth it.

The one rule that matters most: **cook over coals, not over flames.** Flames are wild — they blacken the outside of food while the inside stays raw, and they coat everything in soot. Coals, the glowing orange bed a fire leaves behind, give steady even heat you can actually control.

## You need

- **A fire burned down to coals.** Light it 30–45 minutes before you want to cook. Coals are ready when the flames are gone and the lumps glow orange under a skin of grey ash.
- **A green stick, about as thick as your thumb.** "Green" means cut fresh from a living tree, so it is full of sap and will not catch fire. Willow, hazel, maple, and oak are all safe. **Never use oleander or yew — both are poisonous** and can poison the food on them. If you cannot name the tree, pick another.
- **A flat, dry stone** (optional, for stone-top cooking). Take it from a dry hillside or dry ground — **never from a river, lake, or wet soil.**
- **Food cut small.** Pieces 2–3 cm thick cook through before they burn. Big lumps do the opposite.

## Steps

1. **Rake a bed of coals to one side of the fire.** Keep the main fire burning on the other side so you can rake fresh coals over as these fade.
2. **Test the heat with your hand.** Hold your palm where the food will sit. If you can keep it there for 4–5 seconds before it hurts, the heat is right for roasting. 2–3 seconds means very hot — good for searing, easy to burn.
3. **To roast on a stick:** sharpen the green stick, peel the bark off the tip, and skewer the food along its length so it cannot spin or fall. Prop or hold it 15–25 cm above the coals.
4. **Turn the food every minute or two.** Slow, even turning is the whole skill. If one side hisses and darkens fast, lift it higher.
5. **To cook on a stone:** stand the flat stone at the edge of the fire and let it heat for 30–60 minutes, then drag it out, brush off the ash with a leafy twig, and cook on top of it like a pan. Good for thin meat, fish, eggs, and flatbread.
6. **Roots and tubers** (potatoes and their wild cousins) can go straight into the ash at the edge of the coals. Turn them now and then; they are done when a sharp stick slides in easily — usually 30–60 minutes.
7. **Check that meat is truly done.** Cut into the thickest part: no pink anywhere, and the juices run clear, not red or cloudy. Fish is done when it flakes apart. When in doubt, cook it longer — you cannot see or smell the germs that matter.

## Watch out

- **Wet or river stones can explode in a fire.** Water trapped inside turns to steam and bursts the stone, throwing hot sharp pieces. Only use stones from dry ground, and stand back while any stone heats for the first time.
- **Burned outside, raw inside** means too much heat or open flame. Move the food higher, wait for calmer coals, or cut the pieces smaller.
- **Meat near the bone cooks slowest.** Check there first, not at the thin edge.
- **Raw meat juice on cooked food makes it raw-risky again.** Keep raw and cooked on separate sticks, stones, and leaves, and clean your hands between handling them.
- **Undercooked pork, bear, and freshwater fish carry the worst parasites.** Give these no benefit of the doubt: cook them all the way through, every time.
- **Dripping fat flares into flame.** Expect it, keep your face back, and pull the food aside until the flare dies.
