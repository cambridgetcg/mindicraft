---
title: Start a fire with sparks
summary: Strike sparks from hard stone and steel into your tinder and blow the glow gently into flame.
difficulty: steady
time: a day

A spark is a tiny piece of burning metal, hotter than 800 °C but smaller than a grain of sand. It lives for less than a second. Your whole job is to give it a soft, dry bed where its heat can settle and spread before it dies. People made fire this way for thousands of years: strike, catch, breathe. It asks for patience, not strength.

The tool pair is a hard stone and a piece of steel. The stone must be harder than the steel — flint, chert, or quartz all work. These are glassy stones that break with curved, sharp edges. When their sharp edge scrapes steel, it shaves off tiny metal flakes, and the scraping itself heats those flakes until they burn in the air. That burning flake is your spark.

## You need

- **A hard, sharp-edged stone** — flint, chert, or quartz. Test: it should scratch glass, and a fresh break looks smooth and shell-shaped, not grainy. Break a rounded stone open to get sharp edges.
- **Carbon steel** — an old file, the spine of a non-stainless knife, or any hard steel scrap. Stainless steel and soft iron give poor sparks or none. (A modern ferro rod, if you have one, throws far hotter sparks — use it the same way.)
- **Spark-catching tinder** — this is the make-or-break item. Plain stone-and-steel sparks are too cool for ordinary grass. You need something that catches a bare spark and holds it as a glow:
  - **Char cloth** — cotton cloth baked black without air: cook scraps in a nearly closed tin in a fire's coals until smoke stops, then let it cool shut. Making it needs a fire, so make a batch from your first-ever fire and never be without it again.
  - **True tinder fungus (chaga)** or the felt-like inside of horse-hoof fungus, both from birch trees, dried — the old-world natural spark catchers.
  - Very fine, bone-dry plant down (cattail fluff, scorched cotton) can work but is much less forgiving.
- **A full tinder nest and a laid fire**, ready in your pit before you strike.

## Steps

1. **Get everything ready first:** laid fire in the pit, tinder nest within reach, spark catcher at hand.
2. **Hold the stone in one hand** with a sharp edge facing up and slightly out. Lay a small piece of char cloth (or fungus) flat on top of the stone, right behind the edge, pinned by your thumb.
3. **Strike down and past the edge with the steel** — a fast, glancing scrape, like trying to shave a sliver off the stone's edge with the steel. Wrist snap, not arm swing. Sparks should spray from the edge.
4. **Watch the char.** You are waiting for one spark to land on it and take hold: a tiny orange dot that stays and slowly grows. Ten strikes or a hundred — keep the angle and keep going.
5. **Feed the glow air, gently.** Once a dot glows, a soft breath makes it spread across the char like a sunrise. Do not blow hard yet — a glow this young can be blown out or blown away.
6. **Move the glowing char into the heart of your tinder nest.** Fold the nest loosely around it, like closing a bun around filling, leaving gaps for air.
7. **Blow it into flame.** Hold the nest at arm's length, slightly above your face height so rising smoke misses your eyes. Give long, steady breaths — the kind you'd use to cool soup, not to blow out candles. Smoke will thicken, then turn yellow-grey, then *whoomph*: flame.
8. **Set the burning nest into the doorway of your laid fire**, under the thin sticks. Let the cone catch, then feed sizes upward as the fire strengthens.

## Watch out

- **No sparks at all?** The steel is stainless or too soft, or your stone edge has gone dull. Break the stone for a fresh edge; find harder steel. Sparks come from the *steel*, so the stone only needs to be hard and sharp.
- **Sparks but nothing catches?** Your catcher is damp or too coarse. Char cloth must be fully black and crisp; brown patches were undercooked. Dry everything against your skin or in sunshine before trying again.
- **The glow dies when you blow.** You blew too hard too soon, or the nest is packed tight and airless. Fluff the nest, breathe softer and longer.
- **Flying sparks land where you don't want them.** Strike over your pit or bare earth, never over dry grass. You are literally throwing fire.
- **Skinned knuckles** are the usual first-day injury — follow through past the stone, not into it, and keep fingers behind the edge.
- **Frustration is normal** — plan for a day of practice. Protect your char cloth supply; use small pieces.
