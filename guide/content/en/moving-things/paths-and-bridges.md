Mud steals more from a settlement than storms do — a little every single day. A boggy track twists ankles, soaks feet, swallows sled runners, and turns a ten-minute walk to the spring into a chore everyone dodges. A firm dry path is quietly one of the biggest gifts you can build. And where a stream cuts your world in two, a small bridge stitches it back together.

Both jobs come down to one enemy: water. A path fails where water sits on it. A bridge fails where water reaches it. Build so water always has somewhere better to go, and your work lasts years.

## You need

- **A digging tool** — spade, mattock, or a fire-hardened digging stick — for ditches and levelling.
- **An axe or saw** — for clearing, for corduroy logs, and for the bridge.
- **Stones and gravel** — fist-sized stones for a base, smaller gravel for the top. Riverbeds and scree slopes are your quarry.
- **Logs** — wrist-to-arm thick for boggy sections; one or two thigh-thick (20 cm or more) sound logs for the bridge itself.
- **Stakes and cordage** — to pin logs and mark your line.
- **A helper** — a bridge log is a two-person move at least; use your rollers and levers.

## Steps

1. **Choose the line.** Follow where feet already go — people and animals find the driest route by instinct. Avoid dips where water ponds. On a hillside, slant gently across the slope; never run a path straight downhill.
2. **Clear and strip.** Cut brush, then strip off the turf (the living grass mat) along a strip 60 cm wide for walkers, 1.2 m if a handcart will use it.
3. **Crown the path:** shape it so the centre sits 2–5 cm higher than the edges. Water runs off a crown instead of pooling.
4. **Ditch the wet stretches.** Dig a side ditch a spade deep along the uphill side, and give it an outlet downhill every so often. A ditch with nowhere to drain is just a long puddle.
5. **On soft ground, build up:** lay fist-sized stones as a base, then cap with gravel or coarse sand, and tamp (pound it firm).
6. **Over true bog, lay corduroy:** logs placed side by side across the path like ribs, pinned at the ends with stakes, then covered with gravel or soil. It floats your path on the mud.
7. **For the bridge, pick the spot:** the narrowest point with firm, high banks — 3–4 m across at most for a single-log bridge. Look for the debris line (twigs and grass caught in bushes) that marks the highest flood; your bridge must sit above it.
8. **Seat the ends well.** Lay a flat stone or short beam — a sill — on each bank, half a metre back from the edge, and rest the log ends on those. Stake or pin the ends so a flood surge can't pluck the bridge sideways.
9. **Make the walking surface flat:** hew (axe-flatten) the top of the log, or use a split log laid flat-face-up. Two logs side by side, cleated together with cross-pieces, wobble far less than one.
10. **Add a handrail** on one side — two posts and a pole — and test the bridge by loading it gradually well past one person's weight before anyone trusts it daily.

## Watch out

- **Any path that holds a puddle is becoming mud.** Don't fight it with more walking — crown it, ditch it, or build it up that same week.
- **A straight-downhill path becomes a stream, then a gully.** Slant across slopes, and on any sloping stretch add water bars — a half-buried log or stone ridge angled across the path every 10–20 m to throw water off the side.
- **A bridge set low gets taken by the first big flood.** Build above the highest debris line you can find, and anchor the ends.
- **Bark hides rot.** A bark-on log can rot soft in 1–3 years. Strip the bark, choose rot-resistant wood (oak, larch, cedar — whatever resists rot where you live), and replace the log when its surface goes spongy.
- **Wet or frosty wood is shockingly slippery.** Cut shallow cross-grooves into the walking face for grip, and never carry a heavy load across without checking your footing first.

## How it works

Soil is strong when dry and jelly when soaked, so every path trick — crown, ditch, stone base, corduroy — is really one trick: keep water out of the ground your feet press on. A bridge log works because wood is strong along its length; it fails at the ends, where rot and floods attack — which is why good sills and height above flood matter more than the log's thickness.
