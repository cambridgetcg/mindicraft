Water carries weight for free. A load that would break your back on land floats behind you on a raft, and a river that blocks your path becomes a road. The raft is the simplest boat there is: logs that float, tied so they float together.

But water is also the fastest killer in this whole guide. A raft is for calm, slow water only — a pond, a lake shore, a wide slow river. Fast water, cold water, and open sea will take a raft apart and you with it. Respect that line and rafting is safe, useful work.

## You need

- **5 to 9 dry logs**, each about 20 to 30 centimetres thick and 2.5 to 3 metres long. Dry is the key word: dead standing trees, or logs cut and dried for months. Fresh green wood is half water already and barely floats.
  - Test each log alone first: throw it in. It should float with at least a third of its body above the water. If it sits low, reject it.
  - Light woods float best: pine, spruce, cedar, poplar. Dense woods like oak float poorly.
- **2 to 4 cross-poles**, arm-thick, a bit longer than the raft is wide. They tie the logs into one body.
- **Lots of strong cord** — 20 to 30 metres at least. Real rope, or well-made plant-fibre cordage. Every lashing you skimp on is a log that leaves without you.
- **A push pole**, 3 to 4 metres long, for moving in shallow water, plus a rough paddle if the water is deeper than the pole.
- **A knife or axe** to notch the logs.
- **A helper if possible.** Rafts are heavy, and someone should watch from shore on the first float.

## Steps

1. **Pick your water and your day.** Calm, slow, warm-season water, close to shore, no wind. Walk the crossing point: you want gentle banks on both sides and no current you couldn't swim against.
2. **Build at the water's edge**, half in the shallows if you can. A finished raft is far too heavy to drag over land.
3. **Lay the logs side by side**, thick ends alternating (one log's thick end left, the next one's right) so the raft comes out even.
4. **Notch the logs** where the cross-poles will lie — a shallow flat cut across the top of each log, near each end. Poles seated in notches cannot roll.
5. **Lash the cross-poles down.** At every crossing point, tie a square lashing: wrap the cord over the pole and under the log, alternating directions, 6 to 8 tight wraps, then 2 to 3 frapping turns — wraps that go around the lashing itself to squeeze it tight — and finish with two half hitches.
   - Wet the cord before tying if it is plant fibre; it shrinks as it dries and grips harder.
6. **Add a second pair of cross-poles underneath** if you have cord to spare, mirroring the top ones, so each log is sandwiched. This is the difference between a raft and a loose bundle.
7. **Test empty.** Push the raft out on a line, let it sit, pull it back. Look for logs riding low or lashings gone slack.
8. **Test loaded, in the shallows.** Stand on it in knee-deep water. It should hold you with the deck at or above the waterline. If water washes over the top, add another log — do not "risk it."
9. **Cross kneeling or sitting, near the centre.** Pole against the bottom in shallow water; paddle in deeper. Keep the load tied to the raft, and keep yourself loose — never tie yourself to it.

## Watch out

- **Cold water kills in minutes, not hours.** Below about 15 °C, falling in steals your breath and strength fast. If the water is cold, don't cross — walk around, or wait for a warmer plan.
- **Current.** If a stick thrown in the water moves faster than a slow walk, the river is too fast. A raft cannot be steered against current; it goes where the river goes.
- **Green or waterlogged logs.** The raft floats at first, drinks water for an hour, then sinks under you mid-crossing. Test every log alone before it joins the raft.
- **One big lashing instead of many.** A single wrap around the whole bundle works loose immediately. Lash every crossing point separately.
- **Standing up.** A raft has no keel — no fin under the water to steady it. Stand and it tips. Kneel low, move slowly, keep weight centred.
- **No swimmers alone.** If you cannot swim the distance you are crossing, do not cross it on a first raft. Stay within a rope's length of shore until the raft has proven itself for days.
