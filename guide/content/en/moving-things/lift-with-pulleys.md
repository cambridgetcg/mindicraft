Some jobs are not about dragging — they are about lifting. Raising a roof beam, hauling a stone out of a pit, pulling a waterlogged log up a riverbank. Your arms alone can lift maybe 30–50 kg. A tripod — three poles lashed together at the top, standing like a giant's easel — plus a rope rigged the right way lets one careful person lift several times that, and set it down gently.

The rope trick is called mechanical advantage: arrange the rope so two strands share the load, and your hands only pull half the weight. You pay for it by pulling twice as much rope. Force traded for distance — that is the whole secret.

## You need

- **Three poles**, 3–4 m long and leg-thick (8–12 cm), of sound wood — no cracks, no rot, no woodpecker holes. They will each carry a share of everything you lift.
- **Strong rope, 10–15 m** — before trusting it, have two people yank it hard against a tree, then check every span for fraying. A rope is only as strong as its worst spot.
- **Shorter cord** for the lashing that binds the poles.
- **A smooth crossbar, forked hook, or carved pulley** — a pulley is a grooved wheel that spins on a pin so the rope rolls instead of scrapes. Bare rope over rough wood works, but friction steals up to a third of your pull; a greased smooth bar or a real pulley gives most of it back.
- **Grease** — fat or tallow for wherever rope rubs wood.

## Steps

1. **Test the rope first.** Hard pull by two people, then inspect. Do this every day you use it.
2. **Lay the three poles side by side**, tops level, and bind them 30–40 cm below the tops with a tripod lashing: wrap all three together many times, then wind the cord between the poles to tighten the wraps, and tie off hard.
3. **Stand it up** with a helper and spread the feet into a wide triangle, each foot 1.5–2 m from the others. Wider is more stable but lifts less high.
4. **Stop the feet sliding.** Dig each foot a few centimetres into the ground, and on smooth or sloping ground tie a rope loop around all three feet (a hobble) or drive a stake outside each one. Sliding feet are how tripods fall.
5. **Hang the lifting rope over the head** — over a greased crossbar resting in the crotch of the poles, or through your pulley.
6. **For heavy loads, rig two-to-one:** tie one end of the rope to the tripod head, run it down and through a strong loop or hook on the load, back up over the head, and down to your hands. Now two strands hold the load and each carries half.
7. **Pull smooth and steady**, using your body weight, not just your arms. Hand over hand, no jerking.
8. **Before you touch the load, tie it off:** wrap the rope twice around a tripod leg — the wraps grip by friction — then finish with two half hitches. Only then put your hands near the load.

## Watch out

- **Never stand, reach, or crawl under a hanging load.** Tie off first, then work — and even then keep your body out from underneath.
- **Sliding feet drop everything.** Hobble or stake them every time, even for "just a quick lift."
- **Rope wears through where it rubs.** Pad rub points with cloth or bark, grease them, and inspect between lifts.
- **Lifting a load that hangs outside the triangle of the feet tips the tripod over.** Lift straight up, and swing the load only within the feet.
- **Jerking the rope can double or triple the force in it** for an instant — enough to snap a rope that holds fine under steady pull. Smooth pulls only.
- **Pulling a log from a river:** don't fight the mud head-on. Stand the tripod on the bank, lift the log's near end first to break the suction, then drag. Half the battle is just lifting the nose.

## How it works

When two strands of rope hold a load, each strand carries half the weight — so your hands, pulling on one strand, supply only half. But to raise the load one metre you must pull in two metres of rope. No force is created or destroyed; it is exchanged for distance. A pulley wheel matters because rolling wastes far less effort than scraping — the same reason wheels beat dragging.
