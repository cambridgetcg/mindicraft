Cord is only as useful as the knot on its end. A bad knot slips at the worst moment or jams so hard you must cut good rope to free it. The good news: five knots cover almost everything — fixing a loop, tying to a post, joining a bundle, stopping an end, and lashing poles into a frame. Learn these five well instead of twenty badly.

Two words first. The **working end** is the end you're moving. The **standing part** is the rest of the rope, the part under load. Every knot below is described with those two words.

## You need

- **A practice cord** about as long as your arm span, pencil-thick. Your own twisted cord works; so does any rope or shoelace.
- **A post or thick stick** to tie onto — a chair leg works.
- **Two straight poles**, wrist-thick, for practicing lashing.
- **Patience for repetition.** Tie each knot ten times, then ten more with your eyes closed. A knot you must think about is a knot you don't own yet.

## Steps

1. **Overhand knot — the stopper.** Make a loop, pass the working end through, pull tight. It stops a rope from sliding through a hole and finishes the end of homemade cord. The knot everyone already knows — now it has a job.
2. **Bowline — the loop that never strangles.** Makes a fixed loop that will not shrink tight around what it holds — good for hanging things, hauling, or a loop to clip and hook onto.
   - Make a small loop in the standing part, with the standing part underneath.
   - Bring the working end up through that small loop, around behind the standing part, and back down through the small loop.
   - The story: the rabbit comes out of the hole, around the tree, back into the hole.
   - Pull all three parts tight. Leave a tail at least a hand-width long.
3. **Two half hitches — tie to anything.** Ties a rope to a post, ring, or branch, and holds under steady pull.
   - Pass the working end around the post.
   - Cross it over the standing part and tuck it through the gap — that's one half hitch.
   - Do the same again in front of the first. Two identical tucks, snugged down.
4. **Square knot (reef knot) — close a bundle.** For tying two ends of the SAME cord around something: a bundle of sticks, a rolled blanket, a bandage.
   - Right end over left and tuck; then left end over right and tuck.
   - Check its face: both ends should exit alongside their own standing part, making two neat interlocked loops. If the ends exit crosswise, you tied a granny knot — it slips. Retie.
   - Hard rule: never use it to join two separate ropes that will carry load. It can roll open. It is a binding knot, not a joining knot.
5. **Clove hitch — the lashing starter.** Two crossing turns around a pole: wrap around, cross over the first wrap, wrap around again, and tuck the end under that crossing. Quick, adjustable, and the standard way to begin a lashing. Alone it can loosen, so use it as a starter, not a finisher.
6. **Square lashing — bind two poles at a right angle.** This turns cord and sticks into furniture, racks, and shelter frames.
   - Start with a clove hitch on the upright pole, just below the crossing.
   - Wrap the cord over the cross pole, behind the upright, over the cross pole on the other side, behind the upright again — around the joint in a square pattern. Make 3–4 firm wraps, laying them side by side, not piled up.
   - Now change direction: make 2–3 tight turns BETWEEN the poles, around the wraps themselves. These are frapping turns — they squeeze the whole bundle like tightening a screw.
   - Finish with two half hitches around a pole. The joint should not wiggle at all.
7. **Test everything.** Load each knot harder than its real job, jerk it, then check it hasn't slipped. Do this every time, not just while learning.

## Watch out

- **A granny knot looks almost right and fails.** Always check the square knot's face before trusting it. Symmetric = square. Crosswise = retie.
- **Short tails walk out.** Under repeated jerks, a stubby working end creeps back through the knot. Leave a hand-width of tail on everything.
- **Wet or slick cord slips more and jams more.** Add an extra half hitch on slippery cord, and don't over-tighten knots you'll need to untie.
- **Loose wraps make a wobbly lashing.** Every single turn must be pulled tight as you lay it; you cannot tighten a sloppy lashing afterward. The frapping turns are not optional.
- **Never put a loop that can tighten around a person or animal.** Use the bowline — its loop stays the size you made it.
- **Don't trust one knot for a life.** Anything holding weight over a person gets backed up: an extra hitch, a second cord.

## How it works

A knot holds by friction: every bend forces the rope to press on itself, and pressure plus contact area grips the load. That's also why every knot weakens rope — sharp bends concentrate stress, so a knotted rope holds roughly half to two-thirds of what the plain rope would. Good knots put the sharp bends where the load pins them tighter; bad knots put them where the load levers them open.
