A basket is the first container. With one you can carry twice the food, gather nuts and berries with both hands free, and store things off the ground. It needs no glue, no nails, no knots — just bendy sticks pressed against each other. People wove baskets long before they made pots, and the skill still works exactly the same today.

The whole craft is one move, repeated: a thin rod goes in front of one upright stick, behind the next, in front, behind, all the way around. Everything else is setup.

## You need

- **30 to 40 long thin shoots** — willow is best; hazel, dogwood, or bramble (thorns stripped) also work. Look for shoots that grew in one year: straight, no side branches, about pencil-thick, at least arm-long. Test one: it should bend around your wrist without snapping.
- **6 thicker rods** — thumb-thick and about a forearm long, for the base cross that everything else builds on.
- **A knife or a sharp stone flake** — to cut and trim ends.
- **Water** — fresh-cut ("green") shoots bend right away. Shoots cut more than a day or two ago are stiff; soak them fully underwater for a day or more first.

## Steps

1. **Cut your rods.** Cut each shoot in one clean stroke near the ground and strip off all leaves.
2. **Make the base cross.** Cut 6 thick rods, each about a forearm long. With your knife, split 3 of them in the middle — just a short slit. Thread the other 3 through the slits so you have a cross, 3 rods on top of 3.
3. **Bind the cross.** Take 2 of your thinnest, longest shoots. Weave them as a twisting pair around the four arms of the cross — one shoot in front, one behind, swapping over between arms. Go around twice.
4. **Open the cross into a sun.** Now spread the 12 rod-ends out evenly, like sun rays, and keep weaving the pair over and under single rods. Weave flat until the round base is as wide as you want the basket bottom — two hand-spans is a good size.
5. **Add the uprights.** Sharpen the thick end of each long rod and push one deep into the weave alongside each base rod. Bend each new rod straight up. You need an **odd** number of uprights — if you count an even number, push in one more anywhere. Tie all the tips loosely together at the top, like a tepee, so they stay put.
6. **Weave the sides.** Take one thin shoot. Go in front of one upright, behind the next, in front, behind, around and around. When a shoot runs out, tuck its end inside the basket and start a new one, overlapping the last few uprights.
7. **Pack every row down.** After each round, press the weave firmly down with your fingers. Tight rows make a strong basket.
8. **Finish the rim.** When the sides are tall enough, cut the tie at the top. Bend each upright down and to the right: in front of the next two uprights, behind the one after, then tuck the end into the weave. Do this all the way around.
9. **Trim and dry.** Cut off every poking end and let the basket dry in the shade for a few days.

## Watch out

- **Rods that snap** are too dry. Don't fight them — soak them a full day, or cut fresh ones.
- **An even number of uprights** breaks the pattern: the weave lands on the same side every round and nothing locks. Always odd.
- **Loose weaving** makes a floppy basket that spills. Pack every row down hard before starting the next.
- **Green rods shrink as they dry**, so a snug new basket loosens a little. Weave tighter than feels necessary.
- **Cut ends are sharp** and sit at eye height while you work. Trim points as you go, not just at the end.

## How it works

A basket holds together by friction. Every rod is bent, and a bent rod pushes back like a spring — so each one presses hard against its neighbours in hundreds of places at once. No single crossing is strong, but all of them together grip like a fist.
