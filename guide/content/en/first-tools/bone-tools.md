---
title: Make needles and hooks from bone
summary: Split and grind bones into needles, fish hooks, and hole-pokers that stone cannot match.
difficulty: steady
time: a day
needs: [stone-tools]
---

Stone gives you edges, but stone cannot be thin, springy, and smooth at the same time. Bone can. A bone needle lets you sew clothes that actually keep you warm. A bone hook catches fish while you do something else. A bone awl — a strong spike for poking holes — opens leather and bark so cord can pass through. These small tools change daily life more than any axe.

Every large animal you eat carries a toolkit inside it. Fresh bone works best: it is slightly springy and grinds cleanly. Old, sun-bleached bone is dry and shatters.

## You need

- **Fresh long bones.** Leg bones of deer, sheep, goat, or cattle. Fresh or recently cooked, not weathered white ones from the ground. Simmer the meat off; don't burn it — burnt bone turns chalky and weak.
- **Stone tools.** A sharp flake for scoring (scratching deep lines) and a heavier stone for cracking. This is why stone tools come first.
- **A grinding stone.** A flat piece of sandstone or any rough, gritty rock. Wet it — a splash of water makes grinding faster and keeps the dust down.
- **A pointed stone flake or thorn** for drilling the needle's eye.
- **Water nearby**, for wet-grinding and rinsing.

## Steps

1. **Get blanks by groove-and-snap.** Lay a long bone on the ground. With the sharp stone flake, scratch a straight groove along its length, going over the same line dozens of times until the groove is deep — a third of the way through the bone wall, roughly the depth of a grain of rice. Score a second groove parallel to it, a finger-width away. Pry or tap the strip out. You now have a long splinter — a "blank."
   - Quick-and-dirty alternative: smash the bone with a rock and pick usable splinters from the wreck. Faster, but you waste most of the bone.
2. **Make an awl (hole-poker).** Take a sturdy splinter as long as your finger. Grind one end on the wet stone, rolling it as you go, until it tapers to a strong point. Smooth the rest so it doesn't cut your hand. Done — this alone is worth the day.
3. **Make a needle.** Take a thin blank about as long as your little finger and as wide as two matchsticks.
   - Grind it flat and even on the wet stone, thinning it until it flexes slightly.
   - Grind one end to a point.
   - For the eye: at the blunt end, drill from BOTH sides with the pointed flake, twirling it like a tiny drill, meeting in the middle. Drilling from one side only splits the bone.
   - Polish the whole needle on fine stone, then on leather or your palm, until thread slides over it with no snags.
4. **Make a gorge hook — the easy fish hook.** Grind a short splinter (2–4 cm) to a sharp point at BOTH ends, with a shallow groove around its middle. Tie your line to the groove, bury the gorge in bait, and let it lie along the line. The fish swallows it; when the line pulls tight, the gorge turns sideways in its throat and holds.
5. **Make a curved hook (optional, slower).** Score and snip a flat bone piece into a rough J shape, then patiently grind the inside and outside of the curve with the edge of your grinding stone until it is a slim hook. Cut a small notch at the top for the line.
6. **Test everything.** Push the awl through scrap leather. Flex the needle — it should bend a little and spring back. Yank the hook against its line hard. Better to break it now than on the day it matters.

## Watch out

- **Bone dust is bad to breathe.** Always grind wet. Never dry-grind near your face.
- **Old dry bone shatters into needles of its own.** If a bone is bleached, cracked, or feather-light, leave it. Use fresh bone.
- **Boiling too long makes bone soft and punky.** Simmer just enough to clean it — under an hour is plenty.
- **Rushing the eye splits the needle.** Hours of grinding die in one impatient push. Drill gently, from both sides, and support the bone flat on wood while you drill.
- **Points snap when you pry with them.** An awl pokes straight in and straight out. Twist or lever with it and you'll be grinding a new one.
- **Cuts from raw bone can get infected.** Wash any nick with clean water right away, and get real medical care if it reddens or swells.

## How it works

Bone is a natural composite: hard mineral (the same family as chalk) wound through with tough protein fibers, like gravel in glue. The mineral takes a point and holds a polish; the fibers stop cracks and give spring. That is why bone can be ground paper-thin and still survive being pushed through leather a thousand times — and why, once the protein dries out or burns away, the same bone crumbles.
