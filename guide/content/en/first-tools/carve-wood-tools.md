Stone cuts; wood does everything else. A digging stick gets you roots and tubers you cannot claw out by hand — it can double what you eat in a day. A mallet saves your palm from a thousand blows. A spoon keeps hot food out of your fingers. Pegs pin down shelters and stretch hides. All four come from branches and your stone edge.

Carve **green wood** — wood cut fresh from a living tree. It cuts like hard cheese. Dead dry wood fights every stroke and blunts your edge fast.

## You need

- **A sharp stone edge and a few flakes** — from "Make sharp tools from stone". The big edge does the shaping; small flakes do the fine scraping.
- **Green hardwood branches** — oak, ash, hazel, or beech. Hardwood feels heavy for its size, and your thumbnail barely dents it. Avoid dead standing wood: if it crumbles or feels light and corky, it is rotten.
- **A fire burnt down to glowing coals** — for hardening tips and hollowing the spoon.
- **A stump or flat log** — a solid surface to carve against, so the work is not in your lap.

## Steps

1. **Set three carving rules first — they apply to every step below.**
    - Cut **away** from your body and away from the hand holding the wood.
    - Brace the work against the stump, not your leg.
    - Take many small shavings, never one big forced cut.
2. **Digging stick.** Cut a straight branch about as long as your arm plus half again (around 1 metre), and thick as three fingers. Scrape off the bark.
    - Carve one end into a **chisel tip** — a flat wedge like a screwdriver, not a needle point. A needle point snaps in the first stony ground.
    - **Fire-harden the tip**: hold it near glowing coals, turning it slowly, for 10–15 minutes, until the wood turns toasted brown. Brown, never black.
3. **Mallet.** Find a branch with a heavy knot or root swelling at one end, or cut a forearm-long piece of thick branch with a fist-sized head. Carve the thinner end down until your hand wraps comfortably around it. That's it — a mallet is deliberately simple.
4. **Spoon.** Split a wrist-thick green branch down the middle with a flake and your new mallet. On the flat face, scratch a spoon outline: round bowl, straight handle.
    - Carve the outside shape first, while the piece is still strong.
    - Hollow the bowl with a coal: set one glowing ember on the bowl spot, blow on it gently (a hollow reed helps aim your breath), then scrape out the charred wood with a flake. Burn, scrape, repeat until the bowl is deep enough.
    - Smooth everything by scraping with a fresh flake edge, then rub with sand.
5. **Pegs.** Split straight-grained wood into finger-thick sticks, each a hand-span long. Point one end, leave the other flat so the mallet can strike it. Make ten — you will use them all: tent stakes, hide stretchers, hooks driven into a log.
6. **Dry your tools slowly.** Leave finished pieces in the shade for a few days. Green wood dried fast in sun or by the fire splits open.

## Watch out

- **Cutting toward your thumb** is how most carving wounds happen — usually on small finishing cuts, when you relax. Away from the body, every stroke, even tiny ones.
- **Black char means brittle.** If a fire-hardened tip or spoon bowl turns black and cracked, you held it in flame instead of near coals. Scrape the black off and go slower.
- **Burning through the spoon bowl.** Near the edges and bottom, scrape often and use smaller embers. Wood keeps burning after the ember comes off — wet your finger and press the spot to stop it.
- **Rotten wood shatters.** A mallet made from punky dead wood can burst on the first hard strike. Thumbnail test before you carve.
- **A needle-pointed digging stick** snaps and leaves you carving again tomorrow. Chisel tip, always.

## How it works

Fire-hardening works by drying, not by magic: heat drives the water out of the tip, and dry wood is far stiffer than wet wood. The gentle toasting also slightly toughens the surface. Go past brown into black and you have made charcoal — crumbly, not tough — which is why colour is your guide.
