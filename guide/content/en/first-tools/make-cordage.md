String holds the world together. Without cord you cannot tie a shelter frame, hang food out of reach, string a bow, set a fishing line, or sew anything. And string is hiding in plants everywhere: long, tough fibers that only need to be freed and twisted together. One twisted cord is many times stronger than its loose fibers, the same way a braid of hair is stronger than single hairs.

The method below — the reverse wrap — is the one trick to learn. It takes ten minutes to understand and an hour of rolling to get smooth. After that, you can make cord anywhere on Earth.

## You need

- **Fiber plants.** Any of these works:
  - **Inner bark (bast)** of willow, basswood/linden, or cedar — the soft, stringy layer between the rough outer bark and the wood. Peels off dead branches in long strips, especially after soaking.
  - **Stinging nettle stalks** — tall plants with jagged leaves that sting bare skin. The stalk fibers are excellent. Handle with gloves or folded leaves until crushed; crushing kills the sting.
  - **Dry grass, sedge, or cattail leaves** — weaker, but everywhere. Good for quick lashings.
- **Water**, to soak stiff fibers until they bend without cracking. Slightly damp fibers twist best; soaking wet ones shrink loose later.
- **Your hands and your thigh.** That is the whole toolkit.

## Steps

1. **Gather fiber.** Strip inner bark into ribbons, or crush nettle stalks flat with a stick, snap them, and peel the fiber skin away from the woody core. Longer fibers make stronger cord — aim for pieces at least as long as your forearm.
2. **Clean and soften.** Pull off woody bits. Rub and roll the fibers between your palms until they feel like rough hair, not like straw. Dampen stiff fiber; let soaked fiber dry to just-damp.
3. **Make a bundle and find its middle — off-center.** Take a pencil-thick bundle of fibers. Grip it about one-third of the way along, not at the exact middle, so the two ends run out at different points later (this matters in step 6).
4. **Kink it.** Twist the bundle at that grip point until it kinks and folds back on itself, making a small loop. Now you hold two strands, a longer one and a shorter one, joined at the loop.
5. **Do the reverse wrap.** This is the whole secret:
   - Twist the strand farther from you AWAY from you (roll it between finger and thumb, or along your thigh).
   - Then wrap that tightened strand over the other one, TOWARD you.
   - The strands swap places. Repeat: twist the far one away, wrap it toward you. Away, over, away, over.
   - The two twists fight each other, and that fight locks the cord so it cannot unravel.
6. **Splice in new fiber before you run out.** When either strand thins to about half its thickness — with a few finger-widths still left — lay a new bundle of fiber alongside it, overlapping by a hand-length, and keep twisting the two as one. Because your strands end at different points (step 3), the joins never land in the same spot.
7. **Finish the end.** Tie a simple overhand knot in the last bit, or wrap and tuck the tails back under the twist.
8. **Test it, then make it thicker if needed.** Pull hard between both hands. It should hold your full pull. For rope, take two or three finished cords and reverse-wrap THEM together the same way.

## Watch out

- **Loose twist is fake cord.** If it looks fluffy, it will slip apart under load. Twist each strand until it is hard and round before wrapping.
- **All joins in one place = one weak spot.** Stagger your splices. If both strands were spliced at the same point, that point WILL break.
- **Bone-dry fiber snaps; soaked fiber shrinks loose.** Work fibers just damp. If cord was made wet, expect it to loosen as it dries — retwist or make it dry.
- **Nettles sting until crushed.** Grip firmly through cloth or leaves; a timid brush stings worse than a firm grab. The sting is painful but passes.
- **Never trust untested cord with anything that matters.** Test every length harder than the job will. A snapped bowstring or shelter lashing at the wrong moment is dangerous.
- **Grass cord rots fast when wet.** Fine for a day's lashing; use bark or nettle for anything that must last.

## How it works

A single plant fiber is strong along its length but short. Twisting presses many short fibers against each other so friction carries the pull from fiber to fiber, making a strand as long as you like. The reverse wrap then twists two strands in one direction while wrapping them around each other in the other; each strand's urge to untwist tightens the wrap instead of undoing it. The cord is held shut by its own tension.
