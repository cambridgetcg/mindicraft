A sharp edge is the first tool. With it you can cut meat, shave wood, scrape hides, and make every other tool. The oldest trick humans know is this: some rocks break like glass, and broken glass is sharp. Your job is to find one of those rocks and break it on purpose, in a controlled way. This is called knapping — shaping stone by striking flakes off it.

You do not need skill to get a useful edge on day one. A single lucky flake can be sharper than a steel knife. Skill only makes the flakes come off where you want them.

## You need

- **A rock that breaks like glass.** Look for flint, chert, obsidian (volcanic glass), or fine quartzite. Test: tap it against another rock. Good stone gives a high ring, almost like porcelain, not a dull clunk. Its broken surfaces look smooth and slightly shiny, with ripple marks like a clamshell.
- **A hammerstone.** A hard, rounded river cobble that fits your fist, about the weight of a full water bottle. Rounded, not sharp — sharp hammers shatter.
- **A pad for your leg.** A folded hide, thick bark, or several layers of cloth. You will hold work on your thigh, and flakes cut skin easily.
- **Eye protection if you have any.** Glasses, goggles, anything. If you have none, turn your face slightly away on every strike.
- **A clear patch of ground.** Flakes fly a meter or more and stay sharp for thousands of years. Work where bare feet will never walk, and clean up after.

## Steps

1. **Find your stone.** Check riverbeds, gravel bars, cliff bases, and plowed ground. Break a candidate open with the hammerstone: you want a smooth, glassy break, not a crumbly or grainy one.
2. **Sit down and set up.** Rest the stone (the "core") on the pad on your thigh, held firmly with your free hand, fingers below the strike line — never in front of it.
3. **Pick a striking spot near an edge.** Aim about a finger-width (1–2 cm) in from an edge where the surface meets the side at less than a right angle — a corner that overhangs slightly. Flakes will not come off a blunt, rounded face.
4. **Strike through, at an angle.** Swing the hammerstone so it hits that spot at roughly 60–80 degrees to the surface — a glancing blow, not straight down. Follow through, like chopping. A crisp click and a flake pops off the underside.
5. **Check what you got.** The flake itself is your knife — its thin edge is the sharp part. The core now has a fresh scar; each scar makes a new corner to strike next to. Keep going around the core, taking flakes.
6. **Sort your flakes into tools.**
   - Long thin flakes: knives for cutting meat, cord, and skin.
   - Thick flakes with a steep edge (60–70 degrees): scrapers for hides and wood — they scrape without digging in.
   - The battered core itself: a chopper for rough work.
7. **Sharpen and touch up.** Press or tap tiny flakes off a dulled edge with a pointed stone or the tip of an antler or hard stick. Small, steady pressure at the very edge — this is called retouch.
8. **Clean the site.** Sweep every flake into one pile and bury it or dump it somewhere no one walks.

## Watch out

- **Flying chips hit eyes.** This is the real injury of knapping. Wear anything over your eyes, angle your face away, and never let someone stand across from you.
- **Flakes cut like razors.** Handle them by their thick backs. Expect small cuts anyway; keep them clean.
- **Do not knap into your palm.** Hold the core on the padded thigh, never cupped in your bare hand under the strike.
- **Stone dust is bad to breathe.** Work outdoors, and don't grind stone dry near your face for long. Wet the stone if you must grind it.
- **Dull clunky rock wastes your day.** If it crumbles or breaks with a grainy face, drop it and find better stone. No skill fixes bad rock.
- **Hitting straight down does nothing.** If flakes won't come off, your angle is too steep or your spot is too far from the edge. Move closer to a corner and glance the blow.

## How it works

Glassy stone has no grain, so a sharp blow sends a shock wave through it that peels off a curved flake — the same way a BB cracks a cone out of a window. Because the break follows the shock wave and not any internal weakness, you can steer it: where you hit and at what angle decides the flake. The fresh break ends in an edge only a few molecules thick — sharper than most metal blades, just more fragile.
