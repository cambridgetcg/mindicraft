A stone held in your fist chops slowly and bruises your hand. The same stone on an arm-length handle swings three or four times faster and hits many times harder. Putting a handle on a tool is called **hafting**, and it was one of the great upgrades in human history — the moment tools stopped being rocks and started being machines.

The catch: the joint takes every shock of every blow. A lazy haft sends the stone head flying on the tenth swing. So this whole guide is really about one thing — making a joint that cannot let go.

## You need

- **A stone axe head** — from "Make sharp tools from stone". Palm-sized, one sharp edge, the back end blunt and chunky. If you can, grind or peck a shallow groove around its middle; the lashing will sit in it.
- **A handle** — tough, springy hardwood: ash, hickory, oak, or hazel. Green and straight, about arm length (50–60 cm), a bit thicker than your thumb. Avoid brittle wood like dead pine or willow — it shatters mid-swing.
- **Strong cordage** — and the square lashing from "Tie knots and lashings that hold". Wet rawhide (untanned hide strip) is even better than plant cord, because it shrinks iron-tight as it dries.
- **Two small wedges** — thin slips of hard, dry wood.
- **Pine pitch glue (optional)** — pine resin melted with crushed charcoal, roughly 3 parts resin to 1 part charcoal.

## Steps

1. **Shape the handle.** Scrape off the bark. Round the grip end so it fills your hand. Leave the head end full thickness.
2. **Lash below before you split.** Wrap a tight band of cordage around the handle, one hand-span down from the top, and finish it off firmly. This band is a brake: it stops the split you are about to make from running down the whole handle.
3. **Split the top.** Drive your stone edge into the end grain and open a split down to the lashed band — about 10–15 cm deep.
4. **Seat the head.** Push the stone head into the split until it sits snug, sharp edge lined up exactly with the direction of the swing. A crooked edge makes glancing blows.
5. **Glue if you have it.** Warm the pine pitch gently beside the fire until it flows like honey, and pack it into every gap around the stone. Let it cool firm before lashing.
6. **Lash the joint.** With wet cord or rawhide, lash in an X pattern over the wood above and below the stone, then add tight straight wraps, pulling every turn as hard as you can. Finish with the locked-off ending from the knots guide. The lashing squeezes the split shut around the stone.
7. **Wedge it.** Tap a thin hard wedge into the split beside the stone, from the top, until the head cannot wiggle at all. Trim the wedge flush.
8. **Test in stages.** Tap the head on a log. Then light chops into soft wood. Check the lashing between each stage and retighten. Only then take a full swing — with no one within two arm-lengths, and never with anyone in front of you.

## Watch out

- **A flying head is the real danger.** Before every use — not just the first — grab the head and try hard to twist it. Any movement means stop and re-wedge, re-lash. When you swing, place people behind you, never ahead or beside the arc.
- **A split that runs** past the band ruins the handle. If you forgot the band in step 2, stop and add it before the head goes in.
- **Green wood shrinks.** Your tight new haft will be loose in three or four days as the handle dries. This is normal. Re-wedge and re-lash once, and it will then stay tight.
- **Melted pitch burns skin badly** and catches fire easily. Warm it beside the flames, never in them, and keep it off your fingers — use a stick to pack it.
- **Wrong wood** fails without warning. If the handle came from wood you can snap over your knee, start over with ash, oak, hickory, or hazel.

## How it works

The handle is a lever: the head at the end of a swing moves several times faster than your hand does. The energy of a blow grows with the **square** of speed — double the speed, four times the hit. That is why a hafted axe fells a tree that a hand-held stone can only bruise.
