A hive of bees is a workforce of thirty thousand that harvests every flower within three kilometres and asks only for a dry box. They pay in honey — a food so sugary it never spoils — and beeswax for candles and waterproofing.

This is a partnership: the bees keep the honey they need for winter; you take only the spare.

## You need

- **A top-bar hive** — a wooden trough about 90 cm long, 30 cm wide, and 30 cm deep, with a rain-tight lid and one or two 2 cm entrance holes near one end.
- **Top bars** — loose slats laid side by side to form the ceiling, each 32 to 38 mm wide — bees build one hanging comb under each. Drip melted beeswax along each bar's underside as a building guide.
- **A veil** — fine mesh or thin cloth over a wide hat, tied at the neck. Wear long sleeves, trousers tucked into socks, and light colours — bees attack dark, furry things.
- **A smoker** — a tin of smouldering rotten wood or a bundle of dried leaves. Cool white smoke calms bees; flames enrage them.
- **A hive tool** — a thin blade of hard wood or metal to pry bars loose — bees glue everything shut with propolis, a sticky resin cement.
- **A swarm of bees** — free, from the wild, in late spring.

## Steps

1. **Build the hive before swarm season** (winter or early spring). Bees keep 6 to 9 mm walkways between combs — that is why bar width matters.
2. **Choose the spot:** morning sun, shelter from wind, dry ground, entrance away from paths. Set a shallow dish of water with pebbles nearby so bees do not drown.
3. **Bait the box.** Rub old dark comb or melted beeswax inside — scouts hunt for homes that smell of bees.
4. **Catch a swarm.** In late spring strong colonies split: half the bees leave with a queen and cluster on a branch. A swarm is bees at their gentlest — full of honey, no home to defend. Hold a box under the cluster, give the branch one sharp shake, and if the queen fell in, the rest march in within an hour.
5. **House them at dusk.** Pour the swarm into the hive, lay the bars back on, close the lid, and leave them alone for a week.
6. **Inspect every week or two, gently,** on a warm midday when many bees are out. Two or three puffs of smoke at the entrance, wait a minute, then lift bars slowly, straight up. Look for straight comb and brood — white grubs curled in cells — proof the queen is alive. Press crooked new comb gently straight while small and soft.
7. **Harvest in late summer — surplus only.** Take only capped comb — cells sealed with white wax, meaning ripe honey — from the end farthest from the brood, and leave the colony at least 10 to 15 kg for winter. A first-year colony often has no surplus; taking nothing that year is normal.
8. **Crush and strain.** Cut the comb from the bar, crush it in a bowl, and let it drip through cloth overnight. Jar the honey — sealed and dry, it keeps indefinitely. Wash the crushed wax and melt it in hot water; it sets as a clean floating cake.

## Watch out

- **Stings.** Scrape a sting out sideways with a fingernail at once — the torn-off sac keeps pumping venom. A few stings mean pain and swelling, nothing more. But a swelling mouth or throat, dizziness, or trouble breathing is a severe allergy — it can kill within minutes. Get real medical care immediately.
- **Never give honey to a baby under one year old.** It can carry spores harmless to adults but dangerous to infants.
- **Never open the hive in cold, rain, wind, or evening** — every forager is home and defensive. Warm midday only.
- **Taking too much honey kills slowly** — the colony starves quietly in midwinter. When unsure, leave more.
- **Open honey starts wars.** In late summer it draws robber bees and wasps who then attack the hive — harvest fast, cover everything, narrow the entrance.
- **Never feed a colony honey from elsewhere** — that is how bee diseases spread. If they starve in a flowerless gap, feed syrup of equal parts sugar and warm water.

## How it works

Smoke makes bees act as if a forest fire is coming: they gorge on honey, and a full bee is a calm bee; it also masks their alarm scent, so panic cannot spread. Honey never spoils because bees fan the nectar to less than one-fifth water — too dry and sugary for any microbe.
