Almost all animal sickness is prevented, not cured. The cure kit is boring: clean water, dry bedding, space, and one honest look at every animal every day. Spotted on day one, sickness is a small problem in one pen; on day five it may be the whole flock.

The other half is getting the next generation born alive. Most births need only a quiet watch; the few that go wrong need you within the hour. Telling them apart is the skill.

## You need

- **Clean water, every day** — dirty water is the fastest road to a sick flock.
- **Dry bedding** — straw, dried grass, or dry leaves. Damp, not cold, is the great killer of penned animals.
- **A sick pen** — a separate space out of touch and sneezing range of the flock, with its own dishes.
- **Soap, or ash and water** — for your hands after every handling.
- **Clean cloths** — ready for birth season.

## Steps

1. **Do the look-over at feeding time, daily.** They all come to food, so you see them all. Count them. The one that hangs back is your first warning.
2. **Know what well looks like:** bright eyes, smooth coat or sleek feathers, steady walk, eating, drinking, normal droppings. Learn their normal droppings — a change there is often the earliest sign.
3. **Keep water clean and bedding dry.** Scrub water containers when they turn slimy. Push a hand deep into the bedding — if it feels wet, or the pen smells sharply of pee (ammonia — it scars lungs), muck out and lay fresh dry material.
4. **Give space and air.** Crowding spreads disease and starts fights; air should move above their heads, never as a draft where they sleep.
5. **Quarantine every new animal** in the sick pen for two to four weeks before it joins — new animals carry invisible diseases. Handle it last each day and wash after.
6. **Isolate at the first sign of sickness — the same day.** Hunched, dull, off its food, apart from the group: into the sick pen with warmth, dry bedding, quiet, and water in reach. Many recover with only that. Watch the rest twice a day.
7. **Fight the small enemies.** Part feathers or fur and look for crawling specks — mites and lice; give birds a dust bath of dry earth and ash. Against gut worms, move grazers to fresh ground often — eggs pass in droppings and reinfect the same soil.
8. **At a birth, watch — don't touch.** A mother near her time grows restless and goes apart; keep your distance. A hatching chick can take a full day from first crack to free and must do it alone — "helping" it out of the shell usually kills it. A mammal mother, once hard straining begins, should deliver within about an hour.
9. **Step in only when a mammal birth stalls:** hard straining over an hour with nothing appearing, or something wrong-way — one leg only, or tail first. With washed hands and short nails, feel gently, straighten the legs so the nose and both front feet come like a diver, and pull gently downward only while she strains. If a few careful minutes change nothing, fetch the most experienced help you can — real veterinary care whenever it exists.
10. **Secure the first milk.** The first milk — colostrum, thick and yellowish — carries the mother's defenses; a newborn must drink it within a few hours or it will likely die. See every newborn stand and suckle; if one cannot, milk the mother and spoon-feed it slowly. Keep newborns dry and warm — rub them dry if the mother does not.

## Watch out

- **Animal sickness can pass to people.** Wash your hands after every handling and always before eating. Anyone pregnant must stay away from birthing sheep and goats — infections there can harm an unborn baby.
- **Don't rush a birth** — pulling too early or too hard injures mother and young. Act on the clock, not your nerves.
- **An animal found dead with no clear cause is not food.** Bury it deep, away from water, wash well, and watch the rest closely for days.
- **"It's just one quiet bird" is how every outbreak starts.** Isolate first, wonder later.
- These habits prevent trouble and buy time; they do not replace a vet. **When real care exists, use it.**

## How it works

Animals are prey, and prey hides weakness — an animal that looks sick has usually been sick for days, which is why the look-over watches who hangs back, not symptoms. Most flock disease travels through droppings, damp, and crowding; dry bedding, clean water, space, and fresh ground break that cycle before medicine is needed.
