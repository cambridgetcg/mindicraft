A fence is a wall made of posts and gaps. It does two jobs at once: it keeps your animals where the food and safety are, and it keeps foxes, dogs, and neighbours' goats out of your garden. Every kind of animal-keeping starts here. A goat with no fence is not your goat — it is the forest's goat.

The good news: a fence does not need nails or metal. Posts set deep in the ground, with rails lashed on or thin branches woven between them, will hold for years.

## You need

- **Posts** — straight lengths of wood, about as thick as your arm (8–12 cm) and 1.8–2 m long. Hardwood lasts longest; chestnut, oak, and cedar resist rot. One post every 2 to 2.5 m of fence.
- **Rails or weaving rods** — for a rail fence, poles 3–4 m long. For a woven (wattle) fence, bendy fresh-cut rods thumb-thick or thinner: willow, hazel, or any young shoots that bend without snapping.
- **A digging tool** — a sharpened stake, a shovel, or a heavy pointed pole to make post holes.
- **Cordage** — rope or strong plant-fibre string for lashing rails to posts (this is why you learned knots).
- **A heavy stone or wooden mallet** — for pounding posts firm.
- **A helper, if you can get one** — one person holds the post straight while the other pounds.

## Steps

1. **Walk the line first.** Mark where the fence will go with sticks or a scratched line. Avoid soggy ground — posts rot fast in standing water.
2. **Decide the height for your animal.** Chickens: 1.2 m and they mostly stay (clip one wing's long feathers if they fly over). Goats: at least 1.2 m, better 1.4 m — goats climb and jump. Sheep: 1 m is usually enough.
3. **Dig or pound the post holes.** The rule: **one third of the post goes underground.** For a 1.8 m post, that is about 60 cm deep. A shallow post is a fence that falls over in the first storm.
4. **Set each post.**
   - Sharpen the bottom end to a point so it drives in easier and sheds water.
   - If you have fire, char the buried end in flames until black — charred wood rots much slower.
   - Drop it in, hold it straight up (check by eye from two sides), and pack the hole with soil and small stones, pounding every handful firm.
5. **Test each post** by shoving it hard. If it wobbles, pack more stones around it now, not later.
6. **Fill the gaps between posts.** Pick one way:
   - **Rails:** lash 3–4 horizontal poles across the posts with tight lashings. Lowest rail no more than 15 cm off the ground, or small animals slip under.
   - **Wattle (weaving):** weave bendy rods in and out of the posts like a basket, alternating which side each row passes. Push each row down snug. This makes a solid wall goats cannot poke their heads through.
7. **Make a gate.** The simplest gate is a light panel of lashed poles that lifts out, or a section hinged with loops of rope on one post and tied shut on the other. Tie it with a knot animals cannot nose open.
8. **Walk the whole line as if you were the animal.** Crouch to goat height. Push, lean, look for gaps wider than your fist. Fix them before any animal moves in.

## Watch out

- **Wobbly posts** — the most common failure. Cause: shallow holes or loose packing. Fix: dig deeper, pack with stones, pound hard. There is no shortcut.
- **Rot at the ground line.** Posts break where air meets soil. Char the buried end, use rot-resistant wood, and expect to replace a few posts every couple of years anyway.
- **Goats.** They lean, climb, rub, and test every fence daily. If a goat can get its head through a gap, its body follows or it gets stuck and panics. Keep gaps narrower than a goat's head.
- **Diggers.** Foxes and dogs dig under fences. Where predators are a worry, bury the bottom of the fence 20–30 cm deep, or lay flat stones along the outside base.
- **Building too big too fast.** A small, strong pen beats a long, weak fence. Start with a pen you can finish well in a week; extend later.

## How it works

A fence's strength is almost all in the posts, and a post's strength is almost all underground. The soil grips the buried third and turns every push into a lever fight the animal loses. Weaving and lashing then spread any shove across many posts at once, so no single point takes the whole blow.
