Milk is food that renews itself every day. A good dairy goat gives 2 to 4 litres a day at her peak — drink it fresh, or turn it into yogurt and cheese that keep longer. The hand-work is simple — a week of clumsy mornings to learn. What makes milk safe or dangerous is not the squeezing: it is cleanliness.

An animal only gives milk after giving birth, and the flow tapers off over roughly 8 to 10 months. While her baby is young, you share — the kid drinks, you take what is spare; after weaning the full yield is yours if you keep milking daily.

## You need

- **A milking female** — a goat or sheep who has recently given birth. Her udder (the milk bag between her hind legs) should be soft and full, not hot, hard, or lumpy.
- **Clean hands and short fingernails** — scrubbed with soap right before milking, every time. Long nails hurt her.
- **A clean pot with a wide mouth** — scrubbed, then scalded with boiling water, and dried upside down. A second small cup for the first squirts.
- **A milking stand or a wall hook** — a set spot to tie her short, with a bowl of feed at head height.
- **A cloth and warm water** — to wipe the udder before you start.
- **A cool, covered place for the milk** — the coldest spot you have: a cellar, a stream box, a shaded pot of water.

## Steps

1. **Milk at the same two times every day**, about 12 hours apart — say sunrise and sunset. Keep the schedule and she keeps making milk; skip days and the supply dries up.
2. **Bring her to the same spot, tie her short, and give her feed.** Calm is everything. Talk quietly, touch her flank first, then the udder.
3. **Wash your hands with soap. Then wipe the whole udder and teats** with a warm, damp cloth. This cleans off dirt, and warmth tells her body to release milk.
4. **Squirt the first 2–3 squeezes from each teat into the separate cup, not the pot.** This flushes germs from the teat opening. Look at it against something dark: it should be plain white. Clots, strings, blood, or watery yellow mean infection.
5. **Learn the squeeze — this is the whole trick:**
   - Trap the milk first: close your thumb and first finger in a ring around the *top* of the teat.
   - Then close your remaining fingers one after another, top to bottom, pressing the milk down and out.
   - Release fully, let the teat refill, repeat — two hands, two teats, steady rhythm.
   - **Never pull or yank downward** — pulling hurts her and gives no milk.
6. **Milk until the udder is soft and empty**, then massage it gently upward and strip the last squirts. An udder left half-full signals the body to make less tomorrow — and leftover milk invites infection.
7. **Cover the pot the moment you finish** and get the milk cold fast. Warm milk spoils in hours; cold milk keeps a day or two.
8. **If you cannot keep it cold, make it keep itself:** bring it just to a boil once (this kills germs — the home version of pasteurising), or turn it same-day into yogurt or cheese.

## Watch out

- **Dirty milk is the real danger.** Raw milk can carry germs that cause serious illness. Be strict: clean hands, clean udder, scalded pot, first squirts discarded, milk chilled fast. The safest habit is to bring milk briefly to a boil before drinking, especially for children, the sick, and anyone pregnant.
- **Mastitis** — an udder infection. Signs: a hot, hard, painful udder, or clumpy, stringy, bloody milk in your test cup. Do not drink or mix in that milk. Milk the infected side out gently and often (it helps her heal), keep her bedding clean and dry, and get experienced or veterinary help when you can.
- **A kicked-over pot.** Tie her short, keep the pot slightly forward of her hind feet, and hold it if she is new to this.
- **Sore, cracked teats** mean your grip is wrong or your nails too long. Go gentler, back to trap-then-squeeze, and rub a little clean fat on the teats after milking.
- **Milk that tastes odd** usually means strong-flavoured feed (wild garlic, certain weeds) or a pot not truly clean.

## How it works

Warmth, routine, and touch trigger a hormone that squeezes the udder's tiny milk chambers so the milk "lets down" into the teat; fear blocks it, which is why calm matters. And the body makes tomorrow's milk by how empty you leave it today — full emptying, twice a day, says "keep it coming."
