A chicken is a small machine that turns scraps, bugs, and weeds into eggs. One healthy hen lays an egg most days in the warm months — call it 200 to 250 eggs a year — so three or four hens feed a small family. They need only a safe box to sleep in, a fenced yard, food, water, and someone to shut the door at night.

The whole craft comes down to one sentence: everything wants to eat a chicken. Your real job is out-thinking the fox.

## You need

- **3 to 6 hens** — young females, ideally already laying (about 5–6 months old). Hens lay without a rooster; you only need one for chicks.
- **A coop** — an enclosed box where they sleep and lay. About 0.3 square metres of floor per bird, so a box 1 m by 1 m suits 3 birds.
- **A fenced run** — an outdoor yard, at least 1 square metre per bird; more is better.
- **A roost bar** — a rounded pole, 4–5 cm thick, fixed 30–60 cm off the coop floor. Chickens sleep gripping a perch, not lying down.
- **Nest boxes** — one dark cubby about 30 cm on each side per 3–4 hens, lined with dry grass or straw.
- **Feed** — kitchen scraps, grain if you have it, plus whatever they scratch up. And **grit**: small stones or coarse sand they swallow to grind food, since chickens have no teeth.
- **Water** — a shallow dish they cannot tip over, refilled daily.

## Steps

1. **Build the coop before you get birds.** Solid walls, a roof that sheds rain, a door you can latch. Cover every opening or make it smaller than a fist — a fox squeezes through a palm-sized gap.
2. **Fox-proof the floor line.** Give the coop a solid floor, or bury the wall edges 30 cm into the ground, or set it up on legs. Foxes dig.
3. **Add ventilation up high.** Small mesh-covered openings near the roof. Damp, stale air kills chickens faster than cold; air should move above their heads, never as a draft across the roost.
4. **Fit the roost and nest boxes.** Set the roost higher than the nests — chickens sleep at the highest point they can reach, and sleeping birds poop where they sit.
5. **Keep new hens shut in the coop and run for the first week.** This teaches them "home"; after that they return by themselves every dusk.
6. **Fall into the daily rhythm — about 15 minutes a day:**
   - Morning: open the coop, put out food and fresh water.
   - Midday or evening: collect eggs. Daily collection keeps eggs clean and stops hens learning to eat them.
   - Dusk: once all birds are inside, shut and latch the door. Count them.
7. **Feed sensibly.** Vegetable peels, stale bread, cooked rice, garden weeds, bugs. A handful of grain per bird per day if you have it. Never feed: raw dry beans, avocado, anything mouldy, or salty food — these can kill a chicken.
8. **Clean the coop every week or two.** Rake out droppings and old bedding, add fresh dry material. Old litter becomes superb garden fertiliser after a few months of rotting.

## Watch out

- **One forgotten night can end the flock.** A fox in an open coop kills every bird, not just one. Shutting the door at dusk is the one unbreakable rule.
- **Rats** come for spilled feed, then for eggs and chicks. Feed only what gets eaten by nightfall and store grain under a hard lid.
- **Eggs stop in winter.** Hens lay by day-length; short days mean few or no eggs. This is normal, not sickness — laying returns as days lengthen.
- **A hen who sits on the nest all day**, puffed up and growling, has gone "broody" — she wants to hatch. Give her fertile eggs and 21 days if you want chicks; otherwise lift her off daily until she quits.
- **Sick bird signs:** hunched, dull, apart from the flock, dirty vent (rear end). Separate her in a small box with food and water and watch the rest closely. Wash your hands after handling any bird.
- **Don't eat cracked eggs.** Store clean eggs unwashed and cool — the natural coating keeps them good for weeks. Wash an egg only just before cooking it.

## How it works

A hen's body releases one yolk roughly every 25 hours when daylight is long and food is steady, and the shell forms around it overnight. That is why eggs come almost daily in summer and slow to a stop in midwinter. Hens seek a dark, safe hollow to lay in — give them one and the eggs arrive in the same spot daily.
