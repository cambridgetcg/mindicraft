---
title: Find and clean clay
summary: Dig sticky earth from stream banks, test it with a squeeze and a roll, and knead out the stones and air.
difficulty: easy
time: half a day
needs: [find-water]
---

Clay is earth so fine that water makes it sticky and bendable, like dough. Shape it wet, bake it in fire, and it turns hard as stone. That one trick gives you pots, bricks, ovens, and later the kilns that make metal possible. Everything in this domain starts with a bucket of good clay.

The good news: clay is common. Rivers grind rock into fine dust and drop it wherever the water slows down. You are looking for that dust, packed into sticky layers.

## You need

- **A digging stick or shovel** — clay sits under the loose topsoil, so you have to dig for it.
- **A basket, pot, or folded bark** — to carry the clay home. A double handful is enough to test; a full bucket makes several pots.
- **Water nearby** — for testing the clay and for cleaning it. This is why "Find water" comes first.
- **Your hands** — the squeeze test and the roll test need nothing else.

## Steps

1. **Look where water slows down.** The best spots:
   - The inside bend of a stream, where the bank is smooth and slick.
   - The bottom and edges of ponds, ditches, and puddles that hold water long after rain — clay is what stops the water from draining.
   - Cut banks, road cuts, and animal burrows, where the layers of earth are already exposed.
2. **Dig past the topsoil.** The dark, crumbly top layer (30 cm or so, sometimes more) is full of roots and rot — not clay. Below it, look for a layer that is smooth, dense, and a different colour: grey, red, brown, or yellow.
3. **Do the squeeze test.** Grab a wet lump and squeeze it in your fist. Good clay holds the shape of your fingers. Sand falls apart. Silt (dust finer than sand but not sticky) crumbles when you poke it.
4. **Do the roll test.** Roll a wet lump between your palms into a snake as thick as a pencil, then bend it around your finger.
   - Bends without cracking: good clay. Take it.
   - Cracks a little: usable, but mix in nothing sandy and knead it well.
   - Breaks apart: too much sand or silt. Dig deeper or try another spot.
5. **Carry home more than you think you need.** Half of raw clay is often stones and trash.
6. **Clean it.** Two ways — pick one:
   - **Fast, by hand:** pick out every stone, root, and shell you can feel. Stones left inside can crack a pot in the fire.
   - **Thorough, with water (called slaking):** dry the clay, crush it to powder, stir it into a pot of water until it is like thin soup, and let it sit. Stones and sand sink in minutes; pour the cloudy clay-water into another container and let it settle for a day. Pour off the clear water on top. What is left is smooth, clean clay. Let it dry until it is like stiff dough.
7. **Knead it (potters say "wedge").** Press and fold the clay on a flat surface like bread dough for a good 10 minutes. Cut the lump in half now and then and look inside: you are hunting air bubbles. Keep going until a cut face shows no holes.
8. **Store it wet.** Wrap the clay in wet leaves, cloth, or bark, and keep it in the shade. It keeps for weeks. Dried-out clay is not ruined — crush it, add water, and start again.

## Watch out

- **Air bubbles are the silent pot-killer.** Trapped air expands in the fire and blows the pot apart. If your kneaded clay still shows holes when cut, knead more.
- **Stones and shells crack pots.** Even a pea-sized stone heats differently from the clay around it. Be fussy when cleaning.
- **Too-sticky clay cracks as it dries.** If your test snake is very stretchy and your dried scraps crack a lot, mix in about one part fine sand to four parts clay. Potters call this added sand "temper" — it lets the clay shrink without tearing.
- **Don't dig where the water is foul.** Clay from a sewage ditch or a stagnant, stinking pond can carry disease. Wash your hands after digging, always, and pick a clean stream bank when you can.
- **Grey slimy mud is not always clay.** If it smells of rot and fails the roll test, it is organic muck. Move on.

## How it works

Clay is rock ground down to flakes far too small to see, each shaped like a tiny plate. Water lets the plates slide over each other — that is the stickiness. When fire drives the water out and gets hot enough, the plates fuse together and can never soak apart again. That is why a fired pot is stone, forever.
