Clay is earth so fine that water makes it sticky and bendable, like dough. Shape it wet, bake it in fire, and it turns hard as stone. That one trick gives you pots, bricks, ovens, and later the kilns that make metal possible. Everything in this domain starts with a bucket of good clay.

The good news: clay is common, for a reason. It is not a special kind of dirt — it is ordinary rock that water and weather have slowly rotted into something new, fine as flour, then carried off by streams. Fine dust only falls where water slows and rests — a stream bend, a pond bottom, an old flood plain — so there it settles into sticky layers. You are not hunting something rare; you are reading the land for places where water has long been slow. And your fingers tell clay from its lookalikes better than your eyes: sand and silt are gritty or floury, clay alone is sticky, and skin feels that difference. This guide teaches your hands the tests.

## You need

- **A digging stick or shovel** — clay sits under the loose topsoil, so you have to dig for it. A fire-hardened stick works.
- **A basket, pot, or folded bark** — to carry the clay home. A double handful tests a spot; a full bucket (roughly 10 kg) makes several pots.
- **Water nearby, and two wide containers** — for testing and cleaning the clay (one to stir in, one to settle in). This is why "Find water" comes first.
- **Your hands** — the squeeze test and the roll test need nothing else.

## Steps

1. **Look where water slows down.** The best spots:
   - The inside bend of a stream, where the current is lazy and the bank smooth and slick — the fast outside drops only gravel, so dig the quiet side.
   - The bottom and edges of ponds, ditches, and puddles that hold water long after rain. Clay is what stops the water draining away — so a puddle that lasts is pointing straight down at the clay beneath it.
   - Cut banks, road cuts, and animal burrows, where the earth's layers lie exposed like a cut cake.
   - Where plants hint at wet feet — rushes, willows, sedges like ground that clay keeps damp.
2. **Dig past the topsoil.** The dark, crumbly top layer — a forearm deep, 30 cm or so — is roots and rot, not clay. Below it, look for a layer that is smooth, dense, and a different colour: grey, red, brown, yellow, even blue-grey. Good clay cuts cleanly, shiny like cheese; sand pours off your shovel, clay clings in a slab.
3. **Do the feel test.** Wet a pinch and rub it between finger and thumb.
   - **Gritty, like tiny stones** — sand. Too coarse.
   - **Silky like flour, but not sticky** — silt: rock dust finer than sand, coarser than clay. Close, but it will not hold a shape.
   - **Sticky and greasy, clinging so you must scrape it off** — clay. Keep going.
4. **Do the squeeze test.** Squeeze a wet, egg-sized lump hard in your fist and open your hand. Good clay holds the print of every finger, sharp-edged. Sand falls apart; silt holds loosely but crumbles when poked.
5. **Do the roll test — the one that decides.** Roll a wet lump between your palms into a pencil-thick snake, then bend it slowly around your finger.
   - **Bends into a ring without cracking:** good clay. Take it.
   - **Bends but cracks on the outside of the curve:** usable. Knead it well and work it a little wetter.
   - **Breaks into short pieces:** too much sand or silt. Dig deeper in the same hole — clay often sits under sandy layers — or try another spot.
   - A snake that stretches like warm toffee means very rich clay: lovely to shape, but it cracks drying. The fix is in "Watch out."
6. **Carry home more than you think you need.** Half of raw clay is often stones, sand, and roots — a full carry may boil down to half.
7. **Clean it.** Two ways — pick one:
   - **Fast, by hand:** work the wet clay in handfuls and pick out every stone, root, shell, and hard lump you can feel. One stone can crack a pot in the fire. Good enough for bricks, hearths, and rough pots.
   - **Thorough, with water — settling the clay clean (potters call it levigation):** dry the dug clay hard, crush it to powder (outside, dust wetted down — see "Watch out"), and stir it into a container of water until it is thin soup. Stir hard, then stop. Stones and sand sink at once — you can hear them tick on the bottom. After a slow count of thirty to sixty, pour the still-cloudy water off into your second container, leaving the grit behind. Now wait hours, or overnight: the cloudiness is the clay itself, and it settles into a smooth bed. Pour off the clear water above. What remains is clay finer than fingers could pick. Spread it on cloth or dry stone until it stiffens from cream to dough — done when it holds a dent without sticking.
8. **Knead it — potters say "wedge."** Press the lump down and away with the heels of your hands, fold it back, quarter-turn, press again — like bread, but with one rule: always press, never fold air in. A steady rhythm, a good 10 minutes for a bucket's worth. Cut the lump in half now and then with a taut string, hunting air bubbles: holes in the cut faces mean keep going. Two clean cuts in a row, the lump one softness all through — done. Wedging also evens the wetness out.
9. **Store it wet — and let it rest if you can.** Wrapped in wet leaves, cloth, or bark and kept in the shade, clay keeps for weeks. Better, it improves: clay rested wet for weeks — potters say "soured" — grows smoother and stretchier, and its faint sour smell is a good sign ("How it works" says why). Dried-out clay is never ruined: crush it, add water, begin again. Clay forgives everything until the fire.

## Watch out

- **Air bubbles are the silent pot-killer.** Every bubble kneaded in is a sealed pocket; in the fire its damp air turns to steam, swells hard, and bursts the pot — sometimes throwing hot shards. It is one of the two most common ways a first pot dies — the other, firing a pot still damp inside, is the "Fire a pot" guide's battle. The save here: wedge until two string-cuts in a row show no holes.
- **Stones and shells crack pots.** Even a pea-sized stone heats and swells differently from the clay around it and tears the pot from inside. Shells are worse: the fire changes them into a new stuff, quicklime, which later drinks in water, swells, and pops flakes off the pot's wall. Be fussy; water-settling catches what fingers miss.
- **Never make a habit of breathing dry clay dust.** Wet clay is harmless. Dry clay dust is fine ground rock, and lungs cannot clean it out — years of it scars them (the old miners' disease, silicosis). One dusty afternoon will not hurt you; the habit will. Crush dry clay outside, stand upwind, sprinkle water as you work, and clean up wet, never by dry sweeping.
- **Too-rich clay cracks as it dries.** A snake that stretches like warm toffee, and dried scraps covered in cracks, both say the same thing: the clay shrinks too much as its water leaves. The save: knead in about one part clean fine sand to four parts clay. Potters call added sand "temper" — grains that stay the same size while the clay shrinks around them, giving it something firm to hold onto so it dries without tearing itself apart. Crushed old fired pottery works even better.
- **Don't dig where the water is foul.** Clay from a sewage ditch or a stinking pond can carry disease into cuts and mouths. Pick a clean bank; wash your hands after digging and before eating.
- **Grey slimy mud is not always clay.** If it smells of rot, stains black inside, and fails the roll test, it is organic muck — dead plants, not rock dust. It shrinks to nothing and stinks in the fire. Move on.
- **Mind the bank.** A cut bank you undermine can slump onto you. Dig from the top or the side, never from under an overhang.

## How it works

Start with what clay actually is. Mountains do not last. Rain, frost, and rivers grind rock down — to gravel, then sand, then silt. But grinding alone never makes clay: grind silt finer and you only get smaller grains of the same old rock — still stiff and dead in the hand, never sticky. Grinding changes a grain's size, never its nature. Clay is born from water's slower trick. Rainwater is faintly acid, and over ages it rots certain minerals in the rock — feldspar above all — and rebuilds their stuff as new crystals: flat ones, so small that ten thousand side by side would barely cross your fingernail. That finest flour does not stay put: water carries it, and only lets it fall where the water slows to a rest. Fast water keeps fine dust dancing; still water lets it sleep. That is the whole secret of finding clay — stream bends, pond bottoms, and flood plains are simply places where water has rested for centuries, laying its finest flour in beds.

Now the secret of clay's feel. Clay grains are not little balls, like sand. They are flat plates — tiny sheets, a deck of playing cards shrunk down past seeing. Wet clay is that deck with a film of water between every pair of cards, and the water acts like oil. Push the deck slowly and the cards slide over one another — then stop, and hold their new position. That slide-and-stay is what potters call plasticity, the whole magic of clay: it moves where your hands say and stays where your hands leave it. Sand cannot do this — next to clay plates, sand grains are boulders, and boulders do not stack and slide, they roll apart. Silt sits between: too fine to feel gritty, too round and coarse to slide-and-stay. That is what your fingers read in the feel test — gritty means boulders, silky means in-between, sticky-and-bendy means plates.

The water-cleaning trick works because falling through water is a race that size decides. Stir everything in and let go: big heavy grains punch straight down in seconds, silt takes minutes, and the flat little clay plates — light, and shaped like falling leaves — hang in the water for hours. So the recipe writes itself: wait a minute and the grit is down; pour off the cloud and you carry away almost pure clay; wait overnight and the cloud itself lies down as a smooth clean bed.

Wedging, in this picture, is quality control on the deck. Every air pocket is a gap holding nothing but a bubble — a steam bomb seed waiting for the fire. Wedging squeezes the gaps shut and shuffles wet cards among dry ones until the deck is evenly oiled.

And souring? Fresh-mixed clay still has plates the water never truly reached. Left wet for weeks, water finally creeps between every last pair of cards, and tiny living things grow a trace of friendly slime that greases the deck a little more. The clay is at last wet all the way through — that is why old clay shapes like a dream.

Fire finishes the story: heat drives the water out, then welds the plates to each other so they can never slide — or soak apart — again. The deck of cards becomes one stone. But that is the next guide's tale.

## Go further

- **Try it today:** do the roll test on any earth near you — garden soil, molehills, puddle bottoms. Ten minutes of rolling snakes teaches your hands more than an hour of reading.
- **Jar test:** shake dug earth in a clear container of water and let it stand a day. It settles in order — sand at the bottom, silt above, clay on top — your ground's recipe at a glance.
- **Start a souring lump:** leave one wrapped lump of wedged clay wet in the shade for a month, then compare it with fresh. You will feel the difference before you can explain it.
- **What this unlocks:** clean clay leads straight to pinch pots and coil pots, then bricks and a hearth — and the "Fire a pot" guide shows how the deck of cards becomes stone.
