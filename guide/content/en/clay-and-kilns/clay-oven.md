A bread oven works backwards from a cooking fire. You don't cook over the flames — you burn a fire *inside* a thick clay dome until the walls themselves are soaking hot, then rake the fire out and bake with the heat stored in the clay. The dome gives that heat back slowly and evenly from every direction at once, which is exactly what bread needs: fierce steady warmth with no flame to burn it. One good firing bakes several rounds of bread, then roasts meat, then dries fruit, as the oven slowly cools through the day.

This is the same oven, in its bones, that has baked bread for thousands of years.

## You need

- **Fired bricks or flat stones** for the base and the oven floor. The floor must take direct fire, so sun-dried mud brick isn't enough there.
- **Cob** — the wall mix from kiln-building: about 2 parts clay-rich earth, 1 part coarse sand, a big handful of chopped straw per bucket, trodden stiff.
- **Damp sand, a few basketfuls**, to shape the dome over — you'll dig it out later.
- **Large leaves or wet grass** to lay between sand and clay so they don't stick.
- **A dry spot with a roof or planned cover.** Rain is a mud oven's only real enemy.
- **Dry firewood**, split thin, for the drying fires and every baking day.

## Steps

1. **Build a stand.** Stack bricks or stones into a solid block about waist high — bending to a floor-level oven all day is misery. Fill the middle with rammed earth or stone rubble.
2. **Lay the oven floor.** On top, set fired bricks or flat stones as tight together as you can, and level them by eye with a straight stick. Sweep it clean. Bread will sit right on this.
3. **Pile the sand form.** On the floor, heap damp sand into a smooth dome about 50 cm across and 35–40 cm high at the centre. This dummy dome holds the shape of the empty inside. Pat it firm and cover it with wet leaves.
4. **Build the clay dome over it.** Press brick-sized handfuls of cob against the sand, working around and up in rings, keeping the wall an even 10 cm thick everywhere. Thick walls are the heat store — thin spots make cold spots.
5. **Cut the door.** While the cob is still firm-soft, cut a doorway in the front about 25 cm high and 30 cm wide. The rule that makes the oven breathe right: **door height about two-thirds the height of the inside dome.** Lower, and the fire smothers; higher, and heat pours out.
6. **Let it stiffen, then dig out the sand.** After one or two days the dome holds itself. Reach through the door and scoop out every bit of sand and the leaves.
7. **Dry it with small fires.** Air-dry two or three days, then burn a small fire inside for an hour a day, a little bigger each day, for three or four days. Steam must leave slowly. Smear wet clay into any cracks that open — small cracks are normal.
8. **Bake.** On baking day, burn a lively fire inside for 1.5–2 hours, pushing it around so all the floor gets flame. The sign it's ready: the black soot on the dome's inside **burns away and the walls turn pale** again. Rake every coal out, sweep the floor with a damp (not wet) bundle of grass, and put the bread in. Close the door with a wet wooden board or clay slab. Flat breads take minutes; a solid loaf takes 30–45 minutes.

## Watch out

- **Test the heat with flour before trusting it with dough.** Toss a pinch on the floor: if it blackens instantly, wait a while; if it browns in a few seconds, bake now; if nothing happens, the oven's too cool — fire it longer next time.
- **Rain will dissolve the dome.** Build a simple roof over it, or cover it whenever the sky threatens. A once-soaked oven can often be dried and patched, but don't test that.
- **Big cracks that go right through** come from drying too fast or walls of uneven thickness. Patch with cob mixed a little sandier, and fire more gently.
- **Coals left inside make bitter, sooty bread and burnt bottoms.** Rake and sweep until the floor is clean.
- **The stand must be solid.** A finished oven weighs as much as several people; a leaning stand means a cracked oven.

## How it works

Clay holds heat the way a water skin holds water. Two hours of fire loads the thick walls with warmth; then, with the fire gone, the walls radiate it back into the small closed space from all sides at once. That surrounding, flameless heat cooks the loaf evenly — crisp outside, soft inside — which no open fire can do.
