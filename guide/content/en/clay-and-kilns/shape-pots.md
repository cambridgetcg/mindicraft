A pot is a hole you can carry. Once you can shape one, you can store water, keep grain from mice, and — after firing — boil food over a fire. No wheel, no tools you don't already have. People made pots by pinch and coil for thousands of years before anyone spun a potter's wheel, and the pots were good.

Clay lets you do almost anything, as long as you do it slowly. It has no grain like wood does — no built-in direction that fights back — but it punishes hurry: pulled fast it tears, dried fast it cracks. Every rule here is one lesson in different clothes: *be even, be slow, be gentle.*

Today: a small pinch bowl to learn the feel of clay, then a coil pot big enough to cook in. Your first pot will be lumpy. Lumpy pots boil water just fine.

## You need

- **Clean, kneaded clay** — from "Find and clean clay". It should feel like stiff dough: soft enough to dent with a thumb, firm enough that a wall stands up. Too slumpy: let it dry a while. Cracking at the edges: knead water in. Knead until a cut lump shows no air bubbles — a trapped bubble keeps the clay around it damp, and hidden damp is a little steam bomb waiting for the fire.
- **A small bowl of water** — to keep hands and clay just damp. Damp, not dripping.
- **Slip** — clay cream, your glue for joining. Mash a walnut of clay with a spoonful of water until it is like thick paint — near-equal parts clay and water, not a lump lost in a cup.
- **A flat work surface** — smooth stone, plank, or mat, dusted with dry sand or ash so the pot doesn't stick.
- **A scraping tool** — a flat stick, mussel shell, or spoon-shaped wood, for smoothing walls.
- **A smooth pebble** — river-worn, egg-sized, glassy. For burnishing (polishing) later.
- **A shady, still spot** — finished pots must dry slowly, out of sun and wind, for days.

## Steps

1. **Make a pinch bowl first.** Roll a ball of clay the size of your fist — about 8 cm. Slap it between your palms to drive out air near the surface.
2. **Push your thumb into the middle**, slowly, down to a finger's width from the bottom — you should feel your thumb through the base but not see it.
3. **Pinch and turn.** Squeeze the wall gently between thumb (inside) and fingers (outside), turn a small step, pinch again. Many small pinches, spiralling from the bottom up — never one hard squeeze. The bowl opens like a flower, a little each pass. Aim for an even finger-width wall, about 1 cm. Close your eyes and run fingers up the wall: fingertips find thick and thin spots eyes miss. Even matters more than thin — see below for why.
4. **Start the coil pot.** Pat a pancake about 1 cm thick and as wide as the pot's bottom — a hand-span (about 20 cm) makes a good cooking pot.
5. **Roll a coil.** Roll a lump under flat palms — not fingertips, they make ridges — into a snake finger-thick and forearm-long, working from the middle outward so it stays even. A ribbon means too much pressure; lengthwise cracks mean wet your hands. A good coil feels like it rolls itself.
6. **Score and slip — mud-weld the coil on.** Scratch shallow criss-cross lines (potters say "score") on the base's rim and the coil's underside. Paint both scored faces with slip. Press the coil on all around, wiggling slightly so the rough faces bite. Cut the meeting ends at a slant, score, slip, press. Never lay smooth on smooth: it looks joined but is only leaning — a smooth joint is a crack that hasn't opened yet.
7. **Blend every coil in, inside and out.** Drag clay from the coil down over the joint with your thumb, short strokes all around, the other hand supporting the wall behind the push. Done means you cannot see or feel where one coil ends and the next begins. This isn't decoration — blending welds the coil's clay into the wall's.
8. **Build upward.** To swell the pot outward, lay each coil slightly outside the one below; to close toward the neck, slightly inside — a finger-width of offset at most, or it slumps. Aim for a rounded shape, wide belly, narrower mouth: curves spread stress and heat; corners collect them.
9. **Rest when the walls get tired.** Soft clay carries about a hand-height of wall. When walls bulge or the rim goes oval, stop; let it stiffen in the shade an hour or two until the lower wall feels like cheese, then score, slip, and build on. Keep the rim under a damp leaf or cloth so it stays weldable. Big pots are built in stages.
10. **Scrape and true the walls.** Once slightly firmed, scrape with your shell or stick — long light strokes, inside and out — shaving thick spots until the wall answers your fingers the same everywhere. Smooth the rim with a wet finger. Flatten the base gently, or leave it round for coals (round bottoms heat evenly and crack less).
11. **Wait for leather-hard, then finish.** Let the pot stiffen until it feels like hard cheese or a bar of soap: cool, firm, not sticky, not yet pale. Potters call this **leather-hard** — the golden hour of the craft, when the pot holds its shape but still forgives. Now trim the base, carve decoration, add handles or lugs (small clay ears to grip the pot by): score, slip, press, blend — the same mud-weld as always.
12. **Burnish for a pot that sheds water.** At leather-hard, rub the outside with your pebble in small circling strokes, just hard enough to leave a shiny track. Cover the whole pot, let it stiffen, go over it again. Dull mud becomes a soft low shine — and sheds water noticeably better, even unglazed.
13. **Dry slowly to bone-dry.** Set both pots in the shade, off the ground, out of any breeze, for several days — a week is not too long. Turn them daily so all sides dry alike. **Bone-dry** feels chalky, room-warm against your cheek instead of cool, and surprisingly light: the water has gone. Bone-dry is also most fragile — lift with two cradling hands, never by the rim. Only bone-dry pots may face fire; that's the next guide.

## Watch out

- **Never breathe dry clay dust as a habit.** One dusty afternoon won't hurt; years of it scars lungs — the fine rock dust in clay does the damage. The fix is total: work clay wet, wipe scraps with a wet rag instead of sweeping dry, do dusty jobs outside.
- **Trapped pockets explode in the fire.** It isn't the air itself — it's water. A pocket sealed in a wall keeps the clay around it from ever drying through, and in the kiln that hidden water flashes to steam, which needs over a thousand times the room, and blows the pot apart — sometimes its neighbours too. Knead well, press coils on firmly so no hollow hides beneath, never fold clay over a gap.
- **Thin spots and thick lumps crack.** They dry at different speeds, and the wall tears along the border between them. Check by feel often; even out anything different. Finger-thick everywhere — the base is the usual secretly-thick spot.
- **A drying rim won't take a new coil.** A soft coil on a stiffened rim just sits there, then splits off as they shrink at different rates. The save: score deeply, slip generously, let it soak a minute, then weld.
- **Too much water ruins the day.** Sloppy clay slumps, sticks, and shrinks until it cracks by itself. Water by the fingertip, not the handful. Over-wetted? Walk away and let it stiffen — more working makes it worse.
- **Sharp corners break; curves survive.** Blend a rounded inside corner where wall meets base. Boxy pots die in the fire: a curve passes any push or heat smoothly along to its neighbours, but a corner is a dead end where the passing stops — so stress and heat pool there like water in hollows.
- **Sun and wind tear drying pots apart.** Fast drying shrinks the outside skin while the inside is still wet and full-sized — the pot rips itself open, no fire involved. Shade and still air, always. Rims and handles are edges and dry first: drape them with a leaf or damp cloth so they wait for the body.
- **A cracked bone-dry pot is not worth firing.** Leather-hard cracks can be healed — score, work slip in, press closed. Bone-dry cracks only grow in the fire. Recycle instead: bone-dry clay drinks water back. Soak, knead, begin again — clay forgives everything except fire.

## How it works

Clay is made of flat plates, thousands of times smaller than you can see — a heap of microscopic playing cards. Between the plates sits a thin film of water, and that water is the whole secret: it lets the plates slide over each other, and makes them cling, both at once. Now every rule turns transparent.

*Why pinch and coil work at all:* press gently and the plates slide on their water films into the new shape — and stay, glued there by the same films. No grain, no fibres, nothing fights you. But plates can only slide as fast as the water lets them: pull or bend quickly and they can't rearrange in time, so they let go, and the clay tears. That is clay's whole personality — obedient to patience, merciless to hurry.

*Why joints must be scored and slipped:* press two smooth lumps together and you've laid one flat deck of cards against another — touching, not connected. Scoring makes plates jut out every which way on both faces; slip floods the gap with loose, very wet plates free to move. Press, and the rough faces interlock through the slip like laced fingers. A weld in mud. Skip it, and the joint survives shaping but opens when shrinking pulls on it.

*Why pots shrink, and why drying is a ladder:* drying is water leaving from between the plates. As each film thins, its plates pull closer — millions of tiny gaps closing add up to the whole pot getting smaller. Soft clay has thick films: everything slides. At **leather-hard** the films have thinned until the plates lock — the pot keeps its shape but the surface still yields, which is exactly why that's the moment to trim, carve, and weld on handles. At **bone-dry** the films are nearly gone: chalky, featherlight, done shrinking — and weakest, because the water that made the plates cling has left; now they merely rest against each other, held by almost nothing. Soft, leather-hard, bone-dry: three rungs of one ladder, each rung with less water than the last.

*Why uneven drying kills:* edges, rims, and thin parts lose water first, finishing their shrink while thick damp parts are still full-sized. A finished part welded to a still-shrinking part must tear along the border. Slow, even drying keeps the whole pot on the same rung of the ladder — that is all "dry in the shade" means.

*Why burnishing shines:* the pebble lines the surface plates flat, all lying the same way, like stroking fur smooth. Plates all lying flat the same way act like tiny mirrors facing together — that is the shine — and they leave fewer gaps between them, so water finds fewer doorways in.

One idea under everything: **let the water leave evenly, so every part shrinks by the same amount at the same time.** Uneven shrinking *is* what cracking is.

## Go further

- **Today, with any mud:** make one pinch bowl with your eyes closed, judging the wall only by feel. Fingertips learn evenness faster than eyes — the best hour of practice in pottery.
- **Prove the plate story:** weld two coils with score-and-slip, press two others together smooth. Dry two days, then flex. One breaks like clay; the other pops apart at the joint you never really made.
- **Lids and handles:** a lid is a pinch bowl upside down, sized at leather-hard to the pot's own mouth; a handle is a fat coil mud-welded at both ends. Join at the same wetness as the pot, or shrinkage tears them off.
- **Bigger pots, same hands:** knee-high grain jars are this method with more resting stages — build a hand-height, let it firm, build again. The lower walls scaffold the upper.
- **What this unlocks:** a dried pot is a promise, not a tool. "Fire pottery in a pit kiln" turns these fragile shells into stone that lasts ten thousand years — fire does to the plates what water can never undo.
- **Sibling guide:** "Find and clean clay" — if coils keep cracking however gently you roll, the fault is usually the clay, not your hands.
