A pot is a hole you can carry. Once you can shape one, you can store water, keep grain from mice, and — after firing — boil food over a fire. No wheel needed, no tools you don't already have. People made pots by pinch and coil for thousands of years before anyone spun a potter's wheel, and the pots were good.

Today you will make two things: a small pinch bowl to learn the feel of clay, and a coil pot big enough to cook in. Expect your first pot to be lumpy. Lumpy pots boil water just fine.

## You need

- **Clean, kneaded clay** — from "Find and clean clay". It should feel like stiff dough: soft enough to shape, firm enough to hold a wall up. If it sags, let it dry a little; if it cracks, wet your hands and knead the water in.
- **A small bowl of water** — for keeping your hands and the clay just damp. Damp, not dripping.
- **A flat work surface** — a smooth stone, a plank, a mat of leaves. Dust it with dry sand or ash so the pot doesn't stick.
- **A scraping tool** — a flat stick, a mussel shell, or a smooth pebble, for smoothing walls.
- **A shady spot** — finished pots must dry slowly, out of sun and wind.

## Steps

1. **Make a pinch bowl first.** Roll a ball of clay the size of your fist.
2. **Push your thumb into the middle** of the ball, down to about a finger's width from the bottom.
3. **Pinch and turn.** Squeeze the wall gently between thumb (inside) and fingers (outside), turn the ball a little, pinch again. Go around and around, working from the bottom up, until the wall is an even finger-width thick — about 1 cm. Even thickness matters more than thinness: thick and thin spots dry and heat at different speeds and crack.
4. **Now start the coil pot.** Flatten a pancake of clay about 1 cm thick and as wide as you want the pot's bottom — a hand-span across makes a good cooking pot.
5. **Roll a coil.** Take a lump and roll it under your flat palms on the work surface into a snake as thick as your finger and as long as your forearm. Roll from the middle outward so it stays even.
6. **Score and stick.** Scratch shallow lines (potters say "score") on the rim of the base where the coil will sit, wet the scratches with a fingertip of water, and press the coil on all the way around. Pinch off the extra where the ends meet and blend the seam.
7. **Blend every coil in, inside and out.** Drag clay from the coil down over the joint with your thumb until you cannot see where one coil ends and the next begins. An unblended joint is a ready-made crack.
8. **Build upward, coil on coil.** To make the pot swell outward, lay each coil slightly to the outside of the one below; to close it back in toward the neck, lay them slightly inward. A gently rounded shape — wider in the belly, a bit narrower at the mouth — is strongest in the fire and best over one.
9. **Rest when the walls get tired.** If the pot starts to slump, stop and let it stiffen in the shade for an hour or two, then keep building. Big pots are built in stages.
10. **Smooth and finish.** Scrape the walls even with your shell or stick, smooth the rim with a wet finger, and press the base gently so it sits flat — or leave it round if it will sit in coals.
11. **Dry it slowly in the shade.** Set both pots somewhere shaded and still. They must dry evenly for days before they can face fire — the next guide covers that.

## Watch out

- **Thin spots and thick lumps crack.** Run your fingers over the walls often; even out anything that feels different. Aim for finger-thick everywhere, base included.
- **Dry coils won't stick to a drying pot.** If the pot's rim has stiffened while you rolled coils, wet and score it before adding more. Coils that just sit on top fall off or split apart later.
- **Too much water ruins the day.** Sloppy-wet clay slumps and shrinks badly. Use water by the fingertip, not the handful.
- **Flat, sharp corners break; curves survive.** Where the wall meets the base, blend a rounded inside corner. Boxy pots die in the fire.
- **Sun and wind crack drying pots.** Fast drying makes the outside shrink before the inside — the pot tears itself. Shade, always, and cover the rim with a leaf or cloth so it doesn't dry ahead of the body.

## How it works

Wet clay holds together because water lets its tiny flat grains slide and cling. Every trick in this guide — even walls, blended joints, slow drying — is about letting the water leave evenly, so every part of the pot shrinks by the same amount at the same time. Uneven shrinking is what cracking is.
