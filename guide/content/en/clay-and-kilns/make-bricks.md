A brick is a stone you make wherever you stand. With bricks you can build walls, ovens, chimneys, wells — things too big to shape from one lump of clay. There are two kinds. A **mud brick** (some places call it *adobe*) is just pressed earth dried in the sun: cheap, fast, and strong enough for walls that stay dry. A **fired brick** is a mud brick baked in the kiln until it turns to stone: it shrugs off rain and holds up heavy loads. Make many mud bricks, and fire the best of them for the places water or fire will touch.

The work is simple; the drying is the slow part. One mold, one afternoon of treading mud, and a sunny week can give you fifty bricks.

## You need

- **Earth with clay in it — but not pure clay.** Pure clay cracks as it dries. The good mix is roughly 1 part clay to 2 parts sand. Test yours: roll a damp ball, drop it from waist height. If it splats flat, too sandy; if it stays a ball with barely a dent, too much clay; a ball that flattens some and holds together is right. Fix a too-clayey mix by adding sand.
- **Chopped straw or dry grass**, cut hand-length — a fat handful per bucket of mud. The fibres act like tiny ropes holding the brick together while it dries.
- **A brick mold** — a wooden box with no top and no bottom, inner size about 25 × 12 × 8 cm. That size dries reliably and one hand can lift the brick. Two or four boxes side by side speeds everything up.
- **Water** and a spot to tread mud — a shallow pit or a hide on the ground.
- **A flat, sunny drying yard** covered in dry sand or straw so bricks don't stick to the ground.
- **Your kiln**, for turning the best dried bricks into fired ones.

## Steps

1. **Make the mud.** In your pit, mix earth, sand if needed, and water, and tread it with bare feet until it is an even, stiff paste with no dry lumps — like bread dough you can just barely stir. Tread in the straw last.
2. **Wet the mold** and dust it with sand so the brick releases.
3. **Pack and strike.** Slam handfuls of mud into the mold, pressing into the corners — trapped air makes weak bricks. Scrape the top flat with a stick (this scrape is called *striking off*).
4. **Lift the mold straight up.** The brick stays behind on the drying yard. Rinse the mold and repeat, leaving a hand's width between bricks.
5. **Dry them slowly.** Let them stiffen flat for 2–3 days, out of direct harsh sun on day one if you can (fast drying cracks them — shade with grass). Then stand each brick on its long edge so air reaches all sides, and turn it every day or two. In good sun they're done in 5–10 days: pale all over, and hard enough that a fingernail barely marks them.
6. **Choose and fire the best.** Pick straight, crack-free bricks for the kiln. Stack them inside on edge with finger-wide gaps for flame to pass. Fire slow for the first two hours, then hard for four to six more, until they glow orange inside. Cool sealed for a full day.
7. **Test a fired brick.** Tap two together — good ones ring almost like stone, not thud. Leave one in a pot of water overnight; if it stays hard, you have real fired brick.

## Watch out

- **Damp bricks in the kiln explode.** A brick can feel dry outside and be wet at the heart. Break a test brick: the middle must be one pale colour, not dark. When unsure, give the batch three more days.
- **Cracks while drying** mean too much clay or too-fast sun. Add more sand and straw to the next batch, and shade fresh bricks on their first day.
- **Bricks stuck flat to wet ground rot from below.** Always dry them on sand or straw, never bare damp earth.
- **Rain destroys a drying yard in an hour.** Keep bark, hides, or grass mats ready to cover everything.
- **Mud brick walls melt in rain.** Use them where a roof keeps them dry, or face the weather side with fired brick. Never use plain mud brick for anything holding water.

## How it works

Sand gives the brick a skeleton of grains that don't shrink; clay is the glue between them; straw holds it all while the water leaves. Sun-drying only removes water — wet the brick again and it softens back to mud. Firing past about 900 °C changes the clay itself: its particles fuse together, and that change never reverses. That is the whole difference between mud and stone.
