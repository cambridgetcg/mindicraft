A brick is a stone you make wherever you stand. With bricks you can build walls, ovens, chimneys, wells — things too big to shape from one lump of clay. There are two kinds. A **mud brick** (some places call it *adobe*) is pressed earth dried in the sun: cheap, fast, strong enough for walls that stay dry. A **fired brick** is a mud brick baked in the kiln until it turns to stone: it shrugs off rain and carries heavy loads. Make many mud bricks, and fire the best for the places water or fire will touch.

A brick is also an honest unit: exactly as big as one hand can lift a thousand times in a day, exactly as thick as the sun can dry through. The brick has barely changed in five thousand years — because the hand hasn't. The work is simple; the drying is the slow part. One mold, one afternoon of treading mud, and a sunny week give you fifty bricks. Fifty bricks is a bread oven. Five thousand is a house.

## You need

- **Earth with clay in it — but not pure clay.** Pure clay cracks as it dries. The good mix is roughly 1 part clay to 2 parts sand. Test yours: roll a damp ball, drop it from waist height. Crumbles to pieces: too sandy. Squashes flat like dough without a crack: too much clay — add sand. Flattens some, holds together, maybe a hairline crack: right.
- **Chopped straw or dry grass**, cut about a hand long — a fat handful per bucket of mud. The fibres are tiny ropes holding the brick together while it dries and after.
- **Fresh cow or horse dung** (optional, as old as bricks themselves) — half a shovel per batch. It sounds wrong and works wonderfully: fine chewed fibres and a starchy glue that fight cracking. Once dry it doesn't smell.
- **A brick mold** — a wooden box with no top and no bottom, inner size about a hand-and-a-half long, a hand wide, half a hand deep (25 × 12 × 8 cm). Nail it strongly; wet mud pushes hard on the corners. Two or four boxes joined side by side speed everything up.
- **Water** and a spot to tread mud — a shallow pit, or a hide staked flat.
- **A flat, sunny drying yard** covered in dry sand or straw so bricks don't stick, big enough that every brick gets a hand's width of air.
- **Your kiln**, to turn the best dried bricks into fired ones.

## Steps

1. **Soak the earth first.** Break the dug earth into a pit, cover with water, leave overnight. Soaked earth treads smooth in an hour; dry earth fights you all day. Pick out roots and stones bigger than a fingertip — a stone in a fired brick can pop it.
2. **Tread the mud.** Bare feet, round and round, folding the edges to the middle. Done is an even, stiff paste with no dry lumps and no soupy pockets — like bread dough you can just barely stir, stiff enough that a handful holds its shape on your palm. Tread in the straw (and dung) last, until every handful shows fibres.
3. **Wet the mold** and dust the inside with dry sand so the brick lets go. Fresh for every brick; a sticky mold tears corners off.
4. **Pack and strike.** Slam handfuls of mud into the mold hard — you are chasing out air, and trapped air makes weak bricks and burst fired ones. Press into the corners with your thumbs until mud squeezes back. Scrape the top dead flat with a straight stick in one firm pass (this scrape is called *striking off*). A well-packed brick sounds dull when you slap it; a hollow slap means an air pocket — dig it out and re-pack.
5. **Lift the mold straight up**, smooth and vertical. The brick stays behind, sharp-cornered and glistening. A slumped corner means too-wet mud — tread in drier earth. Rinse the mold, sand it, repeat, a hand's width between bricks. A steady worker fills a mold in under a minute.
6. **Dry them slowly — this is where bricks are won or lost.** Day one: shade them from harsh sun with grass or mats; fast drying cracks them. Days 2–3: they stiffen enough to handle — the color pales at the edges first. Now stand each brick on its long narrow edge so air reaches all faces, and turn it end-for-end every day or two. In good sun they are done in 5–10 days: one pale even color on every face, edges that don't crumble under a thumb, a fingernail that barely marks. A dry brick is also clearly lighter — heft a fresh one and a week-old one and you'll feel the water gone.
7. **Choose and fire the best.** Pick the straight, crack-free bricks for the kiln. Stack them inside on edge with finger-wide gaps everywhere so flame can lick every face — bricks touching flat keep a raw stripe. Fire gently for the first two or three hours (steam out slowly — bricks are thick, and their hearts lag behind their skins), then hard for four to six more, until the whole stack glows orange like the coals and the flames off the top burn clean. Then stop feeding, seal the kiln, and walk away for a full day. Cracking it open hot cracks the bricks.
8. **Test a fired brick.** Tap two together: good ones ring almost like stone; underfired ones thud. Drop one in a pot of water overnight: a true fired brick comes out hard; a half-fired one softens at the corners. Ring plus the overnight bath passed — you have made stone.

## Watch out

- **Damp bricks in the kiln explode — the big one.** Water trapped in a brick's heart turns to steam, steam wants some sixteen hundred times more room, and the brick bursts, spraying hot sharp shards. A brick can feel bone-dry outside and be wet inside. The test: sacrifice one — break it open. The middle must be the same pale color as the skin; a dark core is wet. When in doubt, the whole batch waits three more days. Sun is cheap; eyes are not.
- **Kilns and fresh-fired bricks burn you long after the fire dies.** A brick holds skin-searing heat for a day under an innocent grey surface. Touch nothing from the kiln for a full day, then test with a flick of water — if it hisses, wait.
- **Dry clay dust harms lungs slowly.** Dust from crushing dry earth, sweeping the yard, or trimming dry bricks carries fine quartz dust — silica, the sand's own flour — your lungs never get rid of; the harm builds over years. Make it a habit: work the earth wet, work outside, wet the yard before sweeping, stand upwind. One dusty day won't hurt you; a dusty life will.
- **Cracks while drying** mean too much clay or too-fast sun. Big center cracks: too clayey — more sand next batch. A web of fine surface cracks: dried too fast — shade the first day, add more straw. Cracked mud bricks still serve as filler; cracked bricks never go in the kiln.
- **Bricks stuck flat to wet ground rot from below.** The ground feeds water up as fast as the sun pulls it out the top. Always dry on dry sand or straw, never bare damp earth.
- **Rain destroys a drying yard in an hour** — fifty bricks back to mud. Keep bark slabs, hides, or grass mats stacked at the yard's edge, ready to throw over everything at the first fat drop.
- **Mud brick walls melt in rain — slowly, but surely.** Use them only where a roof keeps them dry, face the weather side with fired brick, and never use plain mud brick for anything that holds water.
- **Frost inside a damp brick splits it** — water swells as it freezes. Late in the year, get bricks dry and under cover before the first hard cold.

## How it works

Start with the deepest fact about mud: **it is strong when squeezed, weak when pulled.** Pile weight on a dry mud brick and it holds astonishing loads — the grains lock like a crowd pressed shoulder to shoulder. But pull it — or bend it, which is a pull in disguise, stretching the outside of the curve — and it snaps, because nothing ties grain to grain. Every trick in brickmaking answers that one weakness.

**Straw is the answer inside the brick.** The fibres are exactly the missing part: tiny ropes running every direction through the mud — and a rope is a thing made for taking a pull. When the brick is knocked or pried at, the mud alone would tear; the straw takes the pull. And drying is itself a pulling: clay shrinks as the water leaves, and shrinking flesh around a fixed skeleton of sand wants to tear itself open. Chopped straw — and dung, with its thousands of even finer chewed fibres plus a starch glue — breaks that one big tearing pull into a thousand harmless tugs, each too small to open a crack. That is why clay-sand-fibre appears on every continent: nobody copied anybody; the mud demanded it everywhere.

**Why brick-sized?** Two rulers set the size. One hand must lift it all day, so a brick weighs what a hand forgives — three or four kilograms. And the sun must dry it through: water leaves only through the surface, so drying time grows brutally with thickness. A brick half a hand thick dries in a week; double the thickness and the middle stays wet for weeks more. The brick is the largest stone the hand and the sun agree on.

**Sun-drying and firing differ in kind, not degree — firing is not just fiercer drying, it makes a new material.** Clay is a dust of flat plates that grip when wet, the way two wet panes of glass cling together. Sun-drying only removes the water; the plates still just rest against each other. Soak them long enough and they slide apart — mud again. Adobe never crossed the one-way door — the change that cannot be soaked back. That is why adobe buildings wear rain-hats: wide eaves throwing water clear of the walls. Under a good hat, mud walls stand for centuries. Firing is the one-way door itself, and it opens in two stages. Around 600 °C the heat drives off water locked inside the plates themselves — not the damp between plates that the sun removed, but water built into each plate — and from that moment the clay can never be mud again, but the brick is still weak. Push on toward 900–1000 °C and the plates' edges begin to melt and weld, plate fused to plate. The brick is now **ceramic** — one stony thing — and rain means nothing to it.

**The clamp — the brick's most beautiful trick — is a stack of bricks that fires itself.** Stack dried bricks into a loose block with fuel tunnels through the base; pack the tunnels with wood, plaster the outside with mud, light it. The stack *is* the kiln, burning for days. Outer bricks come out soft-fired — use them inside walls, away from weather; the heart bricks ring like stone. The product builds its own oven.

**And when you lay them:** stagger every joint, so no joint sits above the joint below. A crack climbing a wall follows the weak mortar lines — staggered, it finds no straight road up. The zigzag on every brick wall you have ever seen is not decoration. It is a maze for cracks.

## Go further

- **Today's practice, no kiln needed:** make one batch three ways — no straw, straw, straw plus dung — mold three bricks of each, dry side by side, watch which crack. You will never skip the straw again.
- **Fire a clamp.** Once you trust your mud bricks, stack thirty into a knee-high clamp with two fuel tunnels and fire them without a kiln. Sort afterward by ring: soft outer bricks for inner walls, hard heart bricks for wet work.
- **Build the first thing:** a bread oven takes about fifty bricks — fired where flame touches, mud brick for the surround. It repays the batch in dinners.
- **Make roof tiles from the same mud** — slabs curved over a log to dry, then fired hard. Tiles are the rain-hat that lets mud walls live forever.
- **Deepen the fire side** in the kiln guide, and **the earth side** in the clay-finding guide: hotter, evener firings and a better clay pit both mean more ringing bricks per batch.
