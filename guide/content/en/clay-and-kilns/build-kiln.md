---
title: Build a simple kiln
summary: Build a clay oven with a fire hole and a draft so pots fire harder and fewer of them crack.
difficulty: hard
time: a week

A **kiln** is a house you build for fire. In an open pit, most of your fire's heat blows away into the sky, and pots sitting in cold spots stay soft or crack. A kiln traps the heat around the pots, so the whole load gets hot together — and hotter than a pit ever gets. A pit fire reaches roughly 700–900 °C. Even a simple kiln can pass 900–1000 °C, and at that heat clay starts turning into something like stone: harder, tighter, and able to hold water without weeping.

The kiln here is the oldest kind, an **updraft kiln**: fire enters low, heat rises through the pots, and smoke leaves through a hole in the top. The rising hot air is the **draft** — it pulls fresh air into the fire the way a chimney does, and that steady pull is what makes the fire burn hot.

## You need

- **Clay-rich earth, a lot of it** — several large basketfuls. It doesn't need to be as clean as pot clay; dig where your pot clay came from.
- **Coarse sand and chopped dry grass or straw**, to mix into the clay so the walls shrink less and crack less as they dry.
- **Water** for mixing.
- **Stones or old broken pots**, for the floor grate that holds pots above the fire.
- **Dry firewood — much more than you think.** A full firing eats a big stack: roughly an arm-load every half hour for six to eight hours.
- **A dry, level spot** sheltered from strong wind, on ground that doesn't flood.

## Steps

1. **Mix the wall material.** Tread together about 2 parts earth, 1 part sand, and a generous handful of chopped straw per bucketful, with just enough water to make a stiff dough that holds its shape. This mix is often called **cob**.
2. **Mark the shape.** Draw a circle on the ground about 60–80 cm across — room for one good batch of pots. Bigger is harder to heat.
3. **Build the fire hole first.** On the side the wind usually comes from, form a low tunnel about 25 cm wide and 25 cm tall — this is the mouth where wood goes in and air is pulled in.
4. **Raise the walls.** Build up the circle in rings of cob, each ring about 10 cm thick, pressing every handful firmly onto the last. Let each day's work stiffen overnight before adding more. Lean the walls slowly inward as they rise, closing toward a dome.
5. **Set the grate.** About 30 cm up, bridge stones or thick pot shards across the inside, with plenty of gaps between them. Pots will sit on this shelf; flame passes up through the gaps.
6. **Leave a chimney hole.** Close the dome at about waist height, but leave an opening 10–15 cm across at the very top. Heat out the top pulls air in the bottom — that's your draft.
7. **Dry it for days.** Let the kiln air-dry three or four days. Then burn a small fire inside for an hour or two each day for two more days, growing it slowly. This bakes the walls so they don't burst with steam later. Small cracks will appear; smear wet clay into them.
8. **Fire your first load.** Stack bone-dry pots on the grate, not touching the walls. Warm them with a tiny fire for two hours, then feed the fire up over four to six hours until the inside glows bright orange. Then stop feeding, block the mouth and top hole with clay slabs, and walk away for a full day. Open only when you can rest your hand on the outside.

## Watch out

- **Wet pots explode.** Water inside clay turns to steam and bursts the pot like a little bomb, wrecking its neighbours. Pots must dry at least a week and pass the cheek test: cold against your cheek means still damp.
- **Heating too fast cracks everything** — pots and kiln. The first two hours are for gentle warming only. Patience is most of the skill.
- **Opening early is how good firings are ruined.** Hot pots hitting cold air crack with a heartbreak *ping*. Wait the full day.
- **Rain on a drying kiln melts it.** Cover unfired cob with bark, hides, or a lean-to until it has been fired once.
- **Fire spreads.** Clear the ground bare for two big steps all around, and never leave a firing kiln alone.

## How it works

Hot air is lighter than cold air, so it rises out of the top hole and fresh air rushes in at the mouth to replace it — a pump made of nothing but heat. The walls trap warmth and glow it back at the pots from every side. Around 900 °C the clay particles begin to fuse at their edges, a change called **sintering**, and the pot stops being dried mud and starts being stone.
