Coiling a pot by hand takes an hour and comes out a little lopsided, because your hands move around the pot. A potter's wheel flips that: the pot spins, your hands hold still, and the spinning itself makes the pot round. The heart of the machine is a **flywheel** — a disc heavy enough that once you spin it, it *keeps* spinning while you work. Weight is the whole trick. A heavy wheel stores your effort and pays it back as long, smooth turning.

Fair warning: the wheel is a machine you can build in two days and a skill that takes two weeks of daily failure to begin to own. Every potter's first twenty attempts collapse. That is the normal price.

## You need

- **A heavy disc, 60–90 cm across** — thick plank sections pegged together crosswise, or a single slab of wood weighted near its rim with a ring of clay or stones. Aim for a weight you can just lift: 20–40 kg. Weight at the *rim* matters most.
- **A pivot** — a fist-sized hardwood or stone block with a smooth, rounded dimple carved in the top, set firmly in the ground. The disc's underside gets a matching hard, rounded peg or stone at its exact centre. Grease the pair with animal fat.
- **A wheel head** — a smooth flat spot at the disc's centre, or a small round board pegged on top, where the clay sits.
- **A stout stick** for spinning the wheel by hand, fitting into a notch near the rim. (Later you can add a taller shaft and kick the flywheel with your foot — same machine, freer hands.)
- **Well-prepared clay** — cleaned, and kneaded until it is even and airless. Wheel clay should be firm-soft: it holds a thumbprint but doesn't stick badly to dry hands.
- **A bowl of water, a sponge or wad of soft grass, a wooden rib** (a palm-sized smooth scraper you carve), and **a thin cord** for cutting pots off.
- **A low seat** so your elbows can brace on your knees.

## Steps

1. **Balance the wheel first.** Set disc on pivot and spin it. It should turn freely for a minute or more and not wobble. Shave wood or shift weights until it runs true — a wobbling wheel makes wobbling pots, and no skill fixes that.
2. **Knead the clay** by pressing and folding it on a board fifty times, like stiff dough. This drives out air bubbles, which otherwise become bumps and blowouts.
3. **Slap a grapefruit-sized ball** hard onto the exact centre of the dry wheel head so it sticks.
4. **Spin up the wheel** — a fast steady spin, counter-clockwise if you're right-handed. Re-spin whenever it slows; a flywheel forgives pauses.
5. **Centre the clay.** Wet your hands. Brace both elbows on your knees, cup the spinning clay with both hands, and squeeze steadily inward and slightly down until the lump runs perfectly still under your palms — no bump, no wobble. This is the hard skill. Steady pressure, never sudden moves. Expect this step alone to take days to learn.
6. **Open it.** Press both thumbs slowly down into the centre of the spinning lump, stopping a thumb's width from the wheel. Pull your thumbs gently apart to widen the hole into a thick ring.
7. **Pull up the walls.** One fingertip inside, knuckle outside, pinching the wall lightly between them, draw both hands slowly upward together. The wall rises and thins. Three patient pulls beat ten hurried ones. Keep hands wet; dry hands drag and tear.
8. **Shape and finish.** Use the wooden rib to smooth and curve the wall. Steady the rim between wet fingers.
9. **Cut it free.** Stop the wheel. Pull the cord flat under the pot, and slide the pot onto a board to dry slowly before firing.

## Watch out

- **Off-centre clay ruins everything after it.** If the lump wobbles, do not push on — re-centre or start over. Ten seconds of honesty saves ten minutes of collapse.
- **Too much water turns clay to slime** and the pot slumps. Wet hands, yes — pooling water, no. Sponge it out of the bottom.
- **Walls thinner at the bottom than the top fall.** Pull from the very bottom each time; leave the base a finger thick.
- **Spinning too fast for the step:** fast for centering, slower for pulling, slowest for the rim.
- **Guard the spinning rim** — a heavy flywheel can bruise a child's reaching hand. Stop it with a stick, not fingers.

## How it works

A spinning heavy rim stores movement the way a raised weight stores a fall — push it once, and it gives the push back over a whole minute. Because the clay turns while your braced hands stay still, any pressure you apply is applied to *every* point of the circle equally. Roundness stops being something you attempt and becomes something the spin guarantees.
