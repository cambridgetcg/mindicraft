Boiling changes everything. Before pots, you could roast food over flames and that was about it. With a pot that sits in the fire, you can boil water safe to drink, simmer tough roots and grains until they are soft food, and make soup that pulls every scrap of goodness out of bones and greens. One pot turns the same forage into more meals, safer meals, and meals a toothless elder or a small child can eat.

Your pit-fired pot is unglazed — it has no glassy coating, so its walls are slightly porous, like very hard bread. The first cooking seals it. After that, treat it gently and it will serve for years.

## You need

- **A fired pot** — one that rings when tapped, from "Fire pots in an open pit". A rounded bottom is best; it heats evenly and sits happily in coals.
- **A steady bed of coals** — coals, not roaring flames. Flames lick one spot and crack the pot; coals give broad, even heat.
- **Three fist-sized stones, or a scooped hollow in the coals** — to hold the pot upright and stable. Use dry stones from high ground, never stones from a stream (river stones can hold water inside and shatter in fire).
- **Something to stir and lift with** — a long wooden spoon or stick, and two thick pads of leaves, bark, or folded hide for lifting the hot pot.
- **Clean water and food** — whatever you have: grain, roots, greens, meat, bones.

## Steps

1. **Season the new pot first.** Fill it with water and a handful of starchy food — grain, flour, or mashed starchy root — and bring it slowly to a simmer for an hour. The starch soaks into the pores and seals them. A new pot may sweat or weep water through its walls the first few uses; that is normal and stops as the pores seal.
2. **Warm the pot before every use.** Set it near the fire for a few minutes, then at the edge of the coals, then in. Clay hates sudden temperature change — going from cold to hot in stages is what keeps the pot alive.
3. **Never heat an empty pot.** Always have water or food inside before it goes near the coals. An empty pot overheats in patches and cracks.
4. **Nest it in the coals.** Set the pot on your three stones with coals raked between them, or twist its rounded base straight down into the coal bed. Bank coals around the sides — most of the cooking heat should come from the sides, not one hot spot underneath.
5. **To make water safe: boil it hard.** Bring it to a full rolling boil — big bubbles breaking the whole surface — and keep it there for a full minute (three minutes if you are high in the mountains, above roughly 2,000 m, where water boils cooler). Boiling kills the germs that cause most stomach sickness. It does not remove mud or poisons, so start with the clearest water you can find and let cloudy water settle first.
6. **To make soup or stew:** put in water first, then the slow things (bones, tough roots, dried food), simmer, and add quick things (greens, soft vegetables) near the end. A gentle simmer — small bubbles, not a violent boil — for one to several hours turns tough into tender and pulls the richness out of bones.
7. **Stir, and mind the bottom.** Thick porridge or stew can scorch where the pot meets the coals. Stir now and then, and keep the heat to coals, not flame.
8. **Take it off the heat gently.** Lift with both pads, and set the hot pot on dry ground, wood, or ash — never on cold wet ground, and never splash cold water into a hot pot. Let it cool before washing.
9. **Clean it kindly.** Rinse with warm water and scrub with sand or ash. Let it dry fully before storing, or it can grow mould in its pores.

## Watch out

- **Thermal shock is the pot-killer.** Cold pot into hot fire, cold water into hot pot, hot pot onto wet ground — each can crack it with a loud tick. Everything in stages, always.
- **A cracked pot near food is a lost pot.** A fine crack whistles or seeps when boiling; retire that pot to dry storage (grain, seeds) before it fails mid-meal and douses your fire.
- **Boiling does not fix everything.** It kills germs; it does not remove chemicals, salt, or rot. Never boil water from a source that smells foul or sits below obvious filth — find better water first.
- **Stream stones explode.** Only use dry stones from high, dry ground as pot supports. Water trapped inside a river stone turns to steam and bursts with real force.
- **Food left warm goes bad fast.** Eat what you cook, or re-boil leftovers hard before eating them. Warm soup left overnight is a common way to get sick.

## How it works

Water can never get hotter than its boiling point, no matter how fierce the fire — so the pot's contents sit at a steady, safe cooking heat that never burns the way flame does. That steady wet heat unwinds tough fibres in roots and meat, bursts starch grains so we can digest them, and kills germs. The pot is simply a way to hold water inside a fire.
