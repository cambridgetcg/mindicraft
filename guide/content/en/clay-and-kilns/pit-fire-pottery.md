An unfired pot is just dried mud — one rain and it is a puddle again. Fire changes the clay itself: heat it past about 600 °C and the clay is remade so it can never soak apart. That change goes only one way — firing is a door that locks behind you. The lock is the whole point: it is why a fired pot can never turn back into mud, and it is also why every mistake in the fire is permanent. A simple pit in the ground reaches 700–900 °C — plenty.

The firing takes two days: one to burn, one to cool — but the pots must be truly dry first, and that drying takes a week or more. Water trapped inside a pot turns to steam in the fire and bursts it like a kettle with no spout. Almost every rule below is one rule in different clothes: never let two parts of a pot live at two different temperatures.

## You need

- **Bone-dry pots** — dried in the shade at least a week (longer in damp weather or for thick walls). Bone dry means room temperature to the touch everywhere, even the base; a pot that feels cool against your cheek still holds water — that coolness is the dampness stealing warmth from your skin.
- **Pots made with temper**, if you can — clay mixed with coarse sand or crushed old pottery (potters call this grog) before shaping. Tempered pots survive the fire far better; "How it works" says why.
- **A pit** — knee-deep (about 50 cm), wide enough for all your pots with a hand-span around each, dug in dry ground away from anything that can catch. Wet ground steams and steals your heat.
- **Lots of dry fuel** — far more than seems reasonable: twigs and bark for kindling, plus a big pile of dry wood, dried dung, or both. Dung burns slow and even; wrist-thick split wood burns hotter than whole logs.
- **A small ordinary fire nearby** — from "Keep a fire day and night" — to pre-warm the pots.
- **Sticks or tongs of green (fresh-cut) wood** — sap-filled, so it doesn't catch; for moving hot things.
- **Broken pottery, flat stones, or earth** — to blanket the pit at the end and slow the cooling.
- **Patience** — the pots cool overnight, untouched. Opening early breaks them.

## Steps

1. **Check every pot is bone dry.** Hold each against your cheek — cool means wet inside; give it more days. Damp clay is darker: watch for a dark patch at the thick base. Any pot with a visible crack stays out: it will only break and smash its neighbours.
2. **Pre-warm the pots (morning of day one).** Stand them around your ordinary fire, an arm's length (about 60 cm) away, mouths toward the heat. Every half hour, turn each pot and move it a hand closer; over 2–3 hours bring them in until almost too hot to hold — summer-sun hot, not cooking hot. The last hidden water leaves as quiet vapour, not violent steam, and the pots enter the pit already warm.
3. **Lay the pit bed.** While the pots warm, cover the pit floor with a hand-deep (about 10 cm) layer of small dry wood, bark, or dung. This bed burns under the pots and keeps their bottoms as hot as their tops.
4. **Load the pots warm**, straight from the pre-warming fire. Set them on the bed upside down or on their sides, a hand-span apart, not touching — touching pots heat unevenly where they meet. Tuck small fuel between and inside them, so each pot is heated from the inside as well as the outside.
5. **Build the fuel over the top.** Lean wood over and around the pots like a tent, then pile more across, until every pot is buried and the mound stands above ground level. It should look like too much fuel. It is not.
6. **Light it from the edges, and go slow.** Light small fires at three or four points around the rim, spread evenly — never one big blaze on one side. First hour: lazy flames licking inward, heat creeping to the centre from all sides. Fast heat in this hour is what kills pots: it is when the last water leaves and the pots pass their most fragile checkpoints (see "How it works").
7. **Feed it to full heat.** Once the whole mound burns on its own — the crackle settled into a deep roar — add wood steadily for 2–4 hours and keep the entire pit blazing, not just one side. Your thermometer is the glow of the pots down among the coals: first dull red means roughly 600–700 °C, the change to ceramic happening; bright red into orange means roughly 800–900 °C — as hot as a pit gets, and enough. Glow is easiest to judge at dusk; in daylight, shade your eyes and peer into the gaps.
8. **Let it die down and blanket it.** Let the flames fall to coals, then cover the pit with broken pottery, flat stones, ash, or earth. This traps the heat so the pots cool over many hours instead of one. Cooling too fast cracks pots just as heating too fast does — the same rule, run backward.
9. **Walk away until morning.** The pit must cool on its own, overnight at least. No peeking, no poking, never any water. Day two: hold your palm above the ashes — any real warmth, wait longer.
10. **Unpack and test.** Lift each pot out only when fully cool and brush off the ash. Expect clouds of black, orange, and grey — fire-marks from flame and smoke, normal and often prized. Tap the wall with a fingernail: fired clay rings clear like a struck cup; raw clay answers with a dull thud. Splash water inside: fired clay may darken but never turns slick or muddy. A pot that thuds and goes slimy never reached the change — back into the clay pile it goes.

## Watch out

- **Steam explosions — the number one failure.** A pot not bone dry, skipped in pre-warming, or hiding an air pocket folded into its wall when it was shaped, bursts in the first hour with a loud pop and can crack every pot near it. Trapped steam is a tiny bomb (see below). The save: dry longer than feels necessary, pre-warm every pot, and when in doubt wait another week. If one pops, never reach in — that trades a pot for a burned arm.
- **Wet rocks are the same bomb.** River stones and rocks from damp ground hold water in their pores and can burst in the fire, throwing hot shards. Ring the pit only with dry stones from a dry place, and stand back in the first hour.
- **Cracks from the "quartz shiver."** A pot split cleanly, or ringing dull with a hairline crack, with no bang, usually crossed the 573 °C checkpoint (explained below) unevenly — one side hotter at the wrong moment. The save is everything that keeps heat even: a calm day (wind heats one side of the pit — or shield the windward side with earth or stones), lighting from all sides, pots not touching, slow first hour, slow cooling.
- **Cooling too fast cracks pots too.** Never pour water on the pit, never lift a warm pot into cold air, never fire when rain threatens — a shower on a hot pit can crack the whole batch. Slow start, slow finish.
- **Burns.** Pots under the ash blanket stay scorching long after the surface looks grey and dead. Test with a hovering palm before you touch; move hot things only with green-wood sticks or tongs.
- **Wildfire.** This is a large open fire. Clear everything burnable for 3 m around the pit, never fire in wind, keep water or loose earth ready, and stay until only coals remain under the blanket.
- **Dust, day after day.** Dry clay dust and the powder from crushing old pots is fine stone dust; breathing it habitually scars the lungs slowly (this harm is called silicosis). Work clay wet, crush and scrape outside, never sweep dry clay dust indoors.
- **Some loss is normal.** Skilled pit-firers still lose a pot or two in ten. Fire several at once and keep every broken piece: crushed, they become temper for the next pots; whole, they blanket the next firing. In pottery, failure is a raw material.

## How it works

Follow one pot down into the fire, checkpoint by checkpoint. Clay is made of flat mineral plates far too small to see, stacked like wet paper with water in between. Firing is the story of driving that water out — in stages, each hotter and more final than the last.

**Around boiling heat (about 100 °C), the free water leaves.** This is the water sitting between the plates — the dampness your cheek can feel. Given time, it walks out calmly as vapour. Rushed, it flashes to steam, which needs more than a thousand times the room the water took. Steam trapped inside a clay wall is a bomb with nowhere to go — the same bomb as a wet rock bursting in a campfire. This is why bone-dry, why pre-warming, why the slow first hour.

**A little hotter, the leftovers burn away.** Any straw, grass, or pond-slime in the clay chars and leaves as smoke. Harmless, so long as the fire is slow enough for the gases to escape.

**At exactly 573 °C, every quartz grain shivers.** Clay nearly always carries grains of quartz, the common glassy sand mineral. At that precise temperature, quartz snaps its inner shape into a slightly roomier arrangement, and every grain jumps a hair bigger, all at once. Call it the quartz shiver. If the whole pot crosses 573 °C together, it shivers together and nothing happens. If one side crosses while the other lags — wind, a draft, one fierce flame — one half has grown while the other has not, and the pot tears itself apart. This is the invisible reason behind every "keep the heat even" rule. The shiver runs in reverse on the way down through 573 °C, which is why cooling must be just as slow.

**Past roughly 600 °C, the door locks.** Now the fire drives out water that was never wet to the touch — water locked inside the mineral plates themselves, part of their structure. Once it is gone, the plates can never take it back. Then, as the heat climbs on toward the pit's peak, their bare edges begin to weld where they touch (this welding is called sintering), like snowflakes packed until they fuse into ice. The clay stops being clay. It is ceramic now — a new stone that water can never soften again. Everything before this point is reversible; nothing after it is.

**Reading heat by glow:** the first visible dull red is roughly 600–700 °C — the door is locking; bright red-orange is roughly 800–900 °C — a pit fire's ceiling. Hotter and longer means more welding and a stronger pot — the whole reason later kilns chase higher heat.

And temper? Those sand grains or crushed-pot bits sit scattered through the smooth clay like boulders in a field: when heat stress starts a small crack, the crack runs into a grain and stops there instead of racing across the pot. Temper doesn't prevent cracks; it keeps small ones small.

## Go further

- **A safe first try today:** roll three thumb-sized clay balls — one plain, one with sand, one still damp — and set them at the edge of a campfire with a green-wood stick, then stand well back. Tomorrow the damp one has likely popped; the others ring when tapped. The whole guide, in miniature.
- **Grow the pit into a kiln.** Walls around the fire hold the heat instead of spilling it to the sky — that is all a kiln is — and push past the pit's 900 °C ceiling toward harder, eventually waterproof pots.
- **Deepen the neighbours:** "Shape pots" for building with temper from the start, and "Keep a fire day and night" for the fire-craft this guide leans on.
