An unfired pot is just dried mud — one rain and it is a puddle again. Fire changes the clay itself: heat it past about 600 °C and the clay grains fuse so they can never soak apart. That is the whole magic of pottery. A simple pit in the ground can reach 700–900 °C, and that is plenty.

The firing itself takes two days: one to burn, one to cool. But the pots must be truly, completely dry before fire ever touches them — and that drying takes a week or more beforehand. Do not rush it. Water trapped inside a pot turns to steam in the fire and bursts the pot like a small kettle with no spout.

## You need

- **Bone-dry pots** — dried in the shade for at least a week (longer in damp weather). Bone dry means: room temperature to the touch everywhere, even the base. A pot that feels cool against your cheek still holds water — wait.
- **A pit** — knee-deep (about 50 cm) and wide enough for your pots with a hand-span of space around each. Dig it in dry ground, well away from anything that can catch fire.
- **Lots of dry fuel** — far more than seems reasonable: an arm-load of twigs and bark for kindling, and a big pile of dry wood, dried dung, or both. Dung burns slow and even and is excellent for this.
- **A small ordinary fire nearby** — from "Keep a fire day and night" — to pre-warm the pots.
- **Sticks or tongs of green wood** — for moving hot things. Green (fresh-cut) wood doesn't catch easily.
- **Patience** — the pots cool overnight. Opening early breaks them.

## Steps

1. **Check every pot is bone dry.** Hold each against your cheek. Cool means wet inside — give it more days. Any pot with a visible crack: leave it out; it will only break and its flying pieces can break its neighbours.
2. **Pre-warm the pots (morning of day one).** Stand them around your ordinary fire, an arm's length away, mouths toward the heat. Over 2–3 hours, move them closer in stages until they are almost too hot to touch. This drives out the last hidden water gently.
3. **Lay the pit bed.** Cover the pit floor with a hand-deep layer of small dry wood or dung.
4. **Load the pots.** Set them on the bed upside down or on their sides, a hand-span apart, not touching. Tuck small fuel between and inside them.
5. **Build the fuel over the top.** Lean wood over and around the pots until they are completely buried in fuel, mounded above ground level.
6. **Light it from the edges, and go slow.** Light small fires at several points around the rim, not one big blaze. Let the heat build over the first hour. Fast heat in the first hour is what kills pots.
7. **Feed it to full heat.** Once everything is burning, add wood steadily for 2–4 hours and keep the whole pit blazing. At full heat the pots glow dull red to orange down among the coals — that glow is your thermometer, roughly 700–900 °C.
8. **Let it die down and cover it (optional but good).** When the flames fall to coals, you can blanket the pit with ash, earth, or broken pottery pieces. This traps heat and slows the cooling.
9. **Walk away until morning.** The pit must cool on its own, overnight at least. Day two: if anything is still too hot to touch, wait longer.
10. **Unpack and test.** Lift each pot out with sticks or bare hands only when fully cool. Brush off the ash. Tap the wall with a fingernail: fired clay gives a clear ring, raw clay a dull thud. Drop a splash of water inside: fired clay may darken but never turns muddy.

## Watch out

- **Steam explosions.** The number one failure. A pot that was not bone dry, or not pre-warmed, bursts in the first hour with a pop and can crack every pot near it. When in doubt, dry another week.
- **Heating or cooling too fast cracks pots.** Clay hates sudden change (this is called thermal shock). Slow start, slow finish. Never pour water on the pit, and never pull a warm pot into cold air.
- **Wildfire.** You are building a large open fire. Clear the ground of everything burnable for 3 m around the pit, never fire in wind, keep water or loose earth ready, and stay until only coals remain.
- **Wind during firing** makes one side hotter than the other and cracks pots. Pick a calm day, or shield the windward side with earth or stones.
- **Some loss is normal.** Even skilled pit-firers lose a pot or two in ten. Fire several at once; keep the broken pieces — they make good cover for your next firing.

## How it works

Up to about 100 °C, fire is only boiling water out of the clay — which is why hidden water explodes. Past about 350 °C the water locked inside the clay grains themselves is driven off, and past roughly 600 °C the grains begin to fuse edge to edge. The clay is now a new material, ceramic: it can never be softened by water again. Hotter and longer means harder and stronger, which is why later kilns chase higher heat.
