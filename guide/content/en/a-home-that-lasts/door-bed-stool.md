A house becomes a home the day the door swings shut behind you. A door keeps out wind, rain, and animals. A bed lifts you off the ground, which steals body heat all night and hosts every crawling thing. A stool and a shelf mean you stop squatting in the dirt and losing your tools in it. None of this needs fancy joinery — just split planks, pegs, and one idea: a flat board is weak alone, strong when crossed with another.

## You need

- **Split planks** — flat boards riven (split along the grain) from straight logs, roughly thumb-thick, smoothed with an axe or knife. About six long ones for the door, more for bed and shelf.
- **Stout poles** — wrist-thick, for bed frame and stool legs. Sound, dry wood.
- **Wooden pegs and a way to make holes** — an auger or drill if you have one; otherwise burn holes patiently with a hot metal rod. Whittle pegs slightly fatter than their holes.
- **A knife and axe** — for trimming, pegs, and notches.
- **Strips of thick leather or rawhide (optional)** — the simplest hinges there are.
- **Dry straw, bracken, or grass, plus a big cloth sack if you can** — the mattress.

## Steps

1. **Build the door as a ledged door.** Lay planks side by side until they fill the doorway with a finger-gap to spare all round. Across them peg two horizontal boards (ledges) — one a hand below the top, one a hand above the bottom.
2. **Add the brace, and mind its direction.** Peg one diagonal board from the bottom hinge-side corner up to the far top corner. The brace carries the door's weight down into the hinges; sloped the wrong way, it lets the door sag within a year.
3. **Hang it.** Two honest choices:
   - **Leather hinges:** peg two doubled strips of thick leather to the ledges and to the frame. Simple, quiet; renew every few years.
   - **Pintle hinges (all wood):** fix a rounded peg pointing up at the frame's bottom corner and down at its top, carve matching holes in extended door edges, and the door pivots wood-in-wood. Grease with fat.
4. **Make it shut and stay shut.** Peg on a wooden latch — a pivoting bar that drops into a notched block on the frame. A leather thong through a hole lifts it from outside; pull the thong in at night — that is a lock.
5. **Build the bed frame.** A rectangle of stout poles, longer than your tallest sleeper by a hand and an arm-and-a-half wide, on four legs about knee height. Notch the corners together and peg through; wedge the pegs.
6. **Fill the frame.** Lay split slats across, a finger-gap apart, or weave rope in a tight zigzag lattice pulled drum-tight (springier; re-tighten when it slackens).
7. **Make the mattress.** Stuff a cloth sack fat with dry straw or bracken — bone dry, or it moulds under you. No sack? A thick loose heap works; re-fluff daily, replace every few months.
8. **Make a three-legged stool.** Bore three holes through a thick slab seat, angled outward. Whittle three leg-tops to fit snug, drive them in, then split each protruding top and hammer in a thin wedge so the leg locks. Trim the legs until it sits level.
9. **Peg up a shelf.** Drive two stout pegs into a wall post at eye height, angled slightly upward, and lay a plank across. Everything you own now lives above the damp and the mice.

## Watch out

- **Three legs, not four, for the stool.** Three sit steady on any lumpy floor; four rock forever.
- **Green wood shrinks as it dries** — a joint tight today is loose in a month. A dry peg in a green hole grips hardest, because the hole shrinks onto it.
- **A brace sloping the wrong way shows only when the door sags.** Check twice: the low end points at the hinges.
- **A bed on the ground is barely a bed.** Below knee height you lose warmth to the earth and share your straw with everything that crawls.
- **Damp straw moulds fast,** and mouldy bedding means coughing all winter. Sun-dry the stuffing first; air the mattress monthly.
- **Test every seat with a shove before a person.** Lean your full weight on it and rock hard — furniture fails cheapest when it fails empty.

## How it works

The diagonal brace turns the floppy door into triangles, and a triangle cannot change shape without a side changing length — that is why braced things stay square. Wedged legs and pegs hold by squeeze, the wood gripping itself. And the bed works because earth pulls heat from a sleeping body far faster than the still air trapped in straw.
