A fire inside keeps you alive through winter — but only if it stays exactly where you put it, and its smoke leaves the house without you. A hearth is the fireproof floor the fire sits on. A chimney is the tall pipe that gives smoke its own door. Done right, the fire becomes the warm heart of the home; done wrong, it is a slow trap. Take the full week — nothing in a house deserves more care than the part that is on fire every day.

## You need

- **Fired bricks or dense stone** — for a floor pad, a three-sided fire box, and a chimney reaching above the roof. Never use river stones or stone that has lived in water — trapped moisture can make it burst when heated.
- **Clay mortar** — clean clay mixed with about twice as much sand, wetted to a stiff paste. The sandy mix handles heat; plain daub does for the outer chimney.
- **A spot against a wall or gable end** — so the chimney passes through the roof at the highest practical point.
- **Extra bricks or flat stone** — for the apron, where sparks will land.
- **A straight pole and string** — to check the chimney rises plumb (straight up); a leaning flue draws badly.

## Steps

1. **Lay the hearth pad first.** On firm, dry ground, lay bricks or flat stones in clay mortar: the fire box footprint plus an apron (a fireproof margin) reaching a full arm into the room. No wood or daub under or beside it.
2. **Build the fire box.** Raise three brick walls on the pad, knee- to waist-high, enclosing a space roughly an arm wide and half an arm deep. Angle the side walls slightly outward — splayed sides bounce heat at you instead of up the flue, and a shallow box warms the room where a deep one swallows its own heat.
3. **Narrow into a throat.** Above the fire box, step the brickwork inward until the opening is about one-tenth the area of the fireplace mouth — roughly two hand-spans across. The throat speeds the smoke upward.
4. **Build the flue straight up,** one ring of bricks at a time, checking plumb every few courses. Smooth the inside with clay — a rough flue catches soot and slows the draw (the upward pull of air).
5. **Keep all wood away from the flue.** Where the chimney passes rafters or wattle, leave a hand-width gap on all sides, packed with clay or stone — never anything that burns.
6. **Take the chimney high.** The top must clear the ridge (the roof's peak) by at least two feet — near thatch, go taller and cap it with a flat stone on small pillars, so sparks die before landing. Short chimneys puff smoke back inside.
7. **Dry it, then cure it.** Air-dry for several days, then burn small twig fires, a little bigger each day for three or four days. Damp clay meeting a roaring fire cracks — and cracks are how fires escape into walls.
8. **Test the draw.** Light a small smoky fire; smoke should pull cleanly up within a minute. If it spills into the room, open a door a crack — a sealed house starves the fire of air. If that fails, build the chimney taller.

## Watch out

- **Smoke that lingers kills quietly.** A dull fire with smoke hanging in the room makes carbon monoxide — a poison gas you cannot see or smell that puts people to sleep before it kills. Anyone headachy, dizzy, or strangely sleepy: everyone outside now, air the house, no fires until the chimney draws well. Never sleep in a closed room with a smouldering fire.
- **Never build the flue from wattle, wood, or daub-over-sticks.** People did, and their houses burned. Flame-touched parts are brick or stone, full stop.
- **Sparks on thatch end the house.** With a thatch roof: extra-tall capped chimney, dry wood only — wet or resinous wood spits sparks.
- **Check for glow, not just flame.** Once a season, during a hot fire, feel the wall behind the chimney. Too hot to keep your hand on? Rebuild that section thicker.
- **Soot builds up and soot burns.** Brush the flue yearly — a bundle of holly on a rope works.
- **A too-big fire box is not warmer** — it just pulls more warm room air up the flue.

## How it works

Hot air is lighter than cold, so the column of hot smoke in the flue floats upward, pulling fresh air into the fire behind it — that pull is the draw. A taller chimney pulls harder; the narrow throat speeds the smoke so gusts cannot shove it backward. The bricks soak up heat all evening and give it back all night.
