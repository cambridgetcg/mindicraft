Everything you have practised comes together here. A house is not one skill — it is a frame that stands, walls that block wind, a roof that sheds rain, a floor that stays dry, a fire that stays where it belongs, and a door that closes. Each part protects the others: the roof keeps rain off the mud walls, the walls hold the roof up, the stone base keeps the wood from rotting.

Work in order and do not rush the slow parts. Mud must dry. Posts must be firm before they carry weight. A season — roughly three months of steady work — is honest for one or two people building a room about 3 by 4 metres, which is enough to sleep, cook, and sit out a winter.

## You need

- **A good site** — flat or gently sloped ground, higher than the land around it so rain runs away, not into your floor. Not under a dead tree. Near water but above any flood line you can see or ask about.
- **Nine to twelve straight posts**, each about as thick as your leg (12–15 cm) and 3 metres long — these are the skeleton. Hardwood lasts longest; char the buried ends in a fire to slow rot.
- **Flat stones** for the base under each post and around the floor edge — stone does not rot, wood does.
- **Long flexible sticks** (hazel, willow, or any springy wood, finger-thick) — hundreds of them, for weaving the walls. This weave is called wattle.
- **Daub** — a mud plaster of roughly 2 parts clay-rich soil, 1 part sand, and a big handful of chopped straw per bucket, mixed with water to thick-porridge texture. The straw is the reinforcement; without it the mud cracks.
- **Your finished sub-skills**: a thatched roof plan, a hearth-and-chimney plan, and a door and furniture ready to fit.
- **Helpers for two days** — lifting the roof frame alone is how people get hurt.

## Steps

1. **Mark the rectangle.** Stretch string between pegs, about 3 by 4 metres. Check it is square: both diagonals must measure the same.
2. **Dig a drainage ditch** around the outside, one spade deep, sloping away downhill. Water is the house's main enemy — deal with it first.
3. **Set the posts.** Dig holes 60 cm deep at each corner, at the middle of each long wall, and either side of the doorway. Drop a flat stone in each hole, stand the post on it, check it is vertical, then pack earth and small stones back in hard, tamping every hand-width of fill.
4. **Tie the tops together.** Lash or peg horizontal beams along the post tops, about 2 metres up. Now the skeleton stands. Push it — it should not sway.
5. **Weave the walls.** Thread the flexible sticks horizontally between the posts, over-under-over, each row alternating with the last, like a giant basket. Leave gaps only for the doorway and one small window.
6. **Build the hearth and chimney.** Follow your hearth guide: stone or fired-clay only near fire, never bare wattle. Do this before the thatch and the daub, so the chimney passes cleanly through and the thatch can be laid well clear of it.
7. **Raise the roof and thatch it.** Follow your roof guide: rafters at 45 degrees or steeper so rain runs off, thatch laid bottom row first and kept at least an arm's length from the chimney. Make the roof overhang the walls by at least 40 cm all round — this eave is the umbrella that keeps rain off your mud.
8. **Daub the walls last, under the finished roof.** Press handfuls of the mud mix into the weave from both sides at once, so the layers grip each other through the gaps. Build up to about 5 cm thick per side. The roof now shields the drying mud from rain and hot sun — daub that dries too fast or gets rained on cracks and falls. Let it dry 1–2 weeks; fill cracks with more daub as they appear.
9. **Fit the door, lay the floor.** Hang the door so it opens inward. Ram the earth floor flat and hard in thin layers; a top layer of the same daub mix, dried, gives a smooth surface.
10. **Move in slowly.** Light small fires for the first week to dry the inside gently. Bring in bed and stool. Watch the roof in the first hard rain and fix any drip the same day.

## Watch out

- **Wood touching wet ground rots in a few years.** Always put stone between earth and timber, and keep the drainage ditch clear.
- **Daubing before the frame is rigid** cracks everything. Shake-test the frame first; brace it diagonally if it moves.
- **Fire is the killer.** Keep thatch at least an arm's length from any chimney surface, and never leave a new hearth burning unwatched.
- **A leaking roof ruins mud walls in one wet season.** Fix drips the day you see them, not later.
- **Building too big.** A small dry room beats a large unfinished one when winter comes. You can always add on.

## How it works

Each material does the one job it is best at: stone resists water, wood carries weight, woven sticks give the mud something to hold onto, straw stops the mud cracking, and thatch sheds rain while trapping warm air. The roof's wide overhang and the drainage ditch keep water away from everything else, which is why houses like this — wattle and daub — have stood for centuries.
