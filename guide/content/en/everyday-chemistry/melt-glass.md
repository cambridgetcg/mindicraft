Glass is melted sand — but there's a catch. Pure sand melts around 1,700 °C, far beyond any charcoal furnace. The secret the first glassmakers found is a **flux**: a substance that helps something else melt at a lower heat. Plant ash is that flux. Mixed with ash and a little lime, sand melts near 1,000–1,100 °C — just within reach of a kiln burning charcoal with air blown in hard. The reward: a material that holds any liquid without tainting it, and beads that have been real wealth in every age.

This sits at the very top of the fire tree. Expect failed melts, cracked crucibles, and grey gritty lumps before your first true glass. "A season of tries" is the plan, not a warning.

## You need

- **Clean quartz sand** — the whiter the better. Dark grains are iron, which turns glass green or black. Wash the sand, dry it, and pick it over. Crushed white quartz pebbles, pounded fine, are purer still.
- **Plant ash, sifted fine.** Ash from seashore plants, seaweed, or bracken is the richest flux; ordinary hardwood ash works but less well. Burn a large pile for a small bag of sifted ash — you need more than you'd guess.
- **A little burnt lime**, crushed fine. Without it, ash-glass slowly dissolves in water.
- **Charcoal, sacks of it** — one melt can eat a whole batch. Wood alone will not get there.
- **A small furnace with forced air**: your kiln rebuilt small and thick-walled, with air blown in at the base — bellows, or two people taking turns on blowpipes. Smaller chamber, fiercer heat.
- **Crucibles** — thick little cups of your most sand-rich, well-fired clay to hold the melt.
- **Tools**: long clay-coated rods for winding beads, green-wood tongs, and a bed of dry ash for slow cooling.

## Steps

1. **Mix the batch.** By weight (or careful equal scoops): about **2 parts sand, 1 part sifted ash, and ¼ part lime**. Mix until utterly even — every streak becomes a flaw.
2. **Fritting: half-cook it first.** Spread the mix in a shallow clay tray and bake it at a dull red glow — roughly 750–850 °C, ordinary kiln heat — for half a day. It fuses into a crumbly cake called **frit**, driving out gases early so the real melt has fewer bubbles.
3. **Grind the frit** back to coarse powder, picking out any black or gritty bits.
4. **Melt it.** Fill a crucible two-thirds full, set it in the hottest spot, and bring the fire up over hours to a fierce **bright yellow-orange glow** — 1,000–1,100 °C. Blow air steadily; this is a team job. Hold that heat four to eight hours, until the melt looks like glowing honey and a dipped rod pulls a smooth thread.
5. **Make beads.** Coat a rod tip in clay slip, dip it in the melt, and twirl — a gob winds around the rod. Keep twirling near the furnace mouth so it rounds itself, then slide the cooled bead off the clay.
6. **Make a small vessel by core-forming.** Shape a cup-shaped core of mud and dung on a rod, dry it, and trail threads of hot glass round it until covered. Smooth by rolling on a flat stone while soft; after the slow cooling in the next step, scrape the core out.
7. **Anneal everything** — annealing just means cooling it very slowly. Bury every finished piece in a pot of hot ash beside the furnace to cool overnight — the slower the better. Glass cooled fast cracks itself apart within hours.

## Watch out

- **This heat maims.** Molten glass sticks to skin and keeps burning. Work with long tools, dry ground (water plus melt spatters violently), no loose clothing, and a clear path behind you. Cool any burn at once in clean cool water for a long ten minutes, and get real medical care when you can.
- **Don't stare into the glow** — furnace-glare damages eyes over time. Glance, don't gaze; check the melt through slitted lids or a bark pinhole.
- **Grey, gritty, or crusty melt**: not hot enough, not long enough, or too little ash — more air, more hours, or a richer batch.
- **Cracked crucibles drain your melt into the fire.** Fire new crucibles empty first, heat them gently, keep a spare.
- **Green glass isn't failure** — it's iron in the sand; use whiter sand next batch. Bubbles mean it needed longer molten or better fritting.

## How it works

Ash is rich in soda and potash, which wedge between the quartz grains' locked atoms and let them slide at far lower heat. Sand-and-soda glass alone slowly washes away in water; lime locks it so it lasts centuries. And glass shrinks as it cools: cool one side faster than the other and it tears itself apart.
