A raw animal skin is meat, and meat rots. Dried raw, it turns to stiff board that softens and stinks the first time it gets wet. **Tanning** changes the skin itself, using **tannin** — the bitter, mouth-puckering stuff in tree bark — so it can never rot again. Tanned skin is **leather**: shoes, bags, belts, straps, water carriers. Nothing else you can make takes years of hard use the way leather does.

The work is simple but slow, and the skin sets the schedule. Most of the month is waiting; your job is to check it every day or two and never rush a stage.

## You need

- **A fresh hide**, worked as soon as possible after skinning. If you must wait, cover the flesh side thickly with salt or dry it flat in shade. A warm wet hide left overnight is often already too far gone.
- **Tannin-rich bark, a lot** — oak is best; chestnut, willow, spruce, acacia, and hemlock also work — hemlock here means the big evergreen *tree*, not the small white-flowered poison-hemlock plant, which is deadly to chew. Chew a sliver: a strong dry, puckering bitterness means tannin. You want roughly as much dry bark by weight as the wet hide weighs — a big basketful, pounded to crumbs, per medium hide.
- **A smooth log (the beam)**, thigh-thick, propped at an angle to drape the hide over.
- **A blunt scraper** — a flat stone edge or split bone, dull enough not to cut. Sharp blades ruin hides.
- **Wood ash** from a clean fire, to make the soak that loosens hair — a double handful per bucket to start.
- **Two large watertight containers** — big pots, a dugout trough, or a lined pit. Wood, stone, or clay; avoid iron, which reacts with tannin and blackens the leather.
- **Rendered fat or tallow** for softening at the end — a fist-sized lump for a deerskin.

## Steps

1. **Flesh the hide the same day.** Drape it flesh-side up over the beam and push the scraper away from you, stripping off every scrap of fat, meat, and the thin filmy membrane. Anything left will rot — and worse, it blocks the tannin: a patch of fat is a wall the bark water cannot cross, so that patch stays raw skin forever. Work until the flesh side is one even, faintly rough surface with no shine of fat left.
2. **Soak off the hair.** Stir wood ash into water — roughly one part ash to five of water — until a fresh egg (or a potato) just floats a coin-width above the surface. The more ash dissolves, the heavier the water becomes, so a floating egg is your sign that enough has gone in to make the soak strong — and that strength is what swells the skin and loosens the hair roots. Sink the hide, stir daily. In 3 to 7 days in mild weather the hair slips out when you push it with your thumb; test daily from day three, because leaving it too long starts to eat the skin itself.
3. **Scrape both sides.** Back over the beam: push off all the hair and the thin grey outer skin (the **scarf skin**) under it, baring the smooth **grain** beneath, then flip and give the flesh side a final pass. Work every hand-width; missed patches tan badly. The clean hide should feel like thick, even, slightly rubbery cloth, the same pale colour edge to edge.
4. **Rinse for two days.** The ash-water is caustic and must all come out, or it fights the tannin and keeps the leather harsh. A stream is perfect; otherwise change the water several times a day until the hide feels slippery-clean, not soapy, and the swollen thickness relaxes back down. Working it over the beam between rinses squeezes the old ash-water out of the middle.
5. **Start the bark soak weak.** Fill a container with water and a few double-handfuls of pounded bark; let it steep a day into a pale tea the colour of weak apple juice. Sink the hide, weight it under so no part floats into the air, and move it about daily so no fold is missed. It must start weak — this is the single rule that saves or ruins the whole month.
6. **Strengthen the soak every few days.** Each time, stir in more pounded bark, or dip fresh tea from a second steeping pot. By the end of the first week the water should be the brown of strong tea; by the second week, dark like coffee. Fresh bark spends its tannin in a few days — a sour or flat smell and a paling colour both mean the soak is used up and wants feeding.
7. **Wait, and test.** A thin small skin takes 2 to 4 weeks; a thick hide takes 2 to 3 months. The tannin walks inward slowly, a hair's width a day, so patience is not optional. Test by cutting a sliver from an edge: tanned leather is an even brown the whole way through, with no pale raw streak in the middle. A pale core means the tannin has not reached the centre — sink it back and keep soaking.
8. **Rinse, oil, and work it soft.** Rinse a day, then let it dry only until damp — bendy and cool to the cheek, not wet, not dry. Rub warm fat or tallow into both sides until it stops drinking it in. Then work it: pull it back and forth over the beam edge, stretch it every direction, over and over as it dries — never walk away and let it dry untouched. Softness comes from this constant working, not from the fat alone.

## Watch out

- **Rot is the great enemy, and it is a race.** A sweet-foul smell, slimy patches, or hair slipping before it should means bacteria are winning the skin before you have preserved it. The save is speed and cold: get the hide into the ash soak or the bark liquor sooner, keep every soak cool and shaded, and never leave a raw hide warm and wet. Salt buys time if you cannot work at once.
- **Sharp tools cut holes.** One nick becomes a tear in the finished leather, because tanning does not heal a cut — it locks it in. Keep the scraper blunt and press the flat of the tool, not an edge.
- **Ash-water and quicklime burn skin and eyes — take this seriously.** The ash soak is a mild lye, and some tanners use stronger **lye** (from soaking wood ash) or **quicklime** (burnt limestone) to loosen hair faster. All of these eat living skin the way they eat the hair. Wear eye protection — even a splash can blind. If you mix a lye or lime solution, always add the **lye or lime INTO the water, never water into the lye**: water hitting concentrated lye can spit boiling caustic back at your face. Handle the hide with a stick or covered hands. On skin contact, flush with lots and lots of clean water for many minutes; in the eyes, flush long and gently and do not stop early. Keep a bucket of clean rinse water beside you before you start.
- **A too-strong first bark soak seals the outside of the hide.** Strong tannin binds the outer fibres instantly into a tight skin the water can no longer pass through — so you get tanned leather wrapped around raw, rotting skin in the middle, and no more soaking can fix it. Weak tea first, always, so the tannin walks all the way in before the outer layer closes.
- **Iron stains blacken the leather.** Tannin plus iron makes ink — good for writing, bad for a clean hide. An iron pot, a nail in the trough, or iron-rich mud will leave dark blotches, so use wood, stone, or clay vessels.
- **Drying stiff isn't failure.** Leave tanned leather to dry untouched and it goes board-hard, because the fibres bond as they dry. Damp it and work it again over the beam; the bonds are young and break easily. Every hide takes several rounds of this.

## How it works

Picture the skin up close. It is built almost entirely of one protein, **collagen**, shaped like countless tiny rope-ladders — long twisted strands with rungs between them, packed side by side into a dense felt. Two things happen to those ladders in raw skin, both disasters. Bacteria find the rungs delicious and chew them apart: that is rot. And when the skin dries, the ladders fall against each other and glue tight, so the whole felt sets into one stiff board: that is rawhide.

Tannin is the fix, and it does one simple thing. A tannin molecule is a little bundle with several sticky arms. When it meets the collagen ladders it grabs a rung on one ladder with one arm and a rung on the next ladder with another — tying the rungs together with a permanent knot. Do this millions of times and every ladder is lashed to its neighbours by knots that never come undone. **That permanent tying is what tanning is.** Now the rot bacteria arrive and find the rungs already knotted — they cannot get a grip. And when the leather dries, the ladders cannot glue flat, because the tannin knots hold them a fixed distance apart like spacers. Rot-proof and it never sets stiff: both problems solved by one knot.

This picture explains every fiddly rule. **Why scrape so hard?** Tannin only works where it can touch collagen, and fat and membrane are walls it cannot cross — a missed patch of fat stays raw forever. **Why weak tea, growing stronger over weeks?** Tannin walks into the felt slowly, tying knots at the surface first. Hit it with strong tea at once and the surface ties into a tight seal that blocks the rest, so you tan a skin around a rotten core. Start weak and the tannin reaches the middle before the outside closes. **Why cut an edge to test?** A cut shows you the middle: even brown means the knots reach the centre; a pale streak is raw, unknotted collagen. **Why work it soft while it dries?** As it dries the ladders still try to glue, and every stretch snaps those new stiff bonds while they are young — so it dries supple instead of board-hard.

There is another road to the same place. **Brain-tan** skips bark: you soak the scraped hide in a mush of animal brains or egg yolk — both rich in fine oils — so the oils coat every ladder and keep them sliding instead of gluing. Then you hang the soft skin in cool **smoke**. Smoke carries **aldehydes**, small molecules that, like tannin, tie the collagen rungs together — making the softness permanent, so the leather dries soft even after a soaking. Bark ties the ladders with tannin knots; smoke ties them with aldehyde knots. Both do the one true thing: lock the rope-ladders together so rot cannot grab and water cannot glue.

## Go further

- **Try a jar first, not a whole hide.** Tan a scrap the size of your hand in a jar of strong oak-bark tea. In a week you can cut it and see the brown creep inward from both faces toward a pale middle — the whole month made visible and small.
- **Taste the tannin map.** Chew slivers of oak, willow, spruce, and any local bark and rank them by how hard they pucker your mouth. The stronger the pucker, the faster the tan and the fewer weeks of waiting.
- **Walk the brain-tan road.** Do a small buckskin by brain and smoke instead of bark. It teaches the same lesson from the other side — oils and smoke instead of tannin — and gives a softer, cloth-like leather.
- **Make ink from a mistake.** Steep a rusty nail in leftover bark tea and watch it go black — the tannin-plus-iron stain you avoid on a hide, turned into the ancestor of writing ink.
- **What it unlocks.** Once you can tan, you can make anything that must bend, hold, and last wet: waterskins, pouches, harness, shoe soles, drum heads, and the cordage that binds tools. Leather is the strap that holds a made world together.
- **Sibling crafts.** *Clean your catch* teaches the fleshing hand this craft begins with; *stone tools* gives you the blunt scraper and the beam-edge that do the soft-working; and any guide on rendering fat feeds you the tallow for the final step.
