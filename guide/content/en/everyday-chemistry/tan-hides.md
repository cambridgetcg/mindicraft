---
title: Tan a hide into leather
summary: Scrape a skin clean, soak it in crushed-bark water for weeks, and work it soft into leather that lasts years.
difficulty: hard
time: a month, mostly waiting
needs: [clean-your-catch, stone-tools]
---

A raw animal skin is meat, and meat rots. Dried raw, it turns to stiff board that softens and stinks the first time it gets wet. **Tanning** changes the skin itself, using **tannin** — the bitter, mouth-puckering stuff in tree bark — so it can never rot again. Tanned skin is **leather**: shoes, bags, belts, straps, water carriers. Nothing else you can make takes years of hard use the way leather does.

The work is simple but slow, and the skin sets the schedule. Most of the month is waiting; your job is to check it every day or two and never rush a stage.

## You need

- **A fresh hide**, worked as soon as possible after skinning. If you must wait, cover the flesh side thickly with salt or dry it flat in shade.
- **Tannin-rich bark, a lot** — oak is best; chestnut, willow, spruce, acacia, and hemlock also work — hemlock here means the big evergreen *tree*, not the small white-flowered poison-hemlock plant, which is deadly to chew. Chew a sliver: a strong dry, puckering bitterness means tannin. You want a big basketful, pounded to crumbs, per medium hide.
- **A smooth log (the beam)**, thigh-thick, propped at an angle to drape the hide over.
- **A blunt scraper** — a flat stone edge or split bone, dull enough not to cut. Sharp blades ruin hides.
- **Wood ash** from a clean fire, to make the soak that loosens hair.
- **Two large watertight containers** — big pots, a dugout trough, or a lined pit.
- **Rendered fat or tallow** for softening at the end.

## Steps

1. **Flesh the hide the same day.** Drape it flesh-side up over the beam and push the scraper away from you, stripping off every scrap of fat, meat, and the thin filmy membrane. Anything left will rot.
2. **Soak off the hair.** Stir wood ash into water — roughly one part ash to five of water — until a fresh egg (or a potato) just floats in it. Sink the hide in it, stir daily. In 3 to 7 days, the hair slips out easily when you push it with your thumb.
3. **Scrape both sides.** Back over the beam: push off all the hair and the thin grey outer skin layer under it, then flip and give the flesh side a final pass. Work every hand-width; missed patches tan badly.
4. **Rinse for two days.** The ash-water is caustic and must all come out. A stream is perfect; otherwise change the water several times a day until the hide feels slippery-clean, not soapy.
5. **Start the bark soak weak.** Fill a container with water and a few double-handfuls of pounded bark; let it steep a day into a pale tea. Sink the hide, weight it under, and move it about daily so no fold is missed.
6. **Strengthen the soak every few days.** Each time, add more pounded bark until, by the second week, the bark water is dark like strong tea.
7. **Wait, and test.** A thin small skin takes 2 to 4 weeks; a thick hide takes 2 to 3 months. Test by cutting a sliver from an edge: tanned leather is brown the whole way through, with no pale raw streak in the middle. Pale core — keep soaking.
8. **Rinse, oil, and work it soft.** Rinse a day, then let it dry only until damp. Rub warm fat or tallow into both sides. Then work it: pull it back and forth over the beam edge, stretch it every direction, over and over as it dries. Softness comes from this working, not from the fat alone.

## Watch out

- **Rot is the great enemy.** A sweet-foul smell, slimy patches, or hair slipping before it should means bacteria are winning. Get it into the ash soak or the bark liquor sooner, keep soaks cool and shaded, and never leave a raw hide warm and wet.
- **Sharp tools cut holes.** One nick becomes a tear in the finished leather. Keep the scraper blunt and your strokes flat.
- **Ash-water burns skin and eyes.** Handle the hide in it with a stick or covered hands; rinse splashes off at once, and flush eyes long with clean water.
- **A too-strong first bark soak seals the outside of the hide** — leather outside, rot inside. Weak tea first, always.
- **Drying stiff isn't failure.** Damp it and work it again over the beam. Every hide takes several rounds.

## How it works

Skin is a mesh of protein fibres. Tannin molecules from the bark bind onto those fibres and tie them together, so the bacteria that eat raw skin can no longer get a grip. The ash soak dissolves the glue holding the hairs, and the fat plus stretching keeps the tanned fibres sliding past each other instead of gluing into a board.
