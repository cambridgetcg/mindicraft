---
title: Mix mortar, plaster, and whitewash
summary: Blend lime with sand and water to glue stones together, seal walls smooth, and paint rooms bright and clean.
difficulty: steady
time: two days
needs: [burn-lime]

Lime is one material that does three jobs. Mixed thick with sand, it is **mortar** — the glue that holds stones and bricks into a wall. Mixed with finer sand and spread thin, it is **plaster** — a smooth skin that seals a rough wall against wind and dirt. Thinned with water alone, it is **whitewash** — a bright white paint that dries hard, fights mould, and makes a dark room feel twice as light.

All three work the same way: soft lime paste slowly drinks a gas from the air and turns back into limestone — the very rock you burned to make it. You are spreading liquid stone.

## You need

- **Slaked lime, as a wet putty.** This is burnt lime that has already been mixed with water and left to soak. If yours is still dry burnt lime (quicklime), slake it first: add the lumps *to* water, never water to lumps, in a pit or tub — it boils and spits on its own. Let the putty rest at least a day; a week is better. Old, rested putty is smoother and stickier.
- **Sharp sand** — sand with gritty, angular grains that crunch, not soft rounded dust. River or pit sand, sieved of pebbles. For plaster, sieve it finer still.
- **Clean water.**
- **A mixing board or shallow pit**, a hoe or sturdy stick, and a flat wooden trowel or paddle.
- **Eye protection and something to cover your hands** — wet lime burns skin slowly and eyes fast.
- **A brush of bound grass or hair** for whitewash.

## Steps

1. **Mix mortar: 1 part lime putty to 3 parts sand.** Measure with the same bucket or basket for both. Chop and press them together on the board until every grain is coated and the colour is even — no white streaks. Add water only by the splash; good mortar stands in stiff peaks like thick porridge.
2. **Lay stones with it.** Wet the stones first — dry stone sucks the water out of mortar and kills it. Spread a thumb-thick bed, press the stone in, and scrape off what squeezes out. Fill every gap; hollow joints let water in later.
3. **Mix plaster: 1 part putty to 2½–3 parts fine sand**, wetter than mortar — like soft butter. Dampen the wall, then spread a first rough coat about as thick as your little finger. Scratch its surface with a stick in a criss-cross so the next coat grips. Let it firm up for a day or two, then spread a thinner second coat and smooth it with the wooden trowel in long strokes.
4. **Mix whitewash: thin lime putty with water until it looks like milk.** It will seem uselessly watery — that is correct. A handful of salt dissolved in, or a splash of boiled hide glue, helps it stick and not rub off.
5. **Brush whitewash on in several thin coats,** letting each dry a day. It goes on see-through and grey; it dries white. Two to four coats make a bright, even wall — inside rooms, over plaster, on animal-shed walls.
6. **Cure everything slowly and damp.** For the first week, shield fresh mortar and plaster from hot sun, drying wind, rain, and frost — hang mats or brush over it, and mist it with water if it pales and dries too fast. Lime that dries out before it has hardened stays weak forever.

## Watch out

- **Lime burns.** Wet lime on skin feels like nothing at first, then burns hours later. Rinse skin promptly. In an eye it is an emergency: flush gently with clean water for at least 20 minutes, keep rinsing on the way, and get real medical care when you can.
- **Never pour water onto a pile of quicklime.** It can spatter boiling, caustic paste. Always add the lime to plenty of water.
- **Frost kills fresh lime.** One freezing night turns uncured mortar to crumbs. Work in mild weather only.
- **Too much sand makes weak, crumbly mortar; too little makes it shrink and crack.** Stay near 1 to 3.
- **Lime mortar hardens over weeks, not hours.** Don't load a fresh wall heavily for at least a week; full strength takes a season. Patience is part of the recipe.

## How it works

Slaked lime hardens by pulling carbon dioxide — the gas you breathe out — from the air, which turns the paste back into calcium carbonate: limestone. That is why it must stay slightly damp and exposed to air while curing, and why thin coats beat thick ones — air can only reach so deep. The sand is the skeleton; the lime is the stone glue growing around it.
