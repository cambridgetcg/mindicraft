summary: Blend lime with sand and water to glue stones together, seal walls smooth, and paint rooms bright and clean.

Lime is one material that does three jobs. Mixed thick with sand, it is **mortar** — the glue that holds stones and bricks into a wall. Mixed with finer sand and spread thin, it is **plaster** — a smooth skin that seals a rough wall against wind and dirt. Thinned with water alone, it is **whitewash** — a bright white paint that dries hard, fights mould, and makes a dark room feel twice as light.

All three are the same trick at different thicknesses. Soft lime paste slowly drinks carbon dioxide, a gas in the air (the same one you breathe out), and turns into limestone — the very rock you burned to make it. Mortar does not dry the way mud dries. It **cures** — it becomes stone. You are spreading liquid rock; over the following weeks the whole wall quietly turns to stone around your stones.

This is also why the craft forgives you: a lime wall heals its own hairline cracks and bends a little instead of snapping. Builders trusted it for eight thousand years, and much of what they built is still standing.

## You need

- **Slaked lime, as a wet putty.** This is burnt lime already soaked with water. If yours is still dry burnt lime (quicklime), slake it first — carefully. Wear eye protection. Add the lumps *to* plenty of water, never water to the lumps: the reaction boils and spits, and water poured onto a pile can spatter boiling caustic paste into your face. Stir with a long stick, standing upwind of the steam. Then let the putty rest with a thin layer of water standing on top of it, like a lid — a day at minimum, a week far better. Good putty feels like thick cream cheese, no gritty bits.
- **Sharp sand** — grains gritty and angular, so they crunch between your fingers, not soft rounded dust. Angular grains lock; round ones roll. River or pit sand, sieved of pebbles, with mixed grain sizes: coarse for the frame, fine to fill the gaps. For plaster, sieve finer still.
- **Clean sand, too.** Swirl a handful in a jar of water: if the water stays muddy-brown long, the sand carries clay — wash it first, or the clay coats the grains and the lime cannot grip them.
- **Clean water.**
- **A mixing board or shallow pit**, a hoe or sturdy stick, and a flat wooden trowel or paddle (a wooden float).
- **Eye protection and something to cover your hands** — wet lime burns skin slowly and eyes fast.
- **A brush of bound grass or hair** for whitewash, and old mats or leafy branches to shade fresh work.

## Steps

1. **Mix mortar: 1 part lime putty to 3 parts sand.** Measure with the same bucket for both (a bucket of putty is roughly ten litres). Chop, press, and beat them together on the board; this beating ("knocking up") spreads the lime glue onto every sand grain and wakes the mix into stickiness, so work it far longer than feels necessary — a few hundred strokes. Done: even colour, no white streaks, standing in stiff peaks like thick porridge. Add water only by the splash; a mix that slumps and shines is carrying extra water, and will shrink when that water later leaves. The trowel test: a dollop clings to the upside-down trowel for a breath before falling.
2. **Lay stones with it.** Wet the stones first — a dry stone drinks the water out of the mortar and starves the cure. Spread a bed about a thumb thick, press and tap the stone down until mortar squeezes from the joint, then scrape off the squeeze and press it back into the gaps. Fill every joint solid; a hollow joint is a rain-pipe into the heart of the wall. When the mortar firms to leather — a thumbnail marks it, a thumb no longer sinks in — tamp the joints firm with a stick and scrub them over with a stiff brush: packed tight but left slightly open-grained. A trowel-polished skin looks neat but seals out the very air the cure needs.
3. **Mix plaster: 1 part putty to 2½–3 parts fine sand**, a little wetter than mortar — like soft butter. Dampen the wall. Spread a first rough coat as thick as your little finger, no thicker, pressing hard so it keys into every hollow — squeezes into the dips and locks there, the way fingers grip handholds. Before it stiffens, scratch it criss-cross with a stick — teeth for the next coat to bite. Let it firm for a day or two until a thumb-press barely marks it but a splash still darkens it, then spread a thinner second coat and smooth it with the wooden float in long, flat strokes. Two thin coats beat one thick one; you will see why below.
4. **Mix whitewash: thin lime putty with water until it looks like milk.** It will seem uselessly watery — that is correct. Thick whitewash rubs off as powder, because the air cannot cure that deep; a thin coat cures right through, and bonds. A handful of salt, or a splash of boiled hide glue or melted tallow, helps it stick and shed rain.
5. **Brush whitewash on in several thin coats,** letting each dry a day. Keep the eye cover on — a brush worked overhead flicks caustic drops into upturned faces. It goes on see-through and greyish and looks like a mistake; overnight it cures white. Two to four coats make a bright, even wall — inside rooms, over plaster, on animal-shed and dairy walls.
6. **Cure everything slowly and damp — this is a chore, not a wait.** For the first week, shield fresh work from hot sun, drying wind, rain, and frost with mats or leafy branches. Visit twice a day; if the surface has gone pale and dusty, mist it until it darkens. Lime that dries out before it has cured stays weak forever — it must stay faintly damp while it slowly eats air. Firm in days; hard in weeks; full stone strength over a season.

## Watch out

- **Lime burns.** Wet lime on skin feels like nothing at first, then burns hours later. The save is water, promptly and plenty: rinse well as soon as you notice. In an eye it is an emergency — flush gently with clean water for at least **thirty minutes**, keep rinsing on the way, and get real medical care when you can.
- **Never pour water onto a pile of quicklime.** The heat can flash the first splash to steam and throw boiling caustic paste. Always lime into water, eyes covered, long stick.
- **Frost kills fresh lime.** Water freezing inside uncured mortar bursts it from within; one cold night turns a week's work to crumbs. Work in mild weather; if a surprise frost threatens, pile dry mats or straw over it.
- **Drying too fast kills it more slowly.** Sun and wind pull the water out before the cure happens, leaving a chalky surface that powders under your thumb. Caught early, mist it damp and shade it. Truly dried-out mortar cannot be revived — rake the powdery joint out and refill it fresh.
- **Too much sand makes crumbly mortar; too little makes it crack.** Starved of glue, the grains fall apart; drowned in glue, the mix shrinks and splits into a net of fine cracks (crazing). Stay near 1 to 3. A crazed first plaster coat can be scratched and covered; a crazed finish coat must be redone.
- **Dirty sand is invisible sabotage.** Clay-coated grains never bond; the wall sheds sand years later. Jar-test before mixing a big batch.
- **Thick coats stay soft inside forever.** Air can only creep so deep; a fat joint or thick coat cures outside and stays cheese in the middle. Build up in thin layers.
- **Unslaked lumps in young putty pop later** — they slake inside the finished wall, swell, and blow little craters in your plaster. The save is patience: rest the putty long, and sieve it.
- **Don't load a fresh wall.** Lime hardens over weeks, not hours. Keep beams and roof weight off for at least a week. Patience is part of the recipe.

## How it works

Lime lives in a circle, and this craft is its homeward half. Limestone is a rock called calcium carbonate — calcium bound to carbon dioxide, the gas you breathe out. Burning it drove that gas off and left quicklime; slaking added water. Now, spread on your wall, the paste does the last step: it pulls carbon dioxide back out of the air and turns into calcium carbonate again. Your wall does not dry into hardness. It *becomes limestone* — the same rock you started with, re-grown between your stones as a lace of interlocking crystals.

That is why "keep it damp" is law. The gas cannot react with dry powder; it must first dissolve into a thin film of water inside the paste, and only there does it turn the lime to stone. Bone dry, nothing happens — ever. Soaking wet, no air gets in. Faintly damp is the open door. And air creeps inward slowly — the stone-front advances at fingernail pace, about as slowly as your nails grow — which is why thin coats cure through and thick ones stay cheese in the middle.

Why the sand? Sand is the bones; lime is only the glue between the grains. Lime paste alone shrinks badly as its water leaves, and cracks apart; sand grains do not shrink at all. In a good mix they lean grain against grain like a dry-stone wall in miniature — coarse grains framing, fine grains filling the gaps — and the lime only fills the last thin spaces and glues the contact points. One part lime to three parts sand is roughly the glue that just fills the voids between the bones. The wall carries its weight through grain touching grain; the lime merely forbids them to slide.

Here is the quiet magic: a lime wall heals itself. Even cured, it keeps a little free, uncured lime. Rainwater dissolves a trace of it, carries it into any hairline crack, and there — meeting air — it cures into fresh limestone. The crack scabs over with new stone. This, plus a slight softness, is why lime forgives what cement punishes: buildings move with heat, frost, and settling ground; rigid cement cracks through and stays cracked, while lime flexes, breathes damp out through its pores, and re-seals its small wounds. Mud, at the other end, never turns to stone at all — rain simply softens it back.

Whitewash is this whole story told one grain thick: each milky coat cures into a paper-thin sheet of limestone. And because fresh lime is fiercely caustic — the same bite that burns skin — it kills mould and germs on contact, which is why barns, dairies, and sickrooms were painted with it.

## Go further

- **Prove the air-eating today.** Two blobs of lime putty in two jars: seal one, leave one open. Weeks later the open blob is stone-crusted, the sealed one still soft. It never needed to dry — it needed to breathe.
- **Watch a cure with your thumbnail.** Scratch a hidden corner of fresh plaster once a day: cheese, leather, eggshell, stone — you are feeling the limestone front creep inward.
- **Strengthen it the old ways:** chopped animal hair kneaded into plaster stitches it against crazing; earth pigments in whitewash give soft, permanent colour that cures into the stone itself.
- **The waterproof secret:** crushed fired brick ground into the mix lets lime harden even under water — the Roman trick for cisterns and harbours. Try a brick-dust patch in a water trough.
- **Where it leads:** chimneys and ovens, grain stores lined dry and bright, building in fired brick — the kiln craft next door. Revisit lime-burning once you've cured a wall: the circle makes more sense when you've walked both halves.
