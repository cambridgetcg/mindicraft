---
title: Make soap from fat and lye
summary: Cook fat and lye together — carefully — until they become the soap that keeps your whole village healthier.
difficulty: steady
time: two days, plus weeks of curing
needs: [ash-lye, render-fat]
---

Soap is what happens when fat meets lye. Cook the two together and they turn into something new: a slippery substance that grabs grease on one end and water on the other, so dirt and germs rinse away instead of smearing around.

Do not skip this craft. Washing hands with soap prevents more sickness than almost anything else you can make. A pot of soap is quiet medicine for the whole village.

## You need

- **Strong lye water** — brown liquid from dripping water through wood ash. It must pass the float test: a fresh egg or small potato floats with a coin-sized patch showing above the surface. Weak lye makes greasy sludge, not soap.
- **Clean rendered fat** — tallow or lard, melted and strained so no meat scraps remain. Roughly one pot of fat to one pot of strong lye water is a fair starting point; you will adjust by testing as you cook.
- **A big pot** — iron or fired clay, filled no more than half way. Never aluminium; lye eats it.
- **A long stirring stick** — arm's length, so spatters miss you.
- **A fire you can control** — you want a gentle boil, not a roar.
- **Salt** (optional) — a few handfuls, if you want hard bars instead of soft soap.
- **A mold** — a cloth-lined box, a basket, or a wide dish.
- **Protection** — covered arms, something over your eyes if you have it, children well away.

## Steps

1. Set the pot outside, on a fire burned to steady coals. Pour in the lye water and bring it to a gentle boil.
2. Add the fat. Stir until it melts in.
3. Boil gently and stir, on and off, for several hours — often 3 to 6. The mix goes cloudy, then creamy, then thick like porridge. Add small splashes of water if it gets too thick to stir.
4. Test whether it is done:
   - Drip some from the stick across the surface. If the drip leaves a visible trail before sinking in, it is close.
   - Take out a pea-sized blob, cool it fully, wet it, and rub it between two fingers. If it lathers a little and does not sting, it is soap. If it stings or burns, there is still raw lye in it — rinse your fingers well and keep cooking.
5. Fix problems as you cook:
   - Stays greasy, fat floating on top: the lye was too weak. Add stronger lye, keep cooking.
   - Still stings after long cooking: too much lye. Add small pieces of fat, one at a time.
6. When it passes the finger test, you have **soft soap** — a brown jelly. Scoop it into a lidded pot. It is ready for laundry, dishes, and hands.
7. For **hard bars**: stir a few handfuls of salt into the hot soap. It will curdle and float as thick curds. Skim the curds into the cloth-lined mold and press them down.
8. Cure the bars: keep them in airy shade for 3 to 6 weeks, turning them now and then. They grow harder and milder every week. A day or two of rest helps even soft soap.

## Watch out

- **Boiling lye and fat spatter.** Every drop is caustic and hot. Long stick, long sleeves, stand back, work outside.
- **Soap boils over fast.** It foams up like milk. Keep the pot half full at most and be ready to pull it off the heat.
- **Fresh soap looks like food** — brown pudding in a pot. Mark it, store it high, warn everyone.
- **Harsh soap that stings skin is not finished.** Cure it longer, or melt it back down with a little more fat. Never use stinging soap on a child.
- **If raw mix gets on skin**, rinse with lots of plain water right away. In an eye: rinse gently for at least 15 minutes, then get real medical care when you can.

## How it works

Lye splits fat into new pieces. Each piece has a tail that loves grease and a head that loves water. When you wash, the tails bury into the dirt and oil on your skin, the heads hold to the water, and the whole mess lifts off and rinses away. Cooking uses up the lye and the fat both — good soap keeps neither bite nor grease.
