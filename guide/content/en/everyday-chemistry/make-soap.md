---
title: Make soap from fat and lye
summary: Cook fat and lye together — carefully — until they become the soap that keeps your whole village healthier.
difficulty: steady
time: two days, plus weeks of curing
needs: [ash-lye, render-fat]
---

Soap is what happens when fat meets lye. Cook the two together and they become something new: a slippery substance that grabs grease on one end and water on the other, so dirt and germs rinse away instead of smearing around.

Do not skip this craft. Washing hands with soap prevents more sickness than almost anything else you can make. A pot of soap is quiet medicine for the whole village.

There is real chemistry hiding in this pot — a molecule cut apart and rebuilt. Once you see it, in "How it works," every choice in this recipe will make sense.

## You need

- **Strong lye water — the caustic grade, not plain ash water.** It must pass **both** tests, not one: a fresh egg or small potato floats with a coin-sized patch showing, **and** a drop of it goes in silent when you stir it into a spoon of vinegar. This matters more than anything else on this list. Plain leached ash lye passes the float test and will still fail you — it is potash, a weak alkali that mostly smears fat around instead of cutting it. What turns it into the real thing is the ash-lye guide's upgrade: stir in slaked lime, let the white mud settle, ladle off the clear liquid above. Fizz in the vinegar means untraded potash is left and it is not ready — take it back to the lime, not to the fire.
- **Clean rendered fat** — tallow (beef or sheep fat) or lard (pig fat), melted and strained so no meat scraps remain. Roughly one pot of fat to one pot of strong lye water is a fair start — perhaps 2 to 3 litres of each for a family batch — but you will adjust by testing as you cook, not by trusting the measure.
- **A big pot** — iron or fired clay, filled no more than half way. Never aluminium; lye eats it.
- **A long stirring stick** — arm's length at least, so spatters miss you.
- **A fire you can control** — a gentle simmer you can walk away from, not a roar.
- **Salt** (optional) — a few handfuls, for hard bars instead of soft soap.
- **A mold** — a cloth-lined box, basket, or wide dish; the cloth lets you lift the block out later.
- **Protection** — covered arms, something over your eyes (improvise if you must — any clear pane or visor beats bare eyes; a splash of hot lye can blind), a bucket of plain water within reach, and children well away.

## Steps

1. **Set up outside**, on a fire burned down to steady coals. Pour the lye water into the pot and bring it to a gentle boil. Remember the rule for all lye work: when mixing dry lye and water, add the **lye into the water**, never water into lye — water splashed onto strong lye can spit boiling caustic back at you.
2. **Add the fat** in fist-sized lumps and stir until it melts in. At first it just floats — a greasy layer on brown water. That is normal; your stirring forces the two to meet.
3. **Boil gently and stir**, on and off, for several hours — often 3 to 6. Each change tells you where you are:
   - First **cloudy**, like dirty dishwater: the lye has started cutting the fat.
   - Then **creamy**, like thin porridge, no longer separating when you rest the stick. The sharp lye smell fades to a plain fatty one. More than halfway.
   - Then **thick**, like stiff porridge that mounds up before sinking. Splash in water whenever it gets too thick to stir — burnt soap smells scorched and turns dark.
4. **Test whether it is done.** Two tests, use both:
   - **The trail test.** Drip some from the stick across the surface. If the drip leaves a visible trail — a raised line that sits a moment before sinking in — it is close. Soapmakers call this "trace."
   - **The finger test.** Take a pea-sized blob, cool it fully, wet it, rub it between two fingers. If it lathers a little and does not sting, it is soap. If it stings, there is still raw lye in it — rinse your fingers well and keep cooking. (Old soapmakers touch a cooled crumb to the tongue: finished soap just tastes soapy; unfinished soap gives a sharp "zap" like licking a battery. The finger test is safer; if you ever try the zap test, use a fully cooled crumb, spit, and rinse.)
5. **Fix problems as you cook:**
   - Stays greasy, fat floating after hours: your lye is not caustic. Test a drop in vinegar before you do anything else. If it fizzes, it is still potash, and adding more of it will never make soap — no amount of a weak alkali does a strong alkali's work. Take it back to the ash-lye guide's lime upgrade and cook this pot again with the silent kind. If the drop is already silent, then it is simply too little: add more, a cupful at a time, and keep cooking.
   - Still stings after long cooking: too much lye. Add small pieces of fat, one at a time, cooking between each, until the sting is gone.
6. **When it passes the finger test, you have soft soap** — a brown jelly, mild and slippery. Before you take it off the heat, consider one last small knob of fat — a fistful per big pot — stirred in and cooked a few minutes more. This is **superfatting**: leaving a little fat uncut so the soap is gentler on skin (the why is below). Scoop the soft soap into a lidded pot; it is ready for laundry, dishes, and hands.
7. **For hard bars:** stir a few handfuls of salt into the hot soap. It will curdle and float as thick pale curds on dark liquid — the salt pushes the soap out of the water. Skim the curds into the cloth-lined mold and press them down firm. Pour the leftover liquid away from where animals drink; it still carries lye.
8. **Cure the bars.** Keep them in airy shade for 3 to 6 weeks, standing on edge with gaps between, turning them now and then. They grow harder and milder every week — mostly water leaving, plus a little quiet mellowing, as you will see. A bar is cured when it is hard, no longer stings the finger test, and lathers cleanly. Even soft soap improves with a day or two of covered rest.

## Watch out

- **Boiling lye and fat spatter.** Every drop burns twice, by heat and by chemistry. Long stick, long sleeves, eye cover, stand upwind, work outside. Never lean your face over the pot.
- **Water into lye is the classic accident.** Mixing the two always makes heat — the question is who gets it. Pour lye slowly into a whole pot of water and the heat spreads through all that water, tamed; let a small splash of water land on strong lye and all the heat crowds into that one splash at once, so it flashes to boiling and spits. Same heat, different share. Always add lye into water, slowly, stirring — and lower fat lumps in gently, never dropped.
- **Soap boils over fast.** Near the end it foams up like milk, in seconds — the very thing that makes lather makes boil-overs. Keep the pot half full at most, and be ready to pull it off the heat or knock the foam down with a splash of cold water.
- **Fresh soap looks like food** — brown pudding in a pot, pale curds in a dish. Mark it, store it high, warn everyone, especially children.
- **Harsh soap that stings is not finished** — the fat ran out before the lye did. A faint sting may mellow with weeks of airy curing (the air slowly tames small traces of lye), but the sure fix is to melt it back down with a little more fat and cook again. Never use stinging soap on a child.
- **Greasy soap that will not lather is the opposite fault** — the lye ran out first, or was never strong enough to begin with. It hurts no one, but it smears instead of cleaning and can go rancid. Vinegar-test your lye: silent, and you simply need more of it; fizzing, and you need the lime upgrade before another hour of cooking is worth anything. Then melt the batch down and cook it again.
- **Dark streaks or a scorched smell** mean the pot ran too hot or too dry. The soap still works but poorly. Cook lower, stir the bottom, splash in water sooner.
- **If raw mix gets on skin**, rinse with lots of plain water right away — keep rinsing until the slippery feel is gone; that slipperiness is lye turning your own skin oils into soap. In an eye: rinse gently for at least 20 to 30 minutes — lye keeps burning as long as any remains, so longer is better — hold the lid open, and get real medical care when you can.

## How it works

Picture a fat molecule as a **hand holding three tails**. The hand is a small piece called glycerin; the three long tails are the greasy chains that make fat feel like fat. Lye is a molecule-scissors. In the hot pot it snips each tail free from the glycerin hand, and every snip costs the scissors a piece of themselves: the cut end of each tail gets a small cap, made from the lye, that loves water. That is why the lye gets used up as the soap forms — no lye left, no more caps, no more snipping. Chemists call this event **saponification** — soap-making. One fat molecule in, three soap molecules out, plus one free glycerin hand (which stays in soft soap and makes it gentle and slightly sweet; salting out bars sends most of it away with the poured-off liquid).

So a soap molecule is a two-natured thing: a long **tail that loves grease** and hates water, and a small **head that loves water**. That split personality is the whole secret of washing. Rub soap into greasy hands and the tails burrow into the grease; the heads stay out, holding hands with the water. Rinse, and the water drags the heads, the heads drag the tails, the tails drag the grease. Each speck of oil leaves wrapped in a ball of soap molecules — tails inward hugging the grease, heads outward facing the water. Chemists call these balls **micelles**; think of them as tiny rafts. Dirt and germs ride the rafts away instead of smearing around. Water alone cannot do this, because water and grease refuse to touch; soap is the handshake between them.

Now the recipe's choices explain themselves. **Hard fat makes hard soap, soft oil makes soft soap**: straight, stiff tails (tallow, lard) stack together tight like planks, and molecules packed tight make a firm solid; bent, floppy tails (liquid oils) pile up loose like crooked branches, and a loose pile stays soft. The **lye matters too**, because the lye becomes the caps: ash lye is potash lye, and soap wearing potash caps never packs firm — it stays loose, soft soap; lye made with soda instead gives caps that let the tails lock into firm bars. Salt plays the trick twice — it crowds the water so the soap is squeezed out as curds you can press into shape, and common salt carries sodium, which trades places with the potassium in the soap's caps, so the soft potash soap you skim out sets into firm soda-style bars.

**Curing** is mostly the water leaving — the cooking already finished the snipping, and your finger test proved it. As the bar dries it grows harder, lathers better, and lasts longer in the dish. It grows a little milder too: any small trace of leftover lye slowly meets the air, whose carbon dioxide tames it into something far gentler that no longer bites the skin. That is why a faint **zap** — the finger sting, the battery-lick jolt — fades over the weeks. A strong sting is another matter: that soap is unfinished and needs recooking with fat, not just rest.

And **superfatting** is deliberate imbalance: give the pot a little more fat than the scissors can cut. The lye keeps snipping until it is all spent — so if any fat is left unsnipped, no lye can be left at all; every scrap of it is certainly used up — and that leftover trace of fat stays in the bar to soften your skin instead of stripping it. Harsh soap and gentle soap are the same soap — the difference is only who ran out first, the lye or the fat.

## Go further

- **See soap molecules race today.** Float a drop of oil on a dish of water, then touch the water with a soaped finger — the oil flees and scatters as soap molecules spread across the surface, slackening the water's skin ahead of them. (The micelles themselves work invisibly, in the wash water.)
- **Make one superfatted batch next to a plain one:** same day, same lye, one extra knob of fat in one pot at the end. After curing, wash with both; your hands will explain superfatting better than any book.
- **Try the fats you have.** All-tallow gives a hard, quiet bar; a share of soft oil gives faster lather but a softer bar. Keep notes — soapmaking rewards a written record.
- **Scent and grit.** Crushed dried herbs at the mold stage for scent; fine sand and ash for a pot-scrubbing soap. Add them after the finger test passes — lye destroys delicate things.
- **Master the neighbours first.** The ash-lye guide is this craft's foundation — consistent lye turns soapmaking from gambling into craft — and the render-fat guide decides whether your soap smells like clean tallow or old kitchen.
- **What this unlocks:** washed wounds, clean birth cloths, laundry that does not spread mites, and a trade good every neighbour wants.
