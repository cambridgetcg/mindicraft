Lye is water that has pulled the strong salts out of wood ash. It looks like weak brown tea, but it is a powerful cleaner: it dissolves grease, whitens cloth, and — mixed with fat — becomes soap. For thousands of years, this was how the world washed.

This guide teaches two grades. Plain ash lye is a **weak** cleaner — good for scrubbing pots and washing cloth. With burnt lime (from the burn-lime craft), you can trade it up into **strong** lye, the kind that makes real, firm soap — one extra hour, one beautiful trick.

Respect both grades. Lye is caustic, meaning it slowly burns skin and can blind an eye. Made and stored with care, it is one of the most useful liquids you can produce.

## You need

- **Hardwood ash** — white or pale grey ash from a clean fire of oak, beech, maple, or fruit wood. Softwoods like pine make weak lye. No plastic, no painted wood, no glossy paper in the fire. Sift out charcoal lumps — you want the fine pale powder.
- **Rainwater** — or other soft water, meaning water carrying no dissolved stone. Hard well water (water carrying dissolved stone) fights the lye and weakens it. Warm it before pouring — warm water pulls the salts out faster.
- **A leaching barrel** — leaching just means trickling water through something so the water carries away whatever dissolves. Any tall container with one small hole at the bottom will do the job: a wooden bucket, a clay pot with a hole, even a hollow log. Knee-high is a good size. Never aluminium — lye eats it. Wood, clay, and iron are fine.
- **Clean pebbles and a fat layer of straw or dry grass** — the filter bed, so ash stays behind.
- **A catch pot** — clay or iron, below the hole.
- **A fresh egg or a small potato** — your strength tester. Fresh matters: an old egg floats even in plain water.
- **For the strong-lye upgrade: slaked lime** — burnt limestone soaked in water until it stops hissing (the burn-lime guide makes it). Roughly a double handful per batch.
- **Protection — run through this list out loud before you start, so nothing gets skipped.** Cover your eyes: goggles, or at the very least keep your face where no splash can reach it. Cover hands with cloth or leather, long sleeves, hair tied away. Keep a big pot of plain rinse water within arm's reach the whole time.

## Steps

1. Set the barrel on stones or a stand, tilted slightly toward the hole, catch pot underneath — outside, on ground you don't mind staining.
2. Lay pebbles over the hole inside, then a hand-deep bed of straw on top. This is the filter. Press it down so ash can't sneak past.
3. Fill the barrel with ash, pressed down lightly. Leave a hand's width of space at the top. A knee-high barrel takes roughly a large sackful — call it ten litres loosely packed.
4. Pour on warm rainwater, one jugful (about a litre) at a time. Use roughly half as much water as ash by volume — four to six jugs across the day. The first fill soaks in and may take two or three hours before anything drips out. Slow is good, because the longer water sits among the ash, the more salt it has time to dissolve and carry: one drip every breath or two makes strong lye; a running trickle races through before it can load up, and makes weak tea.
5. Collect the brown liquid. This is lye water. First runnings are darkest and strongest; later runnings come out pale and weak — save those and pour them over your next batch of ash in place of plain water, so the little salt they carry is not wasted.
6. Test the strength: float the fresh egg or potato in the cooled lye. The more salt the water carries, the harder it pushes the egg up — so how high it rides tells you how strong the lye is.
   - A patch the size of a small coin shows above the surface: strong enough to go on.
   - It floats high, lying on its side: very strong — thin with a little rainwater.
   - It sinks: too weak. Pour the lye back through the same ash, or better, fresh ash. Each pass picks up more salt.
7. Still weak after a second pass? Boil it down. Simmer in a clay or iron pot — outside, lid off, long stick to stir — until the tester floats. A faint soapy, sharp smell is normal. Never in aluminium.
8. **The upgrade — making strong lye. Its old name is causticizing; think of it as a partner-trade, because the lime and the potash are about to swap partners (how it works tells the full story).** Heat your egg-floating lye until it steams but does not boil. Stir in the slaked lime a spoonful at a time — properly slaked lime will not hiss or spit, but keep your face back anyway. Stir a few minutes; the liquid turns milky white. Cover it and walk away for an hour or two.
9. Watch the milk settle. White mud sinks to the bottom — chalk, your proof the trade worked (how it works tells the story). The clear liquid above is strong caustic lye. Ladle it off carefully into its storage pot, leaving the mud behind. Still milky after two hours? Leave it overnight.
10. Test whether the trade is finished: put a drop of the clear lye into a spoon of vinegar. Tiny bubbles mean untraded potash remains — stir in another spoon of lime, settle again. A silent drop means fully strong. Soap makers used this test centuries before anyone could name the chemicals.
11. Store both grades in lidded clay pots, clearly marked, up high, away from children and animals. Lye looks exactly like tea or broth. That is the trap. Tell everyone in camp what it is, and show them the pot.

Done looks like this: clear liquid, tea-coloured (weak grade) or pale (strong grade), floating a fresh egg with a coin of shell showing, slippery on a wetted stick, and — strong grade — silent in vinegar.

## Watch out

- **Lye burns quietly — the slippery feel IS the warning.** On skin it first feels slick like soap, then starts to sting. That slickness is the lye already at work on you (see how it works). Rinse at once with lots of plain water and keep rinsing far longer than feels necessary — many minutes. For a splash in the eye: tilt the head so water runs away from the good eye, rinse gently with clean water for at least fifteen minutes — with lye, longer is better; keep rinsing even on the way — and get real medical care as soon as you can.
- **Lye into water, never water into lye.** When diluting or dissolving anything caustic, add the strong stuff slowly into the larger water. A little water poured onto a lot of caustic can heat up fast and spit into your face.
- **The deadliest mistake is drinking it.** Never store lye in a cup, bottle, or anything ever used for food or drink. Mark the pot. Tell everyone.
- **Boiling lye spits.** Stand back and upwind, long stirring stick, outside only, children far away.
- **Weak lye, and why.** Softwood or dirty ash: start over with clean hardwood ash. Hard water: switch to rainwater. Poured too fast: re-run the liquid through fresh ash, slowly.
- **The egg lies if it is old.** Old eggs hold an air pocket and float in plain water. Use a fresh egg — or a potato, which cannot lie.
- **Lime that hisses is not slaked.** Quicklime (freshly burnt lime) reacts violently with water, spitting and hot enough to scald. Only lime that has been fully soaked and gone quiet belongs near your lye pot. The burn-lime guide covers slaking safely.
- **Milky lye that never clears** means too much lime or too little time. Leave it overnight; if still cloudy, pour through a cloth — a faint chalk haze does no harm to soap.
- **Metal caution:** iron is fine; aluminium dissolves and froths; galvanised (zinc-coated) buckets froth the same way, so no shiny grey-coated pails; brass and tin discolour. When in doubt, use fired clay.

## How it works

Start with the fire. Wood is mostly stuff that burns away as gas — but a tree spent its whole life pumping minerals up from the soil, and minerals do not burn. They stay behind in the ash. The most useful one is a salt of the metal potassium, called potash — proper name potassium carbonate. "Carbonate" just means the potassium is holding hands with a piece of chalk-stuff, the same stuff your breath carries out as gas.

Water trickling through the ash dissolves the potash and carries it out the bottom. That brown liquid is an alkali — the opposite of an acid. Acids taste sour and sting sharply; alkalis feel slippery and bite slowly. Potash water is a *weak* alkali: real, useful, gentle.

The egg test is pure floating. The more salt dissolved in water, the denser the water gets, and the harder it pushes up on anything in it. In plain water a fresh egg sinks. In good lye the water is dense enough to hold it up, coin-patch showing. You are weighing the invisible salt with an egg.

Now the beautiful trick: **causticizing**, the partner trade. Your potash is potassium holding carbonate. Slaked lime is calcium holding hydroxide — hydroxide being the fierce, grease-eating part of any strong alkali. Stir them together in water and the partners swap. Why? Because calcium and carbonate, once they meet, lock together into chalk — and chalk will not stay dissolved. It falls out of the water as white mud. The calcium is gone, the carbonate is gone, and what is left dissolved is potassium now holding hydroxide: potassium hydroxide, true caustic lye, many times fiercer than what you started with. The white mud sinking is the swap made visible — every speck of chalk on the bottom is a trade completed. And the vinegar test reads the same story backwards: carbonate fizzes in sour things (it releases its chalk-gas), hydroxide does not, so a silent drop means every carbonate has traded away.

Last, why lye bites skin. Lye's whole job is turning fat into soap — the make-soap craft does it on purpose. But your skin is coated in oil and built partly of fat, and lye cannot tell the difference: it starts turning *you* into soap. That slippery feeling is not the lye itself — it is your own skin-fat, already converted, sliding under your fingers. The warning and the wonder are the same fact. Rinse with lots of water, and respect the liquid that cleans by eating grease — including yours.

## Go further

- **A safe first handshake, today (fingers only — never sip it):** stir a spoon of clean hardwood ash into a cup of warm water, let it settle, rub a drop of the clear liquid between two fingers. That faint slip is a weak alkali saying hello. Use the cupful to cut grease on a dirty pot, then rinse your fingers well.
- **Vinegar-test both grades** — plain ash lye fizzes, causticized lye goes in silent. Side by side, the whole chemistry lesson in two spoons.
- **Dry potash for the shelf:** boil weak lye all the way down in an iron pot until grey crusty crystals remain — that is crude potash. Keep the pot on the heat and roast the crystals until they whiten: now it is pearl ash — storable for years, tradeable, re-dissolvable. Melt-glass wants it: potash is one of glass's three ingredients.
- **The point of it all: soap.** Strong lye plus rendered fat, cooked and stirred, becomes real soap. The make-soap guide takes over where this one ends — bring your egg-floating, silent-in-vinegar lye.
- **Deepen the lime half:** the burn-lime guide teaches roasting limestone and slaking it — the other parent of strong lye, and the door to lime mortar.
- **Tanners want lye too:** a weak lye bath loosens hair from hides — the tan-hides guide shows the same biting liquid, tamed, preparing leather.
