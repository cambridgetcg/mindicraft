Lye is water that has pulled the strong salts out of wood ash. It looks like weak brown tea, but it is a powerful cleaner: it dissolves grease, whitens cloth, and — mixed with fat — becomes soap. For thousands of years, this was how the world got clean.

Respect it. Lye is caustic, meaning it slowly burns skin and can blind an eye. Made and stored with care, it is one of the most useful liquids you can produce.

## You need

- **Hardwood ash** — white or pale grey ash from a clean fire of oak, beech, maple, or fruit wood. Hardwood ash makes strong lye; soft woods like pine make it weak. No plastic, no painted wood in the fire.
- **Rainwater** — or other soft water. Rain is best; hard well water fights the lye and weakens it.
- **A leaching barrel** — any tall container with one small hole at the bottom: a wooden bucket, a clay pot with a hole, even a hollow log. Never aluminium — lye eats it. Wood, clay, and iron are fine.
- **Clean pebbles and a fat layer of straw or dry grass** — the filter bed, so ash does not run out with the liquid.
- **A catch pot** — clay or iron, set below the hole.
- **A fresh egg or a small potato** — your strength tester.
- **Protection** — cover hands with cloth or leather, keep your face back, tie hair away.

## Steps

1. Set the barrel up on stones or a stand, tilted slightly toward the hole, with the catch pot underneath.
2. Lay pebbles over the hole inside, then a hand-deep bed of straw on top. This is the filter.
3. Fill the barrel with ash, pressed down lightly. Leave a hand's width of space at the top.
4. Pour on warm rainwater, one jugful at a time, slowly. The first fill soaks in and may take hours before anything drips out. Slow is good — slow dripping makes strong lye.
5. Collect the brown liquid. This is lye water.
6. Test the strength: float the egg or potato in it.
   - It floats with a patch the size of a small coin above the surface: strong enough for soap.
   - It sinks: too weak. Pour the lye back through the same ash, or through a barrel of fresh ash.
7. If it is still weak, boil it down. Simmer the lye in a clay or iron pot — outside, lid off — until the tester floats. Never in aluminium.
8. Store the lye in a lidded clay pot, clearly marked, up high and away from children. It looks exactly like tea or broth. That is the trap.

## Watch out

- **Lye burns quietly.** On skin it first feels slippery like soap, then starts to sting. Rinse at once with lots of plain water — keep rinsing for a long time. For a splash in the eye, rinse gently with clean water for at least 15 minutes and get real medical care when you can.
- **The deadliest mistake is drinking it.** Never store lye in a cup, bottle, or anything used for food or drink. Mark the pot. Tell everyone in camp what it is.
- **Boiling lye spits.** Stand back, use a long stirring stick, keep it outside, keep children far away.
- **Weak lye means failed soap.** Always do the float test before you use it for anything.
- **Metal caution:** iron pots are fine; aluminium dissolves and froths. When in doubt, use fired clay.

## How it works

When wood burns, its minerals stay behind in the ash as potash — chalky salts of the metal potassium. Water trickling through the ash dissolves those salts and carries them out. The result is a caustic liquid: it breaks apart grease and oils on contact. That grease-breaking bite is exactly what makes it clean things — and exactly why it bites skin, which is oily too.
