Before nails and screws, glue held the world together. Pitch glue fixed stone points onto spears, feathers onto arrows, and handles onto knives. Softer pitch sealed the seams of baskets, buckets, and boats so they could hold water. The recipe is simple: tree resin, melted with care, mixed with charcoal dust.

Resin is the sticky stuff a pine or spruce tree bleeds to seal its own wounds. You are borrowing the tree's bandage and improving it.

## You need

- **Tree resin** — golden or amber lumps stuck to the trunks of pine, spruce, or fir trees, wherever the bark was wounded. Hard dry lumps are best. Sticky fresh sap works too but needs longer cooking. Collect a good double handful.
- **Charcoal dust** — black lumps from an old fire, ground to fine powder. This is the filler that stops the glue being brittle.
- **A little hard fat or beeswax** (optional) — a piece the size of a walnut. It makes the glue bend a little instead of snapping.
- **A small clay pot, a large shell, or a flat stone with a dip** — something that can sit in heat. It will be sticky forever after; do not use your cooking pot.
- **A fire burned down to steady coals** — resin needs gentle heat, not flame.
- **Sticks** — one for stirring, several for storing glue on.

## Steps

1. Scrape resin lumps off tree trunks with a stick or stone. Pick out big bits of bark and dirt as you go.
2. Grind charcoal to dust between two stones. You want flour, not gravel. Sift it through cloth or your fingers.
3. Set your pot **beside** the coals, not over flame, and add the resin. It melts at about the heat of boiling water.
   - If it smokes hard or bubbles fast, pull it further from the heat.
4. When it is fully liquid, fish out floating bark and dirt with a stick.
5. Stir in charcoal dust, about **1 part dust to 2 or 3 parts melted resin**. Add the fat or beeswax now if you have it.
6. Test it. Dip a stick, let the blob cool fully, then bend it.
   - Shatters like glass: too brittle — stir in a little more fat.
   - Bends and stays sticky: too soft — add more charcoal, or cook gently a bit longer.
   - Snaps clean with effort: just right.
7. Make glue sticks: wind the warm glue around the ends of sticks, like dark lollipops. Cooled, they keep for years.
8. To glue something: warm the glue stick near coals until soft, and warm the two parts too. Smear, press together, hold still until fully cold. Cold is when it is strong.
9. For **pitch** — the sealing kind — make the same mix but softer: more fat, less charcoal. Smear it warm along seams and cracks of containers and boats.

## Watch out

- **Resin catches fire easily.** It can flare up like a torch. Melt it beside coals, never over open flame, and keep a flat stone or lid nearby to smother a fire. Never pour water on burning resin — it spatters.
- **Hot pitch sticks to skin and keeps burning**, like hot sugar. If it gets on you, plunge the spot into cool water at once. Do not wipe it with your bare hand — that spreads the burn.
- **Glue fails on dirty, greasy, or cold surfaces.** Scrape joints clean and warm both parts before gluing.
- **Pitch glue softens in strong heat.** A hafted tool left in hot sun can loosen. For anything that takes hard knocks, bind the joint with cord as well as glue.

## How it works

Resin is the tree's own wound-sealer, and it hardens as it dries. Charcoal dust works like gravel in concrete: a filler that stops cracks from running through, making the glue tougher and harder. The fat is a softener, letting the glue flex slightly instead of shattering. Heat melts it, cold sets it — so the same stick of glue can be used again and again.
