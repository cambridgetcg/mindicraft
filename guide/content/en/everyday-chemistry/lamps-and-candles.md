Firelight ties you to the hearth. A lamp you can carry gives you back the evening: mending, cooking, checking on an animal in the night. The trick behind every lamp and candle is the same: fat will burn, but only as a vapour — fat turned to gas — rising off something hot. The **wick** — a string that soaks up melted fat like a dry stem drinks water — lifts a steady trickle of fuel up to a small flame and feeds it for hours. A spoonful of fat that would flare and be gone in a fire can glow all evening on a wick.

Every choice below — wick thickness, melt heat, time between dips — asks one question: is the flame getting the right trickle of fuel? Too much and it smokes; too little and it dies.

Start with the fat lamp: it is the oldest lamp in the world and takes minutes. Candles are the same idea made solid and portable.

## You need

- **Rendered fat** — tallow (from cattle, sheep, or deer fat, melted and cleaned) is best because it is firm; a candle of soft pig fat sags like warm cheese. Soft fats and fish oil still work in a lamp, where the dish holds them. Good tallow is pale, nearly white, and smells of little; bits of meat and water left in badly rendered fat sputter, stink, and smoke.
- **Beeswax**, if you can get it, makes the finest candles: brighter, harder, far less smoke and smell. Even one part beeswax to three parts tallow stiffens a candle and calms its smoke.
- **Wick material**: two-ply twisted plant cordage about as thick as a matchstick (2–3 mm), or the soft white pith of a rush stem. Loosely twisted wicks draw better than tight hard ones — the fuel climbs the narrow spaces *between* fibres, and a hard twist closes those spaces. Test a wick on water first: dip one end in, and if the wet creeps up the string in a breath or two, it will lift fat the same way.
- **A fireproof dish** for the lamp — a clay saucer, a large shell, or a hollowed soft stone. A pinched lip or notch to rest the wick on helps: the wick must lean out of the pool, not drown in it.
- **A tall, narrow pot** for dipping candles, and a **water bath**: set the fat pot inside a larger pot of hot water so the fat melts gently and cannot catch fire — water never gets hotter than its own boil, so the fat stays tame. Deep fuel in a narrow pot beats shallow fuel in a wide one — a candle can only be as long as the fat is deep.
- **A stick to hang wicks from**, and a calm place out of the wind — a draught makes flames gutter — flicker and struggle instead of burning steady — and coats cool unevenly.

## Steps

1. **Make a fat lamp first.** Put a lump of fat the size of an egg (roughly 50 g) in the dish. Lay a finger-length of wick so one end sits in the fat and the other pokes half a thumb-width (about 1 cm) over the dish's edge or notch — more than that and the flame runs big and smoky. The overhang is the flame's seat; the rest is only a pipe.
2. **Prime and light it.** Melt a little fat and soak the wick tip, or hold a flame to it until melted fat climbs up the string and the flame steadies — patient seconds, because the flame must first melt its own supply line. Trim the tip short and the flame burns small and clean; leave it long and it burns big, ragged, and smoky. An egg-sized lump burns a full evening.
3. **Try a rushlight for cheap light.** Peel a rush stem while it is still green and fresh — once dry, the skin will not strip and the pith crumbles — leaving one thin strip of skin as a spine so the pith doesn't break. Let the peeled pith dry, then drag it slowly through melted fat a few times until soaked. Held at a slant — like a ladder against a wall — in a split stick, one rushlight burns 15 to 30 minutes. The slant sets how fast fresh fuel reaches the flame — too upright the flame starves; too flat, it runs along the stem.
4. **For candles, melt fat or wax in the water bath.** Keep it just barely melted — pourable, never shimmering, never smoking. Tallow at the right heat feels warm to a fingertip, not burning (don't try that with wax, which melts hotter). Too hot, and each dip *melts off* the coat the last dip left instead of adding a new one.
5. **Dip the wicks.** Tie several wicks to a stick, a hand-width apart so the growing candles don't kiss, each a little longer than your melt is deep — you can only dip as deep as the fat, so the pot sets the candle's length. Dip for a heartbeat — down and straight out, no lingering, or the warm melt starts taking back the coat it just gave — then let them harden in the air: count slowly to sixty, longer on a warm day. Straighten each wick after the first dip or two, while the coat is soft; a bend now is a crooked candle forever.
6. **Repeat 20 to 40 times.** Each dip adds a skin about as thick as heavy paper; the candle grows the way an icicle does, coat over coat. Dipping again while the last coat is still warm and glossy melts your work off — wait until the surface is firm and matte. When they are about as thick as your thumb (around 2 cm), hang them somewhere cool for a few hours. Done candles sound faintly solid tapped together; half-cured ones dent under a fingernail.
7. **Trim and burn.** Cut the bottoms flat with a warm knife, trim wicks to half a finger-width (about 1 cm), and stand candles in a lump of clay or a drilled block. A burning tallow candle wants its wick trimmed every half hour or so; beeswax asks less often — but no hand-twisted wick ever trims itself, so keep the knife handy either way.

## Watch out

- **Overheated fat catches fire.** Fat gives off vapour as it heats, and past a certain heat the vapour lights itself. If the pot smokes, it is close to burning — off the heat *now*. If a fat pot does ignite, **never pour water on it**: water sinks under the fat, flashes to steam, and hurls burning fat outward in a sheet of flame. Smother it with a board or lid — no air, no fire. The water bath makes this near-impossible.
- **Hot fat clings to skin and keeps burning it.** Keep your face back when you lean over the melt, and dip with a steady hand, not a drop. If fat splashes on skin, get it under plenty of cool water **at once** and keep the water running for a long while — a slow count to a few hundred, not a quick splash. Water carries the heat away; wiping just spreads it.
- **Every flame needs a fireproof seat.** Dish or candle on clay, stone, or bare earth — never straight on wood, and never left burning while everyone sleeps. A lamp is a small open fire invited indoors; treat it as one.
- **A tipped fat lamp keeps burning as a puddle** — the spill becomes one wide pool with the flame riding on it. Set lamps low and stable, out of reach of children, dogs, and elbows.
- **Big smoky flame? The wick is too long or too thick.** It lifts more fat than the flame can fully burn, and the unburnt part leaves as black smoke. Trim it; don't add fat.
- **Flame drowning — shrinking, blue, guttering in its own pool?** The melt has risen over the wick, or the wick is too thin to climb clear. Tip a little fat out of a candle's pool, or pull more wick up onto the lamp's notch.
- **Candles with cracks, lumps, or coats that slide off** come from too-hot fat or dipping before the last coat set. The save is cheap: melt the failures down and start again.
- **Tallow candles slump in heat and can go rancid.** Store them cool and dark, and expect drips — that's tallow being tallow, not a mistake. A rancid smell means the fat wasn't rendered clean.

## How it works

Here is the secret hiding in plain sight: the flame does not burn the wick, and it does not burn the solid fat either. It burns fat **vapour** — fat turned to gas. Everything in the craft exists to keep a tiny, steady stream of that gas flowing.

Watch a candle as a machine with four parts. The flame's heat melts a small pool of fat below it. The wick drinks from that pool: liquid creeps up the narrow spaces between its fibres, the same way tea climbs a sugar cube dipped at one corner. That creeping is called **capillary action** — liquids pull themselves up any passage narrow enough — and it is the ladder that lifts fuel against gravity with no moving parts. At the top of the ladder the heat turns the liquid to vapour; the vapour mixes with air and burns, making the heat that melts more fat. Melt, climb, boil off, burn: a small engine that runs itself until the fuel is gone.

This one picture explains every rule above. Why does the wick barely char? Because it stays soaked — evaporating fat carries heat away and keeps the string cool, the way a wet cloth cools your skin. Only the very tip, where the fat runs out, slowly blackens; that is why you trim. Why does a too-thick wick choke and a too-thin one starve? The ladder must match the flame: a fat wick floods up more fuel than the flame can vaporise and it chokes; a thin wick can't lift enough and it dies. A clean flame is one where melt, climb, and burn are in balance.

Now look into the flame itself — it has zones. At the base sits a dim blue ring: fresh vapour meeting plenty of air and burning hungrily and completely. Above it glows the bright yellow body, and here is the second secret: that yellow light *is* soot. In the crowded middle there isn't enough air, so some fuel breaks into specks of pure carbon — soot — and those specks, swept up through the heat, glow yellow-white like tiny coals. A candle is a machine for making soot and burning it in mid-air; the glow of that burning is your light. In a balanced flame every speck burns up before it escapes; with a too-long wick or a shoving draught, specks slip unburnt from the tip, and you see them as black smoke.

Why does tallow smoke more than beeswax? Tallow is a soft jumble of fats that melts at a low heat, so its pool floods the wick with more fuel than the flame can finish. Beeswax is harder, melts hotter, and feeds slower and steadier — the flame gets exactly what it can eat, so almost nothing escapes as smoke. Same engine, better-matched parts.

## Go further

- **See the soot for yourself, today.** Hold a cold spoon briefly in the yellow of a flame: it comes out black. You caught the glowing specks before they finished burning — proof that the light is burning soot.
- **Prove the vapour.** Blow out a candle and quickly bring a burning splinter into the ribbon of white smoke above the wick. The flame jumps down the smoke and relights the candle without touching it — that "smoke" is fat vapour, the true fuel.
- **Tune a wick on purpose.** Make three lamps with thin, matchstick, and double-thick wicks; watch one starve, one balance, one smoke.
- **Mould instead of dip.** Pour melted tallow around a wick held taut in a tube of rolled bark or a reed — faster than dipping.
- **Better fuel, better string.** Cleaner rendering makes calmer flame — the render-fat guide is where candle quality is really decided — and every wick begins as good cordage.
- **What this unlocks:** evening work — reading, fine sewing, tending things through the night. A household that owns its light owns its winter evenings.
