Firelight ties you to the hearth. A lamp you can carry gives you back the evening: mending, cooking, checking on an animal in the night. The trick behind every lamp and candle is the same: fat will burn, but only as a vapour rising off something hot. The **wick** — a string that soaks up melted fat like a dry stem drinks water — lifts a steady trickle of fuel up to a small flame and feeds it for hours. A spoonful of fat that would flare and be gone in a fire can glow all evening on a wick.

Start with the fat lamp: it is the oldest lamp in the world and takes minutes. Candles are the same idea made solid and portable.

## You need

- **Rendered fat** — tallow (from cattle, sheep, or deer fat, melted and cleaned) is best because it is firm; soft fats and fish oil work in a lamp but not in a candle. **Beeswax**, if you can get it, makes the finest candles: brighter, harder, far less smoke and smell.
- **Wick material**: two-ply twisted plant cordage about as thick as a matchstick, or the soft white pith of a rush stem. Loosely twisted wicks draw better than tight hard ones.
- **A fireproof dish** for the lamp — a clay saucer, a large shell, or a hollowed soft stone. A pinched lip or notch to rest the wick on helps.
- **A tall, narrow pot** for dipping candles, and a **water bath**: set the fat pot inside a larger pot of hot water so the fat melts gently and cannot catch fire.
- **A stick to hang wicks from**, and a calm place out of the wind to work.

## Steps

1. **Make a fat lamp first.** Put a lump of fat the size of an egg in the dish. Lay a finger-length of wick so one end sits in the fat and the other pokes a thumb-width over the dish's edge or notch.
2. **Prime and light it.** Melt a little fat and soak the wick tip, or hold a flame to it until fat wicks up and the flame steadies. Only the wick tip should stand proud — trim it short and the flame burns small and clean.
3. **Try a rushlight for cheap light.** Peel a dried rush stem, leaving one thin strip of skin as a spine, and drag the pith through melted fat a few times. Held at a slant in a split stick, one rushlight burns 15 to 30 minutes.
4. **For candles, melt fat or wax in the water bath.** Keep it just barely melted — hot enough to be liquid, never smoking. Deep fuel in a narrow pot beats shallow fuel in a wide one.
5. **Dip the wicks.** Tie several wicks to a stick, a hand-width apart, each hanging as long as you want the candle. Dip them in for a heartbeat, lift out, and let them cool and harden — count slowly to sixty. Straighten each wick with your fingers after the first dip or two.
6. **Repeat 20 to 40 times.** Each dip adds a thin skin. Rushing — dipping again while the last coat is still warm — melts your work off. When the candles are about as thick as your thumb, hang them somewhere cool for a few hours to harden fully.
7. **Trim and burn.** Cut the bottoms flat, trim wicks to a finger-width, and stand candles in a lump of clay or a drilled block.

## Watch out

- **Overheated fat catches fire.** If it smokes, it is close to burning. If a fat pot does ignite, **never pour water on it** — it will explode outward in a sheet of flame. Smother it with a board or lid and take it off the heat.
- **Every flame needs a fireproof seat.** Dish or candle on clay, stone, or bare earth — never straight on wood, and never left burning while everyone sleeps.
- **A tipped fat lamp keeps burning as a puddle.** Set lamps low and stable, out of reach of children, dogs, and elbows.
- **Tallow candles slump in heat.** Store them somewhere cool, and expect drips — that's tallow being tallow, not a mistake.
- **Big smoky flame? The wick is too long or too thick.** Trim it; don't add fat.

## How it works

Solid fat doesn't burn — fat vapour does. The flame's heat melts a tiny pool, the wick pulls the liquid up through its fibres (capillary action, the same pull that wets a dry cloth from one corner), and at the tip the heat turns it to vapour and burns it. Flame, melt, climb, burn: a small engine that runs itself until the fuel is gone.
