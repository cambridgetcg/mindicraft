title: Burn limestone into lime

Burn limestone hot enough and it becomes lime — one of the oldest and most useful materials people have ever made. Lime mixed with sand becomes mortar, the glue that holds stone walls together. Thinned with water it becomes whitewash, which brightens and protects walls. It also cures hides for leather and sweetens sour soil. Almost every old stone building you have seen is held together by someone's lime burn.

The whole craft is one circle: fire drives the breath out of a stone, and the stone spends months quietly breathing it back in on your wall. Everything here — the fierce heat, the violent hiss, the sealed pots — makes sense once you see that circle.

This is a big, hot, three-day job, and lime demands respect: fresh-burned lime reacts violently with water and can burn skin and blind eyes. Done carefully, it turns loose stones into buildings.

## You need

- **Limestone, chalk, seashells, or coral** — all the same stone in different costumes. The test: drip vinegar or any sour liquid on it. If it fizzes with tiny bubbles, it will make lime. Gather a big basketful; what comes out of the kiln weighs little more than half of what went in.
- **A kiln** that can hold fierce heat for a full day — a fire chamber below and stone above, or a lined pit with a good draft — a clear path for air to be pulled up through the fire.
- **Lots of fuel** — charcoal is best because it burns hotter and cleaner than raw wood. Gather at least as much fuel as stone by volume, then gather that much again. Running out halfway is the most common failure, and half-burned stone is just hot rock.
- **Water** and a pit, trough, or big pot for slaking (waking the lime) — big enough that the mix fills it only halfway; it will froth.
- **A long stick** for stirring — arm's length is the rule, not a suggestion.
- **Lidded clay pots** for storage — quicklime must be sealed from air the moment it cools.
- **Protection** — cover skin, tie back hair, keep your face back, and guard your eyes above all. Anything clear that shields the eyes, wear it for the slaking.

## Steps

1. **Break the stone** into pieces between egg and fist size. Small enough for heat to reach the middle, big enough to leave air gaps for the fire to breathe through. Bigger than a fist burns outside and stays raw at the heart. Shells go in whole — they are thin, so heat crosses them easily.
2. **Load the kiln.** Either build alternating hand-deep layers — fuel, stone, fuel, stone, to the top — or pile all the stone above a fire chamber, depending on your kiln. Leave gaps; a packed kiln smothers itself.
3. **Fire it, and keep it fierce.** The stone must sit at a bright orange-to-yellow glow — at least 900 degrees Celsius — for many hours. Judge by colour: dull red is not enough, bright orange edging to yellow is right. A small kiln needs 12 to 24 hours of hard firing, so plan the night in shifts and stack fuel within reach before dark. If the glow fades to red for long, the burn stalls. A good sign: the stones seem to shrink and whiten in the fire.
4. **Let the kiln cool** for most of a day — opening it hot can crack the kiln. Then check the stone. Well-burned lime — called **quicklime** — is:
   - noticeably lighter than it went in (a fist-sized lump now weighs in your hand like only half of one),
   - white or pale, dry-looking, and soft enough to crumble at the edges,
   - hissing and cracking when a single drop of water touches it — the one sure test.
   - Heavy, hard, grey lumps that stay silent under the drop are under-burned: set them aside for the next firing. A grey core inside a white shell means the piece was too big — break the next batch smaller.
5. **Unload with covered hands, into lidded pots.** Quicklime is hungry for water — including the water in your skin, sweat, and eyes. It also drinks moisture straight from the air, so anything not slaking today goes under a lid at once.
6. **Slake it** (wake it with water). Half-fill your pit or pot with water — never the other way around; the lime must always be a few guests arriving into a big crowd of water, so its fierce heat spreads thin through the crowd instead of piling up in one boiling spot. Add quicklime a little at a time — a double handful, wait, stir, repeat — with the long stick, standing back and upwind, so the wind pushes the steam away from your face. It will hiss, steam, and get very hot: the water can truly boil, and lumps spit. That heat is normal; a slake that stays cold means dead, air-spoiled lime — it already drank from the air and has no fire left to give. Keep adding lime and water in small turns until you have a thick cream, like porridge dripping slowly off the stick — roughly two to three parts water to one part quicklime by volume, but let the feel decide. This cream is **slaked lime**, or lime putty.
7. **Let the putty rest** under a thin skin of water, covered from dirt and rain. The water lid keeps air off it — air is what turns it back to stone, and you do not want that in the tub. It is usable for whitewash after a day or two; for mortar it only improves with weeks of resting. Old builders buried putty for a year and called it better than gold.
8. **Use it:**
   - **Mortar:** 1 part lime putty to 3 parts coarse, gritty sand, mixed stiff enough to hold a peak. It hardens over days and weeks as it breathes the air back in — the last section tells that whole story. Keep new mortar damp and shaded for the first week; drying too fast starves it.
   - **Whitewash:** putty thinned with water to the look of milk, brushed on in thin coats, each drying before the next. It goes on see-through and dries white — trust it, do not thicken the mix.

## Watch out

- **Slaking is violent.** Water poured onto a big pile of quicklime can boil, spatter, and even set nearby straw on fire. Always lime into plenty of water, always small amounts, always the long stick, never your face over the pot. If it froths toward the rim, stop adding and let it calm.
- **Eyes first, always.** Lime in the eye eats the eye's own water and can blind. If it happens, rinse gently with clean water for at least **thirty minutes** — far longer than feels necessary — keep rinsing on the way, and get real medical care when you can.
- **Lime on skin:** brush dry powder off first (water wakes it, so sweep before you wash), then flood with lots of water for several minutes. On sweaty skin it burns fast — keep arms covered, wipe sweat with your sleeve, not your hand. And the putty bites too: slaked lime, fresh mortar, and whitewash are gentler than quicklime but still caustic — a day of barehanded mixing will crack and burn skin without you feeling it happen. Keep hands out of the mix, rinse well after handling, and flush any splash with lots of water.
- **Under-burned stone is the usual failure.** It comes out heavy, hard, and grey, and barely hisses. The cause is too little heat for too few hours, or lumps too big. The save: break it smaller and send it through the next firing — nothing is lost but time. The cure is more fuel and more hours, not more stone. Do not skimp the night firing.
- **Quicklime dies in storage.** Left open, it drinks moisture and breath from the air and turns back toward chalk within days — it stops hissing and is then good for little. Slake within days of burning; keep the rest sealed in lidded pots. Slaked putty under its water lid, by contrast, keeps for months and only gets better.
- **Lumps in the putty are time bombs.** An unslaked lump plastered into a wall will slake later from rain, swell, and pop a crater in your finish. Stir well, break every lump, and for fine work let the putty rest longer.
- **Kiln fumes choke.** The burning stone exhales heavy, invisible air (carbon dioxide — the same gas as your out-breath) in huge amounts, and it pools in low places. The fuel adds a second, sneakier gas: carbon monoxide, which has no smell, no colour, and gives no warning before it drops you. Work in the open, never lean into the kiln mouth, and never build the kiln in a pit you must climb down into.

## How it works

Limestone is **calcium carbonate** — one of the commonest stones on Earth, and the same stone living things build with. A snail's shell, a bird's eggshell, coral, chalk: all calcium carbonate. That is why seashells work in the kiln exactly like quarried rock: they are the rock, laid down by small living builders instead of by the slow ages of the earth.

Think of the stone as holding its breath. Locked inside calcium carbonate is carbon dioxide — the gas you breathe out — bound tightly to calcium. The bond is strong, so the stone holds that breath for millions of years. Fierce heat breaks the grip. At around 900 degrees Celsius the stone finally exhales: the carbon dioxide streams away up the kiln, and what remains is **quicklime** (calcium oxide). Cooler fires will not do it — below that heat the stone snatches its breath back as fast as the fire pulls it loose, which is why a dull-red kiln burns fuel all night and gives you back plain rock. The breath is heavy stuff, too: nearly half the stone's weight leaves as invisible gas. That is why good quicklime feels so strangely light in the hand — you are holding a stone that has breathed out.

Quicklime is a stone left hungry. The fire tore its breath out and left a gap, and the stone grabs water with real violence to fill it. Add water and it **slakes**: the lime seizes the water, swells, crumbles, and gives back as heat a good part of the fire you poured in at the kiln — enough to boil the slaking water. That stored fire is why quicklime bites skin and eyes, and why it must live in sealed pots: left open, it steals water and breath from the air and spoils. Slaking turns it into **slaked lime** (calcium hydroxide) — calm, creamy, safe to store under water.

Then comes the slow magic that closes the circle. Spread as mortar or whitewash, slaked lime meets the air and does the kiln's work in reverse: it slowly inhales carbon dioxide back — the very breath the fire drove out — and turns, grain by grain, back into calcium carbonate. Back into limestone. Your wall is not glued; it is becoming stone. That is why lime mortar sets rock-hard over weeks, why it needs air and a little damp to cure, and why the putty keeps forever under water, where no air can reach it. Stone, breath out, hunger, drink, breath in, stone again: one full circle of breathing, with your house standing inside it.

## Go further

- **First safe experiment, today:** stir a pinch of slaked lime into a cup of water, let it settle clear, then blow through a straw into the clear water. It turns milky — your own breath turning lime back into limestone before your eyes. The whole circle in a cup.
- **Watch a wall breathe:** whitewash two boards, keep one indoors and one out in the air. Scratch both after a month — the outdoor one is harder. It has inhaled more.
- **Compare your stones:** burn shells beside rock and note which slakes livelier. Shell lime is often purer and whiter — prized for fine plaster.
- **Better mortar:** the sand matters as much as the lime. Sharp, gritty, mixed sizes grip; smooth round sand slides. Squeeze a damp handful — it should hold its shape and bite.
- **What this unlocks:** mortar and plaster for real building, whitewash for bright rooms, lime for curing hides, and a light dusting of slaked lime to sweeten sour garden soil.
- **Sibling crafts:** this burn leans on *build a kiln* and *make charcoal* — master those and the night firing gets much easier.
