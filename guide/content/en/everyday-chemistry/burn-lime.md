Burn limestone hot enough and it becomes lime — one of the oldest and most useful materials people have ever made. Lime mixed with sand becomes mortar, the glue that holds stone walls together. Thinned with water it becomes whitewash, which brightens and protects walls. It also cures hides for leather and sweetens sour soil.

This is a big, hot, three-day job, and lime demands respect: fresh-burned lime reacts violently with water and can burn skin and blind eyes. Done carefully, it turns loose stones into buildings.

## You need

- **Limestone, chalk, seashells, or coral** — the test: drip vinegar or any sour liquid on it. If it fizzes with tiny bubbles, it will make lime. Gather a big basketful to start.
- **A kiln** that can hold fierce heat for a day.
- **Lots of fuel** — charcoal is best because it burns hotter than wood. Gather at least as much fuel as stone by volume, and then gather that much again. Running out of fuel halfway is the most common failure.
- **Water** and a pit, trough, or big pot for slaking (waking the lime).
- **A long stick** for stirring at arm's length.
- **Lidded clay pots** for storage.
- **Protection** — cover skin, keep your face back, and guard your eyes above all.

## Steps

1. Break the stone into pieces between egg and fist size. Small enough for heat to reach the middle, big enough to leave air gaps. Shells can go in whole.
2. Load the kiln. Either build alternating layers — fuel, stone, fuel, stone — or pile the stone above a fire chamber, depending on your kiln.
3. Fire it, and keep it fierce. The stone must sit at a bright orange-to-yellow glow — around 900 degrees Celsius — for many hours. A small kiln needs 12 to 24 hours of hard firing. Feed it through the night; if the glow fades, the burn fails.
4. Let the kiln cool for most of a day, then check the stone. Well-burned lime — called **quicklime** — is:
   - noticeably lighter than it went in (it loses almost half its weight),
   - white or pale, and soft enough to crumble,
   - hissing and cracking when a single drop of water touches it.
   - Heavy, hard, grey lumps are under-burned: put them back for the next firing.
5. Unload with covered hands. Quicklime is hungry for water — including the water in your skin and eyes.
6. **Slake it** (wake it with water). Half-fill your pit or pot with water. Add quicklime a little at a time, stirring with the long stick, standing back and upwind. It will hiss, steam, and get very hot — it can reach boiling and spit. Keep adding lime and water in small turns until you have a thick cream. This cream is **slaked lime**, or lime putty.
7. Let the putty rest under a thin layer of water, covered. It is usable for whitewash after a day or two; for mortar it only improves with weeks of resting.
8. Use it:
   - **Mortar:** 1 part lime putty to 3 parts coarse sand, mixed stiff. It hardens slowly over days and weeks.
   - **Whitewash:** putty thinned with water to the look of milk, brushed on walls in thin coats.

## Watch out

- **Slaking is violent.** Water on a big pile of quicklime can boil, spatter, and even start fires. Always small amounts at a time, always the long stick, never your face over the pot.
- **Eyes first, always.** Lime in the eye can blind. If it happens, rinse gently with clean water for at least 20 minutes, keep rinsing on the way, and get real medical care when you can.
- **Lime on skin:** brush dry powder off first, then flood with lots of water. On sweaty skin it burns fast — keep your arms covered.
- **Under-burned stone is the usual failure.** It stays heavy and barely hisses. The cure is more fuel and more hours, not more stone. Do not skimp the night firing.
- **Quicklime dies in storage.** It slowly drinks moisture from the air and loses its power. Slake it within days of burning; slaked putty under water, by contrast, keeps for months.
- **Kiln fumes choke.** The burning stone gives off heavy, invisible air. Work in the open and never lean into the kiln mouth.

## How it works

Limestone is calcium carbonate: calcium bound to trapped air (carbon dioxide). Fierce heat drives that trapped air out, leaving quicklime, which is why the lumps weigh so much less. Water wakes quicklime into slaked lime, releasing all that stored heat in a rush. Then the slow magic: spread as mortar or whitewash, slaked lime breathes carbon dioxide back in from the air, quietly turning back into stone — which is why lime mortar sets rock-hard between your bricks.
