title: Stay warm without fire

Your body is already a heater. At rest it gives off roughly 100 watts — about as much as a bright old light bulb. You do not need fire on the first night; you need to stop that heat escaping. Two thieves take most of it: the cold ground you lie on, and moving air that carries warmth away.

The fix is insulation — anything that traps still air around you. Dry dead leaves, grass, pine needles, and bracken are nature's stuffing. This is exactly how birds and squirrels survive freezing nights with no fire at all.

## You need

- **A sheltered spot** — out of wind and rain first (see Find shelter fast). Insulation soaked by rain stops working.
- **A big pile of dry, dead plant material** — leaves, grass, pine needles, bracken. Dry means it crackles and crumbles. Gather far more than feels sensible: a loose heap up to your chest is a starting point, not the finish.
- **Branches, evergreen boughs, or slabs of bark** — for a springy layer between you and the ground.
- **Something for your head and neck** — a hood, a spare cloth, or even leaves stuffed under a collar. Any bare skin leaks heat all night.

## Steps

1. **Build the bed first.** Lay branches or boughs on the ground, then pile dry leaves on top until the loose pile reaches your knees — about 40 cm. Your weight will squash it down to a hand's depth.
2. **Test it.** Lie on the bed for one minute. If you can feel the ground anywhere — hip, shoulder, heels — add more under that spot. The ground must never touch through.
3. **Stuff your clothes.** Push crumpled dry leaves or grass between your layers of clothing: shirt, trousers, socks, hat. Fill until you look like a scarecrow. It itches; it also works like a duvet.
4. **Lie down and bury yourself.** Pull the rest of the pile over your legs and body, an arm's length deep if you have it. Leave only your face clear so you can breathe easily.
5. **Cover head and neck.** Wrap whatever you have around them. They are often the only bare parts left, so they become the biggest leak.
6. **Curl up small.** Knees toward chest, hands in armpits. A curled body shows less surface to the cold and keeps fingers warm.
7. **Wiggle, do not march.** If you get cold in the night, tense and release your muscles, wiggle toes, squeeze fists. That makes heat without leaving the nest and without heavy sweating.

## Watch out

- **Wet material is worse than none.** Damp leaves pull heat out of you. Only use stuff that sounds dry — it rustles and crunches. Look under evergreens, under fallen logs, and on the sheltered side of slopes for dry pockets.
- **Sweat is a slow trap.** If you soak your clothes with sweat while gathering, you will freeze in them later. Work at a gentle pace and open your collar while working, then close everything up when you stop.
- **Do not skip the bed because you are tired.** Lying straight on the ground, it steals heat from you all night, faster than the air does. Ten minutes of bed-building buys eight hours of warmth.
- **Cotton stays wet.** If any clothing layer is soaked, wring it hard and put drier layers, or leaf stuffing, between it and your skin.
- **Know the danger signs.** Violent shivering is your body fighting well. Shivering that stops while you still feel cold, plus clumsiness or confusion, means serious hypothermia — the body failing from cold. Add insulation, curl up, and get real medical help as soon as you possibly can.

## How it works

Warmth is not a thing you gather; it is heat your body makes constantly. Still air barely carries heat away, so anything that traps air in tiny pockets — fluffed leaves, dry grass, a bird's down — becomes a warm blanket once your 100 watts warm it up. The ground, by contrast, is a huge cold mass pressed against you, so it drinks heat by direct touch. Trap the air, break the touch, and your own heater does the rest.
