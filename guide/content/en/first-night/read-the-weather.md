Cold rain is the most dangerous thing in your first nights outside. Wet clothes pull heat out of your body about 25 times faster than dry air, and a soaked person can slide into dangerous cold even on a 10 °C night. The good news: the sky almost always warns you, usually 6 to 24 hours ahead. A week of honest watching is enough to start learning its language.

## You need
- Your eyes and a view of the sky — best with some horizon visible, so you can see what is coming, not just what is overhead.
- A few minutes at dawn, midday, and dusk — the signs are clearest at the edges of the day.
- A week of patience — guess the weather, then check yourself the next day. That feedback is the whole lesson.
- Somewhere to scratch notes — bark, dirt, a stick tally. Memory lies; marks do not.

## Steps
1. Learn the cloud heights, from calm to trouble:
   - High, thin, wispy streaks ("mare's tails") — fair weather now, but if they thicken through the day, rain often comes in 12–24 hours.
   - A thin milky sheet that puts a pale ring (a halo) around the sun or moon — moisture arriving high up; rain often within a day.
   - A low grey blanket covering the whole sky — steady rain or drizzle soon, maybe already falling nearby.
   - Tall cauliflower towers growing upward — a storm building. If the top flattens into an anvil shape, expect a thunderstorm within hours.
2. Watch the direction of change. Clouds getting lower and thicker as hours pass means weather is coming. Clouds lifting, thinning, and breaking into blue means it is leaving.
3. Feel the wind twice a day:
   - Note where it comes from at morning and evening (wet a finger, or watch grass and smoke).
   - A steady wind that swings to a new direction often means a change within a day.
   - Wind rising while clouds lower means the storm is hours away, not days.
   - Heavy, still, humid air on a warm afternoon often comes right before a thunderstorm.
4. Read the morning:
   - Thick dew or frost on the grass at dawn means the night sky was clear — the day will likely stay fair.
   - Dry grass at dawn means clouds blanketed the night — rain may be close.
   - A red sky at sunrise often means rain that day; a red sky at sunset often means a fair tomorrow. This works best where weather travels west to east, which is most of Europe and North America.
5. Watch your fire's smoke. Rising straight up: fair. Hanging low and curling toward the ground: the air pressure is falling — rain is likely.
6. Watch the animals. Birds fly high and sing in fair weather; before a storm they fly low or go quiet, because the insects they chase fly low in heavy air. Bees head home early before rain.
7. Score yourself for a week. Three sky checks a day, one written guess — "rain", "dry", or "cold night" — then check it against what actually happened. Most people beat a coin flip within a week.
8. Judge the coming night before dark. Clear sky plus calm wind at dusk means the coldest kind of night, because heat escapes straight up into space — add insulation early. A cloudy night stays milder.

## Watch out
- Never trust one sign alone. Act when two or more agree — say, a halo plus a wind swing.
- Mountains make their own weather. Storms can build in under an hour there; if towers grow over the peaks by noon, be off ridges and summits by early afternoon.
- Thunderstorm right on top of you — sudden cool gust, towering dark cloud, hair prickling with static: get off high ground and away from lone tall trees immediately, and crouch low.
- A beautiful clear evening is not "safe" — it is the recipe for the coldest night. Prepare anyway.
- Folk sayings like "cows lying down" are hints at best; trust the sky first.
- When signs disagree or you are unsure, prepare for the worse option before dark: cover firewood, tighten shelter. Preparation you did not need costs an hour; preparation you skipped can cost the night.

## How it works
Rain rides on huge masses of rising, moistening air, and rising air announces itself in order: high wisps first, then thicker and lower cloud, shifting wind, and falling pressure that makes smoke sink and smells sharpen. Animals feel the pressure and humidity change before you do. Each small sign alone is weak; read three or four together and you have tomorrow's forecast half a day early.
