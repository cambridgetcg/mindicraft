A leaf hut — often called a debris hut — is a sleeping bag made of sticks and leaves. It is a low tent-shaped frame buried under a thick coat of dead leaves, with a nest of more leaves inside. Built properly, it can keep a person alive through a freezing night with no fire and no blanket, because your own body heat warms the trapped air around you.

It is simple, but it eats time and material. Most of the three hours goes into gathering leaves, not building. Start at least three to four hours before dark.

## You need

- **A ridge pole** — one strong, straight pole about one-and-a-half times your height (roughly 3 m) and as thick as your wrist. This is the backbone of the hut. Test it: it must hold your weight when you press hard.
- **A support for one end** — a stump, a forked tree, a boulder, or two crossed sticks, about hip height (roughly 1 m).
- **Ribs** — a few dozen sticks from arm length to leg length, to lean along both sides of the backbone.
- **Light brush** — twiggy branches to lay across the ribs so leaves do not fall through. This mesh is called the lattice.
- **A huge amount of dry debris** — dead leaves, pine needles, dry grass, bracken. Think a pile the size of a small car. Nearly everyone gathers a tenth of what is needed on the first try.
- **A door plug** — a heap of leaves, a bundle of grass, or a stuffed bag, to close the entrance behind you.

## Steps

1. **Pick the spot.** Use the same checks as Find shelter fast: out of the wind, no dead branches above, no flood channel below, and close to deep drifts of leaves so you are not hauling them far.
2. **Set the ridge pole.** Rest one end on the hip-high support and the other on the ground, making a long low wedge. Point the high, open end away from the wind.
3. **Check the size — small is warm.** Crawl underneath and lie down. You want a snug fit: about a hand's width of space around your body and above your feet. A roomy hut is a cold hut, because your body cannot warm the extra air.
4. **Rib both sides.** Lean sticks against the ridge pole at about 45 degrees, close together, all along both sides. Break or trim ends that poke far above the ridge — tall stubs channel rain inside.
5. **Lay the lattice.** Criss-cross light brush over the ribs until leaves dropped on top mostly stay put.
6. **Pile the debris.** Heap leaves on from the bottom up, like roof shingles, until the coat is at least an arm's length thick — about 60 cm — everywhere. Test by pushing your arm in: if your fingers reach sticks, add more. For a frosty night, build toward waist-deep, closer to 1 m.
7. **Cage the coat.** Lay a few light branches over the finished leaf pile so wind cannot peel it away in the night.
8. **Stuff the inside.** Fill the whole hut with dry leaves — completely full. You will crawl in and squash yourself a burrow. Aim for at least 30 cm of leaves under you when compressed.
9. **Go in feet first and plug the door.** Wiggle in backwards, then pull your door plug into the entrance. Leave a small gap for fresh air near your face.

## Watch out

- **Too big.** The most common failure. If you can sit up inside, it is too large to warm. Shrink it or stuff the spare space with leaves.
- **Too thin.** A see-through leaf coat lets rain and cold straight in. Always do the arm test on every side, including the far end at your feet.
- **Bare floor.** More heat drains into the ground than into the air. Never skip the thick leaf bed inside.
- **Damp debris.** Wet leaves steal heat. Hunt dry pockets under evergreens, inside hollow logs, and on sheltered slopes.
- **Running out of light.** Gathering takes about two-thirds of the total time. If dark is closing in, stop building and use the leaf pile itself: burrow into it as in Stay warm without fire.

## How it works

A leaf hut is a giant nest — the same trick a squirrel uses in its ball of leaves. The thick coat traps millions of tiny pockets of still air, and still air barely carries heat. Your body slowly warms the air held in the nest around you, and the leaf shell keeps wind and rain from carrying that warmth away.
