You can live about three days without water, but you turn slow, clumsy, and foolish after just one. So once you are sheltered, water is the next search. The search is simpler than it feels, because water obeys one rule: it runs downhill and gathers in low places. Every plant and animal near you needs it too — and they have already found it. Read the land, go down, and follow their signs.

## You need

- **Half a day of light** — search in the cool morning, not the hot afternoon, so you sweat out less than you find.
- **A way to mark your trail** — snapped twigs, scratched arrows, small stacked stones every 20–30 paces, so you can always walk back to your shelter.
- **A container if you have anything** — a bottle, a bag, a curved piece of bark. Even a soaked cloth carries water when wrung out.
- **Your ears and patience** — running water can be heard long before it is seen, especially in still air at dawn and dusk.

## Steps

1. **Stop and listen first.** Stand silent for five full minutes. Turn your head slowly. A faint steady hiss or trickle is worth walking toward.
2. **Read the land from any high point.** Look for valleys, ravines, and folds where slopes meet — these are the land's funnels. Note the lowest green line and head for it, downhill.
3. **Follow the green.** A ribbon of plants greener, taller, and thicker than everything around it — willows, reeds, rushes, ferns, thick moss — means water at or near the surface. Cliff bases and the feet of hills often hide springs, places where water seeps out of the ground.
4. **Follow the animals.** Game trails join together downhill like branches joining a trunk; follow the joins. Birds flying low, fast, and straight at dawn or dusk are often heading to water. Clouds of insects at evening usually stand over damp ground.
5. **Try a dry stream bed.** Find the lowest point, often at the outside of a bend, and dig with a stick or your hands. Damp sand within about 30–60 cm is a good sign — widen the hole, wait, and let water seep in and the mud settle before you scoop from the top.
6. **Catch what falls from the sky.** Rain caught straight into a container is the safest wild water there is. At dawn, drag a cloth through dew-heavy grass and wring it out — slow, but it adds up.
7. **Choose the best source you can.** Flowing beats still. Clear beats cloudy. A spring at its source beats the pool below it. Walk fifty paces upstream and check for dead animals or heavy droppings before you trust any spot.
8. **Mark the spot and the way home.** A water source you cannot find again is no source at all.

## Watch out

- **Clear water can still carry invisible germs** — tiny living things, like giardia, that cause days of vomiting and diarrhoea, which drain your water faster than thirst does. The safe rule: bring water to a rolling boil for one minute (three minutes high in the mountains, above about 2,000 m) before drinking, once you have fire and a container. If you truly cannot treat it yet and are running dry, take the cleanest, fastest-flowing source you can find — dying of thirst today is worse than risking sickness next week — and get real medical care if illness follows.
- **Never drink seawater or urine.** Both are saltier than your blood, and your body spends more water flushing the salt out than it gains. They make dehydration worse, always.
- **Do not eat snow.** Melting it costs body heat and chills your core. Melt it first — in a container, in the sun, or packed in a bag carried between clothing layers.
- **Skip stagnant, scummy, or smelly pools** and any water near a dead animal. If better water exists within reach, keep walking.
- **Do not sweat away more than you find.** Rest in shade through the hottest hours, breathe through your nose, and move at a pace you could keep all day.
- **Do not get lost looking.** Mark your trail without fail. Shelter plus no water beats water plus no shelter at nightfall.

## How it works

Rain falls everywhere, but gravity gathers it: every fold in the land is a funnel feeding a bigger funnel, ending in streams and pools. Plants stand where their roots reach water, so green marks it on the surface. Animals must drink daily, so their trails are the memory of thousands of walks to water. You are simply borrowing maps that already exist.
